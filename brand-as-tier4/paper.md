## Abstract

The brand-portfolio architecture literature has produced a mature taxonomy of relationships between brands but no theory of which organizational layers carry the brand boundary. This paper proposes that brand IS Tier 4 — the Product specification surface — of the six-tier business architecture in Zharnikov (2026ag), projected through an observer perceptual filter into observed brand-perception space. Three downstream phenomena unify under this single structural identity: multi-brand capacity is the firm's tier-level separability profile; failed-brand recovery is tier-level salvage of brand-agnostic infrastructure under brand-bound failure; and marketing, advertising, and branding are Tier-6 organizational functions that create, operate, and retire Tier-4 instances. Seven falsifiable propositions are derived. P1 predicts portfolio capacity scales with Tier-3 / Tier-5 / Tier-6 independence rather than Business Model diversification. P2 predicts recovery success scales with the brand-agnostic substrate preserved through failure. P7 operationalizes the Aaker–Joachimsthaler Brand Relationship Spectrum as a single Tier-3-visibility-in-Tier-4 parameter. The Spectral Brand Theory eight-dimension framework (Zharnikov 2026a) supplies the per-instance Tier-4 specification. Two diagnostic instruments — the Multi-Brand Capacity Diagnostic and the Recovery Salvage Matrix — are sketched for empirical validation.

**Keywords**: brand portfolio architecture; multi-brand strategy; corporate rebranding; brand failure recovery; tier-level decomposition; brand-business separability; marketing organization; cross-brand function

---

The brand-portfolio architecture literature, anchored by Aaker and Joachimsthaler (2000) and elaborated through Rao, Agarwal and Dahlhoff (2004), Morgan and Rego (2009), and Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016), classifies the *relationship between brands* in a portfolio — branded house, sub-brand, endorsed, hybrid, house of brands — and quantifies how that classification predicts firm value, stock returns, and risk. What this literature does not do is decompose the *business architecture* underneath the brand layer. The orthogonality question — whether two brands can coexist on one firm's substrate — has been answered only at the level of relationship type. The deeper question is *which organizational tiers are brand-bound and which are brand-agnostic*. This paper supplies the missing decomposition by treating brand as one tier in a six-tier business architecture and deriving multi-brand capacity, failed-brand recovery, and marketing-as-cross-brand-function as three downstream consequences of that single structural identity.

The structural claim is that brand IS Tier 4 — the Product specification surface — of the six-tier business architecture proposed in Zharnikov (2026ag). Tier 4 is the customer-facing instance specified by the firm and projected through the observer perceptual filter into the observed perception cloud. Tiers 1 (Owner Intent), 2 (Business Model), 3 (Business Entity), 5 (Process), and 6 (Organization) are *brand-agnostic* in their foundational components: a single Tier-3 legal entity can host multiple Tier-4 brand instances; a single Tier-5 manufacturing process can produce output for multiple brands; a single Tier-6 organization can operate multiple brands through cross-brand functions. Multi-brand capacity, failed-brand recovery, and marketing-as-cross-brand-function are not three separate phenomena. They are three manifestations of one structural fact: brand binds at Tier 4 only.

This paper makes three contributions to the brand-portfolio, corporate-rebranding, and marketing-organization literatures. First, it operationalizes the Aaker–Joachimsthaler (2000) Brand Relationship Spectrum as a measurable parameter rather than a typological dimension. The four spectrum positions become four separability profiles distinguished by Tier-3 visibility in Tier-4. P7 makes this precise. Prior work treats the spectrum as a strategic-choice variable; the present framework treats it as a tier-level diagnostic with a continuous-valued parameter. Second, it supplies a theory of failed-brand recovery as tier-level salvage. Existing work (Muzellec and Lambkin 2006; Merrilees and Miller 2008; Marques, Vinhas da Silva, Davcik and Tamagnini Faria 2020) catalogs rebranding outcomes; the Marques et al. finding that rebranding *transferred* prior equity but did not *create* net-new equity has remained an unexplained empirical regularity. The Tier-4 framing supplies the mechanism: when only Tier 4 is re-specified and Tiers 1–3 + 5–6 persist, equity rides on the persisting substrate; net-new equity creation requires changes at Tier 1 or Tier 2. The framework distinguishes three recovery pathways and predicts which infrastructure is salvageable (P2, P6). Third, it gives a tier-level account of marketing, advertising, and branding. Webster (1992), Moorman and Rust (1999), Verhoef and Leeflang (2009), and most recently McAlister, Germann, Grewal and Lehmann (2023) and Whitler, Rego and Morgan (2025) frame marketing as a function within the firm. None localizes the function on a layered ontology. The framework here positions marketing as a Tier-6 organizational function whose work product is the creation, operation, and retirement of Tier-4 brand instances. The CMO is a portfolio manager of Tier-4 configurations, not a single-brand custodian. P4 derives the centralization-versus-decentralization question as a tier-level separability choice.

The theoretical implications are unification, finite-axis specification, and ontological discipline. The framework subsumes three currently disjoint literatures — brand-portfolio architecture, corporate rebranding, and marketing-organization — under one tier-level decomposition and supplies a finite-axis specification basis for brand instances through the Spectral Brand Theory eight-dimension framework (Zharnikov 2026a). It distinguishes brand-as-projection (a Tier-4 ontological surface) from brand-as-asset (the legal-trademark sense, a Tier-3 artifact), pre-empting an ontological-mixing reviewer objection that has dogged earlier attempts to integrate these literatures. It predicts the empirical asymmetries documented by Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016) and Umashankar, Bahadir and Bharadwaj (2022) as consequences of which tier carries the boundary, not as discrete-category effects. It connects to the brand-extension literature (Aaker and Keller 1990; Park, Milberg and Lawson 1991; Peng, Bijmolt, Völckner and Zhao 2023) by predicting axis-specific transfer along the Cultural and Ideological dimensions of the SBT specification (P5).

The practical implications are two diagnostic instruments. The *Multi-Brand Capacity Diagnostic* (MBCD) extends the Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic from Zharnikov (2026ag) to predict how many additional brands a firm can host on its current substrate without performance degradation. The *Recovery Salvage Matrix* converts the Tier-1-through-Tier-6 decomposition into a pre-failure infrastructure-audit instrument that distinguishes brand-bound from brand-agnostic components and forecasts which substrate is preserved under brand-level failure. Both are operationalizable from existing firm data. Both make the abstract framework actionable for a CMO weighing portfolio expansion and for a board weighing recovery strategy after brand failure.

The paper proceeds as follows. The Theoretical Foundations section engages the four nearest literatures (brand portfolio architecture, corporate rebranding, marketing as cross-brand organizational function, brand extension) and the upstream framework. The Brand as Tier-4 Projection section states the formal Tier-4 claim and pre-empts the brand-versus-trademark ontological-mixing objection. The Tier-Level Brand-Agnostic / Brand-Bound Decomposition section supplies the per-tier decomposition and operationalizes Tier-3 visibility in Tier-4 as the parameter that maps the Aaker spectrum. The Multi-Brand Architectures section applies the parameter to four canonical architectures and reanalyzes the Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016) risk-return findings through the tier lens. The Failed-Brand Recovery section develops three recovery pathways with case treatment. The Marketing, Advertising, and Branding as Cross-Brand Tier-6 Functions section completes the unification. The Discussion and Validation Roadmap section states limitations and a four-priority validation roadmap.

## Theoretical Foundations

### *Brand portfolio architecture*

Brand-portfolio architecture is the most developed adjacent literature. The Aaker and Joachimsthaler (2000) Brand Relationship Spectrum continues to anchor the field; its four-strategy continuum (branded house, sub-brand, endorsed brand, house of brands) plus nine sub-strategies organizes both academic and practitioner discourse on portfolio choice. Aaker (2004) extended the spectrum to a full portfolio-strategy treatment, identifying brand portfolio roles, range-brand structures, and portfolio-graphics as the operational backbone of architecture choice. Empirical follow-on work links spectrum position to firm value: Rao, Agarwal and Dahlhoff (2004) demonstrated, across a panel of 113 firms, that corporate (branded-house) branding strategies are associated with higher Tobin's q than house-of-brands strategies; Morgan and Rego (2009), examining 72 firms over a decade, decomposed portfolio-strategy outcomes by portfolio coherence, brand-quality means, and the number of brands in the portfolio; and Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016), extending the taxonomy to five classes across 302 firms, demonstrated systematic differences in returns and stock-specific risk across the spectrum. Sridhar, Germann, Kang and Grewal (2016) examine advertising-vehicle-to-firm-value links that are indirectly portfolio-relevant. Wiles, Morgan and Rego (2012) document asymmetric stock-market reactions to brand acquisitions and disposals as a function of the acquirer's marketing capability. Bahadir, Bharadwaj and Srivastava (2008) measure the financial value of brands as transferred assets in mergers and acquisitions. Umashankar, Bahadir and Bharadwaj (2022) find that customer-satisfaction outcomes decline in M&A even when efficiency outcomes improve, foreshadowing the Tier-3 / Tier-4 separation that the present framework formalizes. Landsman and Stremersch (2026), examining biopharma M&As, document downstream effects of M&As on marketing decisions and effectiveness — again, a phenomenon that the framework localizes to Tier-6 reorganization following a Tier-3 transfer.

Hatch and Schultz (2003) treat corporate-versus-product branding as a two-level alignment problem (Vision–Culture–Image) — the closest existing precedent for treating brand as architecturally layered, but stopping at two levels. Strebinger (2014) and Strebinger and Treiblmaier (2018) introduce multi-level drivers and multi-axis architectures, motivating but not delivering a fully layered ontology. Yu (2021) supplies the first formally-modeled equilibrium treatment of architecture choice. Nguyen, Zhang and Calantone (2018) develop a brand-portfolio coherence scale that quantifies *coherence among Tier-4 instances* but does not partition the firm itself. Ward, Beal, Dawes, Trinh and Romaniuk (2026), in *Journal of Brand Management*, develop portfolio-cohesion measures along family-resemblance lines. None of these works decomposes the firm into tiered organizational layers and assigns brand to one of them. The cumulative gap is precise: the literature classifies the *relationship between brands* but does not partition the *firm itself* into layers across which the brand boundary can be drawn.

### *Corporate rebranding and brand recovery*

The corporate-rebranding literature is mature on rebranding *as* structural — driven by mergers, acquisitions, ownership change, repositioning — and converges on a continuum from cosmetic name-and-visual change through full identity reconstitution (Muzellec, Doogan and Lambkin 2003; Stuart and Muzellec 2004; Daly and Moloney 2004; Muzellec and Lambkin 2006; Merrilees and Miller 2008; Muzellec and Lambkin 2009; Miller, Merrilees and Yakimova 2014). Horsky and Swyngedouw (1987) provided the foundational financial-market test of corporate name change. Recent work has added internal-perspective and consumer-attachment dimensions (Yakimova, Mavondo, Freeman and Stuart 2021; Hu and Bradford 2024) and structured-literature-review consolidations (Sangroya, Jebarajakirthy, Maseeh, Saha and Gupta 2026). Dion and Mazzalovo (2016) treat "sleeping beauty" brand revival through rearticulated heritage — a special case of the Tier-4 re-specification pathway developed in the Failed-Brand Recovery section.

The Marques, Vinhas da Silva, Davcik and Tamagnini Faria (2020) private-label study is the closest empirical anchor for the Tier-4 separability claim: across 466 observations, rebranding *transferred* prior equity to the new brand but did not *create* net-new equity. The "brand replacement on stable parent infrastructure following original-brand failure" pathway is empirically attested but unnamed in the academic literature; cases such as Tropicana 2009 (re-collapse on existing infrastructure within weeks), Lululemon-Ivivva (cohort-extension brand on shared substrate), and BlackBerry's pivot from device to enterprise security (cross-Tier-2 re-specification on the same Tier-3 entity) are catalogued in practitioner outlets but lack a structural account. Keller (1999) supplies the canonical brand-equity-level "back to basics" recipe for revitalization but does not derive recovery feasibility from the persistence of infrastructure beneath a failed Tier-4. The framework here supplies that mechanism.

### *Marketing as cross-brand organizational function*

The marketing-organization literature establishes marketing as a function within the firm but does not localize it on a layered ontology. Webster (1992, 1997) initiates the framing of marketing as evolving from a discrete department toward a distributed firm-wide capability, with the 1997 essay extending the framing further toward the cross-functional model that the present Tier-6 argument relies on. Moorman and Rust (1999) connect customer to product, service-delivery, and financial accountability. Homburg, Workman and Krohmer (1999) measure marketing influence empirically across more than 200 firms. Verhoef and Leeflang (2009) identify accountability and innovativeness as primary drivers of marketing-department influence within the firm. Recent work has extended the framing to CMO presence and effects on firm value (Boyd, Chandy and Cunha 2010; Germann, Ebbes and Grewal 2015), CMO board interlocks (Ebbes, Germann and Grewal 2025), CMO pay benchmarking (Feng, Whitler and Wiles 2025), CMO role design (Whitler, Rego and Morgan 2025), and a behavioral taxonomy of marketing organizations (McAlister, Germann, Grewal and Lehmann 2023). Edeling, Srinivasan and Hanssens (2021) review the marketing–finance interface and document a sharp accountability gap: in a large practitioner sample, 91% of firms held marketing responsible for the brand but only a small minority held marketing accountable for stock-market performance.

The Marcom-Leaders cluster identified by McAlister et al. is the closest empirical signature in the literature of a Tier-6 marketing function operating Tier-4 brand instances at the cross-brand level; the framework here supplies the structural account that the McAlister et al. taxonomy describes only behaviorally. The Whitler, Rego and Morgan (2025) finding that more than half of CMO roles are misaligned along scope and authority dimensions is, under the framework, a misallocation of Tier-6 capabilities across Tier-4 instances rather than a measurement failure.

### *Brand extension and dimensional transfer*

The brand-extension literature is one of the most replicated streams in marketing. Tauber (1981, 1988) introduced brand-franchise extension and brand leverage. Aaker and Keller (1990) established the canonical fit-times-parent-quality model. Park, Jaworski and MacInnis (1986) introduced the strategic brand-concept-image distinction between functional, symbolic, and experiential brand concepts. Park, Milberg and Lawson (1991) demonstrated that *what counts as fit* depends on the parent's concept type, decomposing transfer into feature similarity (functional brands) and concept consistency (symbolic brands) — the closest existing approximation to an axis-specific transfer claim. Smith and Park (1992) supplied market-share and advertising-efficiency evidence. Völckner and Sattler (2006) ranked drivers; Yorkston, Nunes and Matta (2010) added implicit-theory moderators; Monga and John (2007, 2010) added cultural-thinking-style and brand-elasticity asymmetries; Spiggle, Nguyen and Caravella (2012) added brand-extension authenticity (heritage, essence) to the predictor set. Torelli and Ahluwalia (2012) supplied the cleanest existing evidence of cultural-schema transfer as a separate channel that can override perceived fit. Peng, Bijmolt, Völckner and Zhao (2023) settled the meta-analytic baseline across more than two thousand effect sizes. Deng and Messinger (2022) decomposed fit into multiple dimensions on the way toward a more granular treatment. None of this work proposes a *fixed, finite, ordered* dimensional basis on which transfer occurs nor makes axis-specific transfer predictions. The SBT 8-dimension framework supplies that basis; P5 makes the axis-specific prediction (Cultural and Ideological dimensions transfer; Experiential and Economic do not).

### *The six-tier business architecture*

The structural foundation for this paper is the six-tier ontology of Zharnikov (2026ag) (concept DOI 10.5281/zenodo.19895813). Tier 1 (Owner Intent, governed by owner psyche / board / charter) sits at the top of the constraint hierarchy. Tier 2 (Business Model, governed by market reality plus owner choice) translates Tier-1 intent into a viable economic structure. Tier 3 (Business Entity, governed by law and regulator) supplies the legal substrate. Tier 4 (Product, governed by customer or beneficiary acceptance) is the customer-facing instance. Tier 5 (Process, governed by management) and Tier 6 (Organization, governed by people and culture) supply the operational and human substrate. Zharnikov (2026ag) develops this ontology for acquisition-target transferability and proves seven falsifiable propositions about which tiers transfer cleanly across acquisition events. The current paper extends the same ontology to brand and the three downstream phenomena.

### *Spectral Brand Theory eight-dimension specification*

The per-instance Tier-4 specification is supplied by the Spectral Brand Theory eight-dimension framework (Zharnikov 2026a; concept DOI 10.5281/zenodo.18945912). The eight dimensions — Semiotic, Narrative, Ideological, Experiential, Social, Economic, Cultural, Temporal — are the canonical ordered axes on which a brand instance is specified. Each dimension is a continuous parameter; brand instances are points (or compact regions) in the eight-dimensional specification space. The eight dimensions are not the only specification possible; they are a *precision specification instrument* for Tier 4. Other instruments are admissible. The framework here is agnostic to which specification basis a firm uses; the SBT eight-dimension framework is the worked example.

## Brand as Tier-4 Projection

### *The Tier-4 ontological surface*

The formal claim. Brand IS Tier 4 in the six-tier architecture. Tier 4 is the customer-facing Product specification surface, governed by customer acceptance. The brand is the *specification* of that surface — the choice of which Semiotic, Narrative, Ideological, Experiential, Social, Economic, Cultural, and Temporal positions to instantiate (in the SBT eight-dimension case, or any equivalent specification basis). The brand is *not* the customer's perception of that specification; the perception lives downstream, after the projection through the observer perceptual filter. The brand is *not* the legal trademark; the trademark is a Tier-3 legal-entity artifact registered to protect the Tier-4 specification. The brand is *not* the marketing communications about the specification; communications are a Tier-6 organizational-function output that *operates on* the Tier-4 instance.

The Tier-4 specification inherits constraint from Tier 1 (Owner Intent). An owner's intent constrains which Tier-4 specifications are admissible. A founder whose intent is "premium quality at any price" cannot consistently specify a Tier-4 instance pursuing low-cost mass-market positioning; the constraint propagates from Tier 1 through the Tier-2 Business Model into the admissible Tier-4 specifications. The Tier-4 specification also rests *instrumentally* on Tier 5 (Process) and Tier 6 (Organization) — without operational and human substrate, the specification is unimplementable. But the specification itself lives at Tier 4. This separation matters because it allows the firm to vary its Tier-4 specification (relaunch, repositioning, brand extension, brand replacement) without disturbing Tiers 1, 2, 3, 5, or 6 — and conversely, to vary Tiers 5 or 6 (process redesign, organizational restructuring) without disturbing the Tier-4 brand instance the customer sees.

The Tier-4 specification also has a *temporal* character that is sometimes obscured in the literature. A Tier-4 instance is a specification at a moment in time; over time, firms vary the specification (relaunches, refreshes, extensions, reconceptualizations) on the same trademark. The Hatch and Schultz (2003) Vision–Culture–Image alignment construct treats the brand as a continuously-managed object; the framework here decomposes that continuous management into Tier-4 specification updates against persisting Tier 1 through Tier 3 plus Tier 5 and Tier 6 substrate. The Dove longitudinal trajectory examined in Zharnikov (2026p) is a worked example: a single Tier-4 brand instance evolves substantially on the SBT Ideological dimension over more than a decade while Tiers 1, 2, 3, 5, and 6 persist. Tier-4 evolution within a single brand identity is *not* recovery; it is specification drift on a stable substrate. Recovery, by contrast, is what happens when the Tier-4 specification fails and the firm must re-specify or replace it. The framework distinguishes the two, even though both look like "rebranding" to an outside observer.

The Hatch and Schultz (2003) corporate-brand alignment framework deserves a more substantive treatment because it is the closest existing precedent for treating brand as architecturally layered. Their Vision–Culture–Image (VCI) construct models the corporate brand as an alignment problem across three internal-and-external surfaces: top-management vision, organizational culture, and stakeholder image. VCI gives the field a vocabulary for misalignment failures (vision-culture gaps, culture-image gaps, vision-image gaps) and supplies a corporate-versus-product brand duality on which most subsequent corporate-branding work has been built. What VCI does *not* do is decompose the firm beneath the brand surface. The framework treats the brand as a single layer (the corporate brand), examines alignment among three internal facets of that layer, and stops at the corporate-versus-product brand distinction without articulating a tier ontology that locates *which* organizational layers are brand-bound and *which* are brand-agnostic. There is no transferability axis, no separability prediction across tiers, and no account of why some corporate-brand failures cascade through the firm while others remain confined to a single product line. The present six-tier framework extends VCI by treating Hatch and Schultz's three-facet alignment as a Tier-4-internal coherence problem operating on persisting Tier-1-through-Tier-3 substrate plus Tier-5 and Tier-6 substrate; this re-positioning preserves their alignment construct while supplying the tier-level decomposition VCI was never designed to provide.

### *Projection through the observer perceptual filter*

Every Tier-4 specification is realized in customer perception only after passing through an observer perceptual filter. The observer's spectral profile (Zharnikov 2026a) determines which dimensions of the specification are amplified, attenuated, or inverted. The same Tier-4 specification produces different perceived brand instances in different cohorts. This is the *projection*. The brand-as-Tier-4 specification is the firm's controllable input; the *perception cloud* is the cohort-specific output. The framework here concerns the input side. The specification surface (Tier 4) is what the firm can manipulate; the perception cloud is what the firm can measure but cannot directly control.

The projection construct is consistent with Zharnikov (2026h)'s specification-impossibility result: the observer perceptual filter is non-degenerate, and no Tier-4 specification fully determines perceived brand. This is a constraint on the framework, not a contradiction of it. The Tier-4 specification is necessary but not sufficient; the projection makes the connection between specification and perception lossy by construction. The framework thus does not promise that a CMO controlling the Tier-4 specification controls the perception cloud directly. The CMO controls the input; the cohort spectrum controls the output. Diagnostics that conflate the two are diagnosing the wrong tier.

The projection framing also clarifies the relationship between Tier 4 and the perception-side constructs that dominate the brand-equity literature. Brand awareness, brand associations, perceived quality, and brand loyalty (the Aaker brand-equity components) are all properties of the perception cloud, downstream of the projection. They are measured on the observer side and integrate over many factors — the Tier-4 specification, the cohort's spectral profile, the marketing communications operating on Tier 4, the competitive perception cloud surrounding Tier 4, and historical exposure. The framework here does not replace these constructs; it identifies the *upstream* object (the Tier-4 specification) whose configuration partly determines them. A firm that wants to manage its perception cloud must manage its Tier-4 specification, manage its Tier-6 communication function operating on Tier 4, and accept that the cohort spectrum is a parameter it can measure but not directly set. The brand conviction strength of any cohort — how committed members are to the perceived brand — is a downstream consequence of Tier-4 specification stability over time on persisting Tier-1-through-Tier-6 substrate.

### *Brand as Tier-4 instance versus trademark as Tier-3 artifact*

A reviewer could object that "brand" colloquially means the legal-trademark asset registered to a firm, which is a Tier-3 (Business Entity) artifact, not a Tier-4 specification. The framework distinguishes these as categorically different objects. The trademark is a legal asset registered to a Tier-3 entity, conferring the legal right to defend a particular sign against infringement. The brand-as-Tier-4 specification is the customer-facing instance the trademark protects. The two co-occur but are not the same object. The trademark "Coca-Cola" registered with the United States Patent and Trademark Office to The Coca-Cola Company (a Tier-3 legal entity) is one object. The Coca-Cola Tier-4 specification — its Semiotic, Narrative, Ideological, Experiential, Social, Economic, Cultural, and Temporal coordinates — is a different object. A firm can hold a trademark with no live Tier-4 specification (a defensive registration). A firm can run a Tier-4 specification with the trademark registered to a different Tier-3 entity (a licensed brand). A firm can re-specify Tier 4 without re-filing the trademark (a relaunch). A firm can transfer the trademark across Tier-3 entities while preserving the Tier-4 specification (an asset sale).

This pre-emption tracks the ontological-mixing pre-emption in Zharnikov (2026ag) and is necessary to keep the framework's claims sharp. Multi-brand capacity is a property of how many *Tier-4 specifications* a firm can host, not how many *trademarks* it can register. Failed-brand recovery is recovery of a *Tier-4 specification* on persisting substrate, not legal disposition of a trademark. References to "brand" throughout the rest of the paper should be read in the Tier-4-specification sense unless explicitly noted; the legal-asset sense will be flagged with the word "trademark." The framework loses nothing by giving up the colloquial overload, and gains the ability to make sharp claims about which tier carries which property.

## Tier-Level Brand-Agnostic / Brand-Bound Decomposition

### *Per-tier decomposition*

The central operational claim is that each of the six tiers contains a mix of brand-agnostic and brand-bound subcomponents. The mix determines how separable that tier is across multiple Tier-4 brand instances. Table 1 supplies the per-tier decomposition.

**Table 1: Per-Tier Brand-Agnostic / Brand-Bound Decomposition.**

| Tier | Brand-agnostic subcomponent examples | Brand-bound subcomponent examples | Separability under multi-brand operation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Owner Intent | Strategic horizon; risk tolerance; portfolio preference | Founder-as-spokesperson; brand-face commitments | Low: owner intent constrains admissible Tier-4 specifications |
| 2. Business Model | Revenue-model class (subscription, transactional); channel structure; cost structure | Brand-bound channel partners; brand-specific pricing power | Medium: same Business Model can host multiple Tier-4 instances if channel allows |
| 3. Business Entity | Legal entity; tax structure; regulatory licenses; trademark registry | Trademarks tied to specific Tier-4 instances | Medium-high: one entity can host many trademarks; one trademark per Tier-4 instance |
| 4. Product | Specification surface itself — entire tier IS the brand instance | Entire tier is brand-bound by definition | Zero: each Tier-4 specification is a distinct brand instance |
| 5. Process | Manufacturing; logistics; analytics infrastructure; data pipelines; media-buying systems | Brand-specific creative production; brand-specific customer-service tone | High: shared manufacturing routinely hosts multiple Tier-4 instances; creative is per-brand |
| 6. Organization | Cross-brand functions (analytics, finance, legal, IT); shared services | Brand teams (brand managers, creative directors, brand-specific customer service) | High: shared services are brand-agnostic; brand teams are brand-bound |

*Notes*: The decomposition is firm-specific in detail but follows the same pattern across firms in a sector. P&G, Unilever, Inditex, and General Mills all instantiate high Tier-3, Tier-5, and Tier-6 separability with shared brand-agnostic substrate hosting many brand-bound Tier-4 instances. Tier 4 is the only tier with zero separability under multi-brand operation by construction; every other tier admits both shared and brand-specific subcomponents.

The diagnostic value of Table 1 is that it identifies, for each tier, the substrate that carries multi-brand capacity (the brand-agnostic subcomponents) and the substrate that does not (the brand-bound subcomponents). A firm that audits its operations along this decomposition can quantify the proportion of each tier that supports multi-brand operation. A firm whose Tier 5 is dominated by brand-bound creative production has low Tier-5 separability; a firm whose Tier 5 is dominated by general-purpose manufacturing has high Tier-5 separability. The same is true at Tier 6: a firm with deep Tier-6 brand-team specialization but shallow shared services has low Tier-6 separability; a firm with strong cross-brand shared services has high Tier-6 separability.

Two notes on the row structure deserve elaboration. First, Tier 1 (Owner Intent) appears as low-separability for a reason internal to the framework: Owner Intent is itself a single object — there is one principal whose Intent governs the firm — and that Intent cannot be partitioned across brands without producing the kind of Tier-1 inconsistency that Zharnikov (2026ag) shows produces failure cascades. A firm operating multiple brands does not host multiple Owner Intents; it hosts one Owner Intent whose admissible Tier-4 instances may include multiple brand specifications, but those specifications must be jointly compatible with the single upstream Intent. Branson's Virgin works as a multi-brand portfolio because the Tier-1 commitment ("challenger to incumbents wherever incumbents are complacent") is sufficiently general to admit multiple Tier-4 instances. A founder whose Tier-1 commitment is brand-bound — "I built this brand and I am this brand" — cannot host a multi-brand portfolio without first re-specifying Tier 1, which most founders are unwilling to do.

Second, the Tier-5 row distinguishes the kind of process that can be shared from the kind that cannot. Manufacturing infrastructure, logistics networks, analytics pipelines, customer-data platforms, and media-buying systems are routinely shared across brands. P&G shares Tide and Pampers through one media-buying operation. Inditex shares Zara, Massimo Dutti, and Pull&Bear through one centralized logistics infrastructure. Creative production, brand-specific copy, brand-specific tone of voice, and brand-specific customer-service scripts are not routinely shared because they are *constitutive* of the Tier-4 specification at each brand. A media buyer is fungible across brands because a media slot is not part of the brand specification; a creative director is not fungible across brands because creative output specifies the brand. The Tier-5 / Tier-6 split between brand-agnostic and brand-bound functions is therefore not a question of cost — it is a question of whether the function's output specifies Tier 4 (brand-bound) or operates on Tier 4 from outside (brand-agnostic).

Tier 4 is the only tier whose entire content is brand-bound by construction; multi-brand capacity is therefore a property of how separable the *other five tiers* are.

### *The Tier-3-visibility-in-Tier-4 parameter*

The decomposition in Table 1 enables a single parameter that captures the Aaker–Joachimsthaler (2000) Brand Relationship Spectrum continuously rather than typologically. *Tier-3 visibility in Tier-4* — denoted V — is the degree to which the Tier-3 corporate identity is exposed at the Tier-4 brand instance. V = 1 means the corporate identity is fully visible (a branded house: Virgin Atlantic carries the Virgin Tier-3 imprint at every Tier-4 surface). V = 0 means the corporate identity is hidden (a house of brands: P&G's Tier-3 identity is invisible behind Tide, Pampers, Gillette, and Pantene). Sub-brand and endorsed-brand architectures occupy intermediate values of V.

The parameter projects onto the SBT Social and Economic dimensions. High V increases shared Social positioning across Tier-4 instances (the Virgin "challenger" Social position propagates) and shared Economic positioning (the Virgin price-tier signal propagates). Low V allows independent Social and Economic positioning per Tier-4 instance (P&G's Tide and Gillette occupy entirely different Social and Economic positions).

**P7 — Aaker Spectrum Operationalization.**

*Statement.* The Aaker–Joachimsthaler (2000) Brand Relationship Spectrum is operationalizable as a single Tier-3-visibility-in-Tier-4 parameter V projecting onto the SBT Social and Economic dimensions, making the spectrum measurable rather than only typological.

*Mechanism.* V is a continuous parameter on [0, 1]; spectrum positions correspond to V-value ranges; the SBT Social and Economic axes carry the propagation of Tier-1 / Tier-3 imprint into Tier-4 perception.

*Empirical anchor.* Aaker and Joachimsthaler (2000); Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016); Strebinger and Treiblmaier (2018); Zharnikov (2026a).

*Confirming criteria.* Inter-rater agreement on V across raters using the SBT eight-dimension coding scheme exceeds .80; V correlates with the Hsu et al. (2016) five-class taxonomy at r > .75; V predicts firm-value outcomes (e.g., the Hsu et al. risk-return profiles) above and beyond the discrete-class assignment.

*Falsifying criteria.* V cannot be reliably coded; or V adds no predictive power over the discrete-class taxonomy; or the projection onto SBT Social and Economic axes does not carry the propagation effect.

### *Multi-brand capacity as separability profile*

Multi-brand capacity — the number of Tier-4 brand instances a firm can host on its existing substrate without performance degradation — is a function of the firm's separability profile across Tiers 3, 5, and 6. Tier-2 model diversification is *not* the binding constraint; a firm with one Business Model can host many brands as long as Tier-3, Tier-5, and Tier-6 separability is high. Conversely, a firm with many Business Models but tightly-coupled Tier-3, Tier-5, or Tier-6 cannot host many brands. P&G's two hundred-plus brands operate within a small set of Business Model classes (mass consumer packaged goods, premium beauty) but on extraordinarily separable Tier 3, 5, and 6 substrate. Tesla, by contrast, operates several Business Models (vehicles, energy storage, services) on tightly-coupled Tier 3 (one entity) and Tier 6 (one design and engineering organization) with low-V branded-house architecture.

The prediction inverts a long-standing intuition in the strategy literature that diversification at the business-unit level is the relevant capacity constraint. Under the framework, diversification at the Tier-2 Business Model level is neither necessary nor sufficient for multi-brand capacity. It is not necessary because P&G hosts many brands within one Business Model class. It is not sufficient because a firm with multiple Business Models on a tightly-coupled Tier 3, Tier 5, or Tier 6 substrate cannot extend to multiple brands within any one of those Business Models. The relevant capacity question is structural separability across Tier 3, Tier 5, and Tier 6 — not Business Model diversification. The framework re-specifies what the brand-portfolio literature has been measuring under the heading of "portfolio strategy."

**P1 — Multi-Brand Capacity.**

*Statement.* Portfolio capacity — the number of Tier-4 brand instances a firm can host on its existing business substrate without performance degradation — scales with Tier-3, Tier-5, and Tier-6 independence in the firm's separability profile, and *not* with Tier-2 Business Model diversification.

*Mechanism.* Brand binds at Tier 4 only. Tier 3 (Business Entity) carries trademark plurality; Tier 5 (Process) carries shared manufacturing, logistics, and analytics infrastructure; Tier 6 (Organization) carries shared services. Tier 2 (Business Model) constrains admissible Tier-4 specifications but does not host them.

*Empirical anchor.* Morgan and Rego (2009); Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016); Ward, Beal, Dawes, Trinh and Romaniuk (2026); Nguyen, Zhang and Calantone (2018). P&G, Unilever, and Inditex are high-Tier-3-5-6-separability firms hosting many Tier-4 instances under one Business Model class.

*Confirming criteria.* Across a multi-firm panel, multi-brand capacity (count of distinct Tier-4 instances at which the firm achieves median sector profitability) correlates positively (r > .40) with a constructed Tier-3-Tier-5-Tier-6 separability index and weakly or not at all (|r| < .15) with a Tier-2 Business Model diversification index.

*Falsifying criteria.* Capacity is independent of Tier-3-5-6 separability; or capacity is primarily explained by Tier-2 Business Model diversification.

## Multi-Brand Architectures Mapped to Tier Profiles

### *The four architectures as separability profiles*

Each of the four canonical Aaker–Joachimsthaler architectures is a separability profile. Table 2 maps them onto the framework's variables.

**Table 2: Architecture-to-Separability Mapping.**

| Architecture | Tier-3 visibility in Tier-4 (V) | Tier-5 separability | Tier-6 separability | Risk-return profile |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Branded House (Virgin, FedEx) | V ≈ 1 | Low–medium (shared operations, brand-bound assets) | Low (shared organization) | High Tier-1 systemic risk; high upside on Tier-1 strength |
| Sub-brand (Toyota / Lexus) | V ≈ .6–.8 | Medium (separate dealer networks for sub-brands) | Medium (separate dealer organization) | Moderate Tier-1 isolation; moderate upside |
| Endorsed (Marriott Courtyard) | V ≈ .3–.5 | High (shared back-office, separate customer-facing operations) | Medium-high | Low Tier-1 systemic risk; selective Tier-3 amplification |
| House of Brands (P&G) | V ≈ 0 | High (separate manufacturing lines or shared with brand-bound creative) | High (brand teams independent) | Low Tier-1 systemic risk; per-brand Tier-4 risk only |

*Notes*: V is the Tier-3-visibility-in-Tier-4 parameter introduced in the Tier-Level Brand-Agnostic / Brand-Bound Decomposition section. Risk-return columns track the Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016) findings, reanalyzed through the tier lens.

Virgin is the canonical *branded-house* case and a clean flip-test for the framework. Virgin is routinely treated as a "brand" in the colloquial sense, but it operates as an architecture in which Tier 1 (Owner Intent — Branson's challenger ethos) and Tier 3 (Virgin Group as licensor) propagate across Tier-2 Business Model variants (airlines, telecom, finance, space). The Tier-4 instance for Virgin Atlantic and the Tier-4 instance for Virgin Money differ on most SBT dimensions, but both inherit a strong Tier-3 imprint visible in the SBT Social and Economic dimensions. The framework correctly classifies Virgin as a high-V architecture in which Tier-1 propagation is the dominant mechanism. Competing portfolio-architecture frameworks classify Virgin only by relationship-spectrum position and miss the propagation mechanism.

Toyota–Lexus is the canonical *sub-brand* case. Lexus is a Tier-4 specification with substantial independence on the SBT Cultural, Experiential, and Economic dimensions, but with explicit Tier-3 endorsement by the Toyota corporate parent. Tier 5 is partially separated: Lexus runs on dedicated production lines and separate dealer networks. Tier 6 is partially separated: Lexus has its own brand organization while sharing engineering with the Toyota parent. V sits in the .6 to .8 range — the corporate parent is visible at the Tier-4 surface but does not dominate it.

Marriott Courtyard is the canonical *endorsed* case. The Courtyard Tier-4 specification differs from the Marriott parent on the SBT Economic dimension (mid-tier rather than full-service), but the Marriott endorsement appears at the Tier-3 level on the property and in booking systems. Tier 5 (back-office reservations and loyalty infrastructure) is fully shared; Tier 6 (property management and customer service) is partially separated by brand. V sits in the .3 to .5 range.

P&G is the canonical *house of brands*. Tide, Pampers, Gillette, and Pantene have no Tier-3 corporate visibility at the Tier-4 surface; each Tier-4 instance is independent in SBT Social, Economic, Cultural, and Experiential dimensions. Tier 3 is highly shared (one corporate entity registers all trademarks); Tier 5 is highly separable (manufacturing lines vary by category but are shared within each); Tier 6 is highly separable (brand teams operate independently with shared services for analytics, finance, legal, and IT). V approaches zero.

### *Empirical reanalysis through the tier lens*

The Rao, Agarwal and Dahlhoff (2004) finding that corporate (branded-house) branding raises Tobin's q and the Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016) finding that house-of-brands architectures lower firm-specific risk are both predicted by the framework. Branded-house architectures concentrate risk and return at Tier 1; house-of-brands architectures isolate Tier 4 from Tier-1 / Tier-2 shocks. The mechanism is the Tier-3-visibility parameter V: at V ≈ 1, every Tier-1 / Tier-2 shock propagates into every Tier-4 instance through the visible corporate imprint, generating concentrated risk-return; at V ≈ 0, Tier-1 / Tier-2 shocks affect the corporate parent's financials but do not propagate to the Tier-4 perception cloud at any individual brand. The Wiles, Morgan and Rego (2012) finding that brand-acquisition and -disposal returns are asymmetric by marketing-capability fit is consistent with Tier-6 separability being the critical moderator. The Umashankar, Bahadir and Bharadwaj (2022) finding that customer satisfaction (a Tier-4 outcome) declines in M&A even when efficiency (a Tier-3 outcome) improves is direct evidence of the Tier-3 / Tier-4 separation. Bahadir, Bharadwaj and Srivastava (2008) measure transferred brand value as a Tier-3-asset event without the Tier-4 perception consequences that Umashankar et al. later document; the framework reconciles the two literatures by distinguishing what transfers (Tier 3, by document) from what does not transfer (Tier 4, which must be re-specified into the new Tier-3 host).

**P3 — Architecture Risk-Return.**

*Statement.* Branded-house architectures expose all Tier-4 instances to Tier-1 / Tier-2 shocks; house-of-brands architectures isolate Tier-4 instances from Tier-1 / Tier-2 shocks. The risk-return profiles documented in Rao, Agarwal and Dahlhoff (2004) and Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016) are derivable from this isolation parameter.

*Mechanism.* Tier-3-visibility-in-Tier-4 parameter V controls the propagation of Tier-1 / Tier-2 shocks into Tier-4 perception. High V means high propagation; low V means isolation. The Spectral Immunity result of Zharnikov (2026ac) — that awareness-gate sufficiency inversion isolates Tier-4 instances perceptually as well as structurally — supplies the perceptual companion to the structural prediction.

*Empirical anchor.* Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016); Rao, Agarwal and Dahlhoff (2004); Umashankar, Bahadir and Bharadwaj (2022); Zharnikov (2026ac).

*Confirming criteria.* Cross-firm panel: V predicts firm-specific stock risk (negatively) and Tobin's q response to Tier-1 / Tier-2 shock events (positively for high-V firms when Tier-1 is strong, negatively when Tier-1 is weak).

*Falsifying criteria.* V is uncorrelated with risk concentration at Tier 1 / Tier 2.

### *Boundary cases*

Digital-platform multi-brand architectures (Salesforce Connect, Shopify storefronts, Stripe Connect, multi-tenant SaaS) stack the framework: the platform owner runs one Tier-4 specification on its own substrate; tenant brands run their own Tier-4 specifications on substrate the platform owner provides for Tier 3 (regulated services, payment rails), Tier 5 (transactional infrastructure), and Tier 6 (customer-support routing). Each layer is a complete instance of the framework. The framework predicts that platform-level multi-brand capacity is bounded by tenant-Tier-4 specification independence under platform-mediated Tier-3, Tier-5, and Tier-6 — which is empirically what platform-quality concerns (tenant fraud, brand-safety) measure. The platform brand-management problem is, under the framework, a problem of operating one Tier-4 instance whose perception cloud is partially driven by the Tier-4 instances of its tenants. The treatment here is a sketch; a fuller theory of stacked-instance architectures is deferred to future work.

## Failed-Brand Recovery as Tier-Level Salvage

### *Three recovery pathways*

The framework identifies three structurally distinct recovery pathways under brand failure, distinguished by which tiers persist and which are re-specified.

*Pathway A — Re-collapse on existing infrastructure.* The Tier-4 specification fails (typically through perceived consumer rejection), all other tiers persist intact, and the firm re-collapses to the original Tier-4 specification or a near-neighbor. *Tropicana 2009* is the canonical case: the redesigned Tier-4 specification (new packaging, new Semiotic surface) failed within weeks; PepsiCo re-collapsed to the original Tier-4 within two months. Tier 1 through Tier 3 and Tier 5 through Tier 6 persisted unchanged; the failure was confined to a single Tier-4 re-specification that was reversed. The framework names the SBT-specific phenomenon "re-collapse" (Zharnikov 2026a) precisely because the firm returns to a near-neighbor specification rather than reconstructing the brand from scratch.

*Pathway B — New brand on same brand-agnostic substrate.* The Tier-4 specification fails (or is intentionally retired), Tiers 1 through 3 plus 5 through 6 persist with adaptation, and a new Tier-4 specification is launched. Three sub-cases recur in the literature. Lululemon → Ivivva (2009 launch as cohort-targeted new brand on shared Lululemon substrate, eventually folded back in 2017) is the cohort-extension case; the Lululemon Tier 1 (founder Owner Intent), Tier 2 (premium athleisure Business Model), Tier 3 (Lululemon legal entity), and Tiers 5 and 6 persisted; only Tier 4 was new. BlackBerry phones to BlackBerry enterprise security is the cross-Tier-2 case (a new Tier-2 Business Model on the same Tier-3 entity); the Tier-3 entity persisted and the Tier-1 owner persisted, but Tier 2 was substantially re-specified, and Tier 4 followed. The Pillsbury–Häagen-Dazs trajectory is the absorbed-brand-on-stable-parent-substrate case: a Tier-4 specification (Häagen-Dazs) acquired and operated on the parent Pillsbury Tier-3 / Tier-5 / Tier-6 substrate continued to grow as a Tier-4 instance even as Pillsbury itself underwent corporate transitions, illustrating that Tier-4 brand life can be longer than the Tier-3 entity that hosts it at any particular moment.

*Pathway C — Sell or shut down.* The Tier-4 specification fails and the firm cannot or chooses not to preserve the substrate. Pathway C subdivides into (i) sell — the substrate transfers to a new Tier-1 owner; (ii) shut down — *Pets.com 2000* is the canonical termination case where Tier 3 dissolves with the brand. In the sell sub-case, the Tier-3 entity (or its core assets) transfers to a new Tier-1 owner who may run an entirely new Tier-2 Business Model; the prior Tier-4 specification rarely survives the transition intact and typically operates either as a salvaged secondary brand or is retired. In the shutdown sub-case, all six tiers are dissolved; the trademark may persist as a defensive registration on some other Tier-3 entity but no live Tier-4 specification operates on it.

### *The salvage matrix*

The salvage matrix in the table below operationalizes the three pathways.

**Table 3: Per-Tier Salvage Matrix.**

| Tier | Salvageable under Pathway A | Salvageable under Pathway B | Salvageable under Pathway C(sell) | Salvageable under Pathway C(shutdown) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Owner Intent | Yes (unchanged) | Yes (must accept new Tier 4) | Transfers to new owner | Lost |
| 2. Business Model | Yes | Possibly modified | Transfers | Lost |
| 3. Business Entity | Yes | Yes | Transfers (or asset sale) | Dissolved |
| 4. Product | Re-specified to original | Re-specified de novo | Transfers | Lost |
| 5. Process | Yes | Yes | Transfers | Lost or auctioned |
| 6. Organization | Yes (some attrition) | Yes (some attrition) | Transfers (some attrition) | Dispersed |

*Notes*: "Yes" means the tier persists materially intact through the recovery event. The matrix is the operational backbone of the *Recovery Salvage Matrix* diagnostic instrument introduced in the Practical contribution subsection of the Discussion. Boards weighing recovery strategy after brand failure can run the matrix in advance to forecast which pathway is feasible given their substrate audit.

The salvage matrix supplies an empirical prediction. Across a corpus of failed-brand recovery events, the proportion of tiers preserved through the failure should correlate with the probability that the new Tier-4 specification reaches the predecessor's revenue band within thirty-six months. The Marques et al. (2020) finding — equity transferred but not created — is consistent with this prediction: in cases where Tier 1 and Tier 2 are bound to the prior Tier-4, the new Tier-4 cannot generate net-new equity because the owner's commitments are upstream of the brand specification. P2 makes this prediction precise.

**P2 — Recovery Substrate.**

*Statement.* Failed-brand recovery success — the probability that the new Tier-4 instance on the salvaged substrate reaches the predecessor's revenue band within thirty-six months — scales with the proportion of brand-agnostic infrastructure (Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3, Tier 5, Tier 6 components untouched by the failure event) preserved through the failure.

*Mechanism.* Equity transfers along persisting substrate (the Marques et al. 2020 finding); net-new equity creation requires changes at Tier 1 or Tier 2.

*Empirical anchor.* Marques, Vinhas da Silva, Davcik and Tamagnini Faria (2020); Tropicana 2009 case; Lululemon-Ivivva case; Pillsbury–Häagen-Dazs trajectory; Zharnikov (2026p) longitudinal Tier-4 evolution.

*Confirming criteria.* Across a corpus of failed-brand recovery events, thirty-six-month revenue-recovery rate correlates positively (r > .35) with a per-event preserved-substrate-proportion score.

*Falsifying criteria.* Recovery rate is independent of preserved substrate; or recovery rate is primarily explained by post-failure marketing investment with no role for substrate persistence.

### *Tier-1 Owner Intent as binding constraint on recovery*

The framework predicts that Tier-1 Owner Intent compatibility with the new Tier-4 specification is the binding constraint on Pathway-B recovery success — *not* Tier-5 or Tier-6 capacity availability. An owner whose Tier-1 commitments are bound to the failed Tier-4 cannot launch a successful new Tier-4 even when Tier 5 and Tier 6 are fully preserved. The Marques et al. (2020) finding that equity *transfers* but does not get *created* is consistent with this constraint: in cases where Tier 1 is bound to the prior Tier-4, the new Tier-4 cannot generate net-new equity because the owner's commitments are upstream of the brand specification. Yakimova, Mavondo, Freeman and Stuart (2021) document the internal-perspective dynamics of this constraint: when employees and owners experience the rebranding as identity rupture rather than as Tier-4 re-specification, the new Tier-4 fails to take.

**P6 — Recovery Tier-1 Constraint.**

*Statement.* Brand recovery is more constrained by Tier-1 (Owner Intent) compatibility with the new Tier-4 configuration than by Tier-5 / Tier-6 capacity availability. Owners with Tier-1 misalignment to the new brand-face fail recovery even when substrate is fully preserved.

*Mechanism.* Tier 1 sits at the top of the constraint hierarchy; admissible Tier-4 specifications are bounded by Tier-1 commitments. Tier-5 / Tier-6 capacity is necessary but downstream.

*Empirical anchor.* Marques, Vinhas da Silva, Davcik and Tamagnini Faria (2020); Yakimova, Mavondo, Freeman and Stuart (2021); Zharnikov (2026h).

*Confirming criteria.* In a recovery corpus, Tier-1 misalignment (founder unwilling to abandon prior brand-face commitments) predicts failure independent of preserved Tier-5 / Tier-6 substrate.

*Falsifying criteria.* Tier-1 alignment is uncorrelated with recovery outcome once Tier-5 / Tier-6 substrate is controlled.

The brand-extension literature provides an additional empirical handle on the Tier-4 transfer mechanism. P5 makes the axis-specific transfer prediction at the dimensional level.

**P5 — Brand Extension SBT-Overlap.**

*Statement.* Brand-extension success is predicted to scale with SBT-dimensional overlap between parent and extension on the Cultural and Ideological dimensions; failure is reliably predicted by mismatch on those axes.

*Mechanism.* The Cultural and Ideological dimensions are the *highest-transfer* axes in the SBT specification basis; Experiential and Economic are the *lowest-transfer*. Brand extension that aligns on high-transfer axes succeeds; misalignment on those axes fails even when fit on low-transfer axes is high. Park, Milberg and Lawson (1991)'s finding that concept consistency dominates feature similarity for symbolic brands is the closest existing precursor; Torelli and Ahluwalia (2012) provide the cleanest existing evidence that cultural-schema overlap can override perceived feature fit.

*Empirical anchor.* Aaker and Keller (1990); Park, Milberg and Lawson (1991); Torelli and Ahluwalia (2012); Spiggle, Nguyen and Caravella (2012); Peng, Bijmolt, Völckner and Zhao (2023) meta-analysis.

*Confirming criteria.* Re-coded extension corpus on the SBT eight axes; Cultural plus Ideological alignment predicts extension success above and beyond standard fit and parent-equity controls.

*Falsifying criteria.* Cultural plus Ideological alignment adds no predictive power over standard fit; or other axes (Experiential, Economic) dominate transfer prediction.

## Marketing, Advertising, and Branding as Cross-Brand Tier-6 Functions

### *Tier-6 marketing function and Tier-4 brand instances*

Marketing is a Tier-6 organizational function whose work product is the creation, operation, and retirement of Tier-4 brand instances. Advertising is the Tier-6 sub-function dedicated to message emission *from* a Tier-4 specification *to* downstream observer cohorts. Branding is the Tier-6 strategic-selection sub-function that decides which Tier-4 specifications a firm should run, which to retire, and which to re-specify. The CMO is a portfolio manager of Tier-4 configurations, exercising Tier-6 capabilities. The marketing function is brand-agnostic in its analytic and infrastructural subcomponents (media buying, marketing-mix modeling, marketing analytics, customer-data platforms, marketing-finance accountability) and brand-bound in its creative and brand-management subcomponents (brand teams, creative production, brand-specific customer-service tone).

This framing resolves the marketing-organization debates documented by McAlister, Germann, Grewal and Lehmann (2023) and Whitler, Rego and Morgan (2025) as tier-level scope questions. The Whitler, Rego and Morgan (2025) finding that more than half of CMO roles are misaligned along scope and authority dimensions is, under the framework, a misallocation of Tier-6 capabilities across Tier-4 instances — too much on a single brand, too little on the portfolio, or a confusion of the Tier-6 portfolio manager role with a single-Tier-4 custodian role. The McAlister et al. (2023) Marcom Leaders cluster is the empirical signature of Tier-6 marketing functions explicitly operating on portfolios of Tier-4 instances; the Service Providers cluster aligns with Tier-6 functions operating Tier-4 plus heavy Tier-5 process work; the Growth Champions cluster straddles Tier 2 and Tier 4. Boyd, Chandy and Cunha (2010) and Germann, Ebbes and Grewal (2015) document conditions under which CMO presence creates firm value; the framework predicts that the conditions reduce to Tier-2 / Tier-4 fit, which is what these papers have shown without naming the tier structure.

The Edeling, Srinivasan and Hanssens (2021) accountability-gap finding (a large majority of firms hold marketing responsible for the brand but only a small minority hold marketing accountable for stock-market performance) is, under the framework, predictable: marketing is responsible at Tier 4 (where the brand lives) but Tier 1 / Tier 2 / Tier 3 own the firm-value linkage. The accountability mismatch is a tier-level scope mismatch, not a measurement failure. A firm that wants to hold marketing accountable for firm value must either expand the marketing function's authority into Tier-2 Business Model decisions (which most firms reserve for the CEO or board) or measure marketing on Tier-4 outcomes (which most firms find too narrow to satisfy investors). The framework names the trade-off the literature has only described.

The framework also clarifies what advertising is *not*. Advertising is not the Tier-4 specification. It is a Tier-6 function whose work product modulates which subset of an existing Tier-4 specification reaches an observer cohort. An advertising campaign that emphasizes the SBT Cultural and Ideological dimensions of an existing Tier-4 instance does not change the Tier-4 specification; it changes which dimensions of the projection a cohort sees. Conversely, a brand relaunch that re-specifies the Tier-4 instance is not an advertising activity even when the relaunch is announced through advertising channels. The two functions can be performed by the same Tier-6 sub-function but they are operations on different objects. The Tropicana 2009 case illustrates the distinction sharply: PepsiCo re-specified Tier 4 (Pathway A in the Failed-Brand Recovery section) using channels that the marketing function operated; the failure was not an advertising failure but a Tier-4 specification failure that the advertising function executed faithfully. The framework allows the two failure modes to be distinguished diagnostically.

### *Centralization versus decentralization as tier-separability choice*

The framework re-specifies the marketing centralization-versus-decentralization debate as a tier-level separability choice. Functions with Tier-4-bound output (creative, brand management, customer-service tone) belong with the Tier-4 instance. Functions with Tier-4-agnostic output (media buying, analytics, data infrastructure, marketing-mix modeling, finance, legal) belong with the firm's shared Tier-6 services. The optimal degree of centralization is determined by where on the Tier-4-bound versus Tier-4-agnostic spectrum a function sits. Centralization decisions made on grounds of cost reduction alone — without explicitly auditing which functions are Tier-4-bound — predictably produce the misaligned CMO roles documented by Whitler, Rego and Morgan (2025).

**P4 — Cross-Brand Marketing Effectiveness.**

*Statement.* Cross-brand marketing-function effectiveness is a function of Tier-6 governance separation: shared services for brand-agnostic functions (media buying, analytics, marketing-mix modeling, platform tooling); per-brand teams for brand-bound functions (creative, brand management, customer-service tone).

*Mechanism.* Tier-6 separability optimizes when Tier-6 sub-functions are partitioned along their Tier-4-bound versus Tier-4-agnostic split.

*Empirical anchor.* McAlister, Germann, Grewal and Lehmann (2023); Whitler, Rego and Morgan (2025); Verhoef and Leeflang (2009); Edeling, Srinivasan and Hanssens (2021).

*Confirming criteria.* Marketing organizations whose Tier-6 partition aligns with the Tier-4-bound versus Tier-4-agnostic split outperform misaligned organizations on marketing-effectiveness metrics, controlling for firm size and sector.

*Falsifying criteria.* No effect of partition alignment on effectiveness.

## Discussion and Validation Roadmap

### *Theoretical contribution*

The framework unifies the brand-portfolio architecture, corporate-rebranding, and marketing-organization literatures under one tier-level decomposition. It supplies a finite-axis specification basis (the SBT eight-dimension framework) for Tier-4 instances. It distinguishes brand-as-Tier-4-projection from brand-as-Tier-3-trademark-artifact, pre-empting an ontological-mixing reviewer objection. It predicts the Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016) risk-return asymmetries and the Umashankar, Bahadir and Bharadwaj (2022) M&A customer-satisfaction asymmetry as consequences of which tier carries the boundary. It connects to the brand-extension literature with an axis-specific transfer prediction (P5) along the SBT Cultural and Ideological dimensions. And it positions marketing-as-cross-brand-function as a Tier-6 partition problem, recovering the McAlister et al. (2023) behavioral taxonomy and the Whitler, Rego and Morgan (2025) misalignment finding as predictable consequences of Tier-6 design choices.

### *Practical contribution*

Two diagnostic instruments emerge from the framework. The *Multi-Brand Capacity Diagnostic* (MBCD) extends the Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic from Zharnikov (2026ag) to predict multi-brand capacity. Inputs: the firm's per-tier brand-agnostic / brand-bound decomposition, scored on each tier on a five-point scale of separability under multi-brand operation. Output: the maximum number of Tier-4 brand instances the firm can host on its current substrate without performance degradation, with diagnostic decomposition by tier. The MBCD is operationalizable from existing firm data on Tier-3, Tier-5, and Tier-6 separability and is described in Table 4.

**Table 4: Multi-Brand Capacity Diagnostic (MBCD) Instrument Specification.**

| Component | Input | Scoring rule | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier-1 audit | Owner Intent statements; founder-as-spokesperson commitments | 1 (fully bound) – 5 (fully separable) | Tier-1 separability score s₁ |
| Tier-2 audit | Business Model class diversity; channel structure across brands | 1 (single bound) – 5 (multiple separable) | s₂ |
| Tier-3 audit | Trademark count; entity count; regulatory license overlap | 1 (single overloaded) – 5 (modular) | s₃ |
| Tier-5 audit | Manufacturing share; analytics infrastructure share; media-buying share | 1 (per-brand bound) – 5 (fully shared) | s₅ |
| Tier-6 audit | Cross-brand shared services; brand-team independence | 1 (per-brand bound) – 5 (fully shared) | s₆ |
| Tier-3-visibility-in-Tier-4 | V parameter from the Tier-3-visibility-in-Tier-4 parameter subsection | Continuous on [0, 1] | V |
| Capacity prediction | Weighted combination of s₃, s₅, s₆ moderated by V | Composite formula (proposed) | Predicted multi-brand capacity |

*Notes*: Tier 4 is excluded from the audit because Tier 4 is the brand instance itself; capacity is measured in Tier-4 units. Weights on s₃, s₅, s₆ are calibrated empirically against firm-level capacity benchmarks. V moderates the relationship between separability and capacity by indexing how much Tier-1 propagation reduces the effective independence of Tier-4 instances. The composite formula is left for empirical specification rather than imposed a priori.

The *Recovery Salvage Matrix* converts Table 3 into a pre-failure infrastructure-audit instrument. Inputs: the per-tier salvage entries from Table 3 conditional on the firm's specific configuration. Output: under each of the three recovery pathways (A, B, C), which substrate is preserved, what residual value can be re-specified, and which Tier-1 commitments would be violated by Pathway B. Boards weighing recovery strategy after brand failure can run the matrix in advance.

### *Limitations*

Tier-2 brand-bondedness varies more in platform and two-sided markets than the framework's standard treatment suggests; the platform-stack boundary case treated in the Multi-Brand Architectures section is a sketch, not a developed theory. Cross-form extension to NGOs, cooperatives, and state-owned enterprises is deferred — Tier 1 in those forms is more diffuse and the framework's predictions would require adaptation, in line with the form-invariance treatment in Zharnikov (2026ag). The empirical-validation agenda below requires data the framework predicts but does not itself supply: a multi-firm panel coded on the V parameter, a corpus of brand-failure events coded for tier-level salvage, and a re-coded brand-extension dataset on the SBT axes. Each is feasible but represents new measurement work. Finally, the framework treats the SBT eight-dimension specification as the worked example of a Tier-4 specification basis. The theoretical claims do not depend on this specific basis; alternative specification instruments are admissible as long as they satisfy completeness and observer-projection properties.

### *Validation roadmap*

A four-priority empirical agenda follows from the propositions.

1. Operationalize the Tier-3-visibility-in-Tier-4 parameter V across a panel of branded-house, sub-brand, endorsed, and house-of-brands firms; test P3 (Architecture Risk-Return) by predicting Hsu, Fournier and Srinivasan (2016) risk-return profiles from V.

2. Construct the Recovery Salvage Matrix for a sample of brand failures (drawing from the failed-brand corpus implicitly assembled in Sangroya et al. 2026 SLR and supplementary practitioner sources); test P2 (Recovery Substrate) by correlating preserved-substrate proportion with thirty-six-month revenue recovery.

3. Test P5 (Brand Extension SBT-Overlap) by re-coding the Peng, Bijmolt, Völckner and Zhao (2023) meta-analytic effect-size database on the SBT eight axes; predict extension success from Cultural plus Ideological dimensional overlap, controlling for fit and parent equity.

4. Test P4 (Cross-Brand Marketing Effectiveness) by classifying marketing-organization configurations on the Tier-4-bound versus Tier-4-agnostic split; correlate with marketing-effectiveness measures from McAlister, Germann, Grewal and Lehmann (2023) and Verhoef and Leeflang (2009).

### *Concluding remark*

Multi-brand capacity, failed-brand recovery, and marketing-as-cross-brand-function are not three separate phenomena. They are three downstream manifestations of one structural fact: brand IS Tier 4, and Tier 4 is one layer in a six-tier business architecture. The framework supplies the unifying lens and two diagnostic instruments. Empirical validation is the next step.
