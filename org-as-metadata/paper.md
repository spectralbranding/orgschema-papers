## Abstract

Why do structurally distinct organizations produce equivalent value outputs? Equifinality — different paths to the same end state — cannot explain the more fundamental phenomenon: organizations with permanently different structures, executing the same process, produce output indistinguishable to a given evaluative observer. The paper names this condition organizational metamerism, borrowing from color science, where two physically distinct spectral distributions produce identical percepts under a given illuminant. The equivalence is observer-relative: configurations metameric for one observer need not be metameric for another, because observer disagreement follows from differences in evaluation function sensitivity. The paper locates organizational metamerism within a temporal stability hierarchy — a stability ordering of value outputs, processes, and configurations by characteristic rates of change — developed mechanistically in the Organizational Metamerism section. The metadata metaphor explains why the metamerism set expands as coordination costs fall, illustrated by the progressive absorption of coordination from Toyota's pull systems through API-first architectures to AI-mediated coordination. Tacit knowledge intensity is the primary boundary condition on set expansion. Four propositions are stated with explicit confirming and falsifying evidence criteria, including the prediction that AI-era organizations exhibit decoupled restructuring frequency from process change frequency. The theory reframes organizational change as schema migration.

**Keywords**: organizational metamerism, equifinality, organizational routines, temporal hierarchy, coordination costs, organizational form, AI and organizations, observer-relativity

Consider two software development organizations contracted to maintain the same platform. The first operates as a conventional project hierarchy: a vice president of engineering, three product managers, and fifteen engineers organized by feature area, with weekly sprint reviews and an escalation protocol for cross-team dependencies. The second is a self-organizing network: twelve engineers and two coordinators who rotate responsibility for integration, operating through an API-first architecture where coordination obligations are encoded in interface contracts rather than in authority relationships. Both organizations receive identical requirements, meet identical service-level agreements, and produce releases that the client's quality assurance team consistently rates as indistinguishable. A customer evaluating the delivered software — the output-evaluating observer — cannot tell which organization produced which release. Yet the organizations are structurally distinct and will remain so; neither is converging on the other's form. This is the phenomenon the present paper addresses.

The persistence of organizational diversity is one of organizational theory's most durable puzzles. If competitive selection should converge on efficient forms, and if institutions should converge on legitimate ones, why do we observe a sustained plurality of structural configurations executing comparable processes — family firms and corporate subsidiaries, cooperatives and proprietary platforms, flat and hierarchical organizations — that appear to produce equivalent value outputs? The standard answer draws on equifinality: multiple structural paths can lead to the same organizational outcome. Yet this answer, we argue, is incomplete in a specific and consequential way. Equifinality is a path-equivalence claim — different histories, different sequences of decisions, same destination. It does not address the observation that structurally distinct organizations can be simultaneously and permanently equivalent, not by having converged on the same destination, but by occupying different positions in an organizational form space that maps, for a particular evaluative observer, to the same value output.

This paper introduces organizational metamerism as the name for this condition. The term comes from color science, where metamerism describes the phenomenon in which two physically distinct spectral power distributions produce identical color percepts when projected through the cone sensitivities of a particular observer under a particular illuminant (Wyszecki and Stiles 1982). The equivalence is observer-relative: two spectra that are metamers under daylight may not be metamers under incandescent light. The metamerism concept has been previously extended from color science to multi-dimensional brand perception evaluation (Zharnikov 2026e); the present paper takes the next logical step, extending the same structural logic from brand signals to organizational configurations. The metamerism concept encodes several structural features that do not travel with equifinality: the equivalence is a state equivalence rather than a path equivalence, it is defined relative to an observer with particular evaluative sensitivities, and the set of equivalent configurations (the metamerism set) is a function of the observation conditions rather than a fixed structural property of the configurations themselves.

Three features make organizational metamerism a productive theoretical extension. First, it sharpens the ontological claim: we can characterize precisely what is preserved (value output as evaluated by a particular class of observer) and what varies (the organizational configuration), rather than asserting vaguely that "different structures achieve similar performance." Second, the observer-relativity generates a prediction that equifinality does not: different classes of stakeholders — workers, customers, regulators, algorithmic procurement systems — will disagree about which configurations are equivalent, because their evaluative sensitivities differ. Third, the metamerism set is a derived quantity: its size is a function of the coordination cost regime and the tacit knowledge intensity of the underlying process, not merely an empirical regularity to be catalogued.

The paper develops organizational metamerism by situating it within a temporal stability hierarchy — a stability ordering of value outputs, processes, and organizational configurations by their characteristic rates of change, asserted on the basis of structural observations about differential sensitivity to coordination technology and developed mechanistically in the Organizational Metamerism section — and by grounding this hierarchy in two deep literatures: the equifinality program initiated by Gresov and Drazin (1997), which is the paper's primary foil, and the organizational routines program of Pentland and Feldman (2005), which provides the most direct theoretical precedent for observer-relative equivalence in organizational behavior. We also distinguish the contribution from Beane and Leonardi's (2025) pace layering framework, which addresses rate-of-change heterogeneity across organizational layers but does not derive a state-equivalence result.

We offer a state-equivalence account that is jointly observer-relative, process-bound, and coordination-cost-derived. Pentland and Feldman (2005) supply the routine-level precedent; Puranam et al. (2014) frame the substitutability question; the present paper supplies the structural derivation at the configuration level — a mechanism with specific and testable implications for the AI era of organizational design.

The paper develops the theoretical foundations in Theoretical Foundations, engaging in depth with Gresov and Drazin (1997), Pentland and Feldman (2005), and Beane and Leonardi (2025). Organizational Metamerism defines the concept formally, develops the temporal stability hierarchy that explains why metamerism exists, and introduces the metadata metaphor as the explanatory mechanism. Propositions states the paper's four propositions with explicit evidence criteria. Implications develops theoretical and methodological implications. Discussion and Limitations addresses boundary conditions and future research directions.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Equifinality and Its Limits

The concept of equifinality was introduced to organizational theory from general systems theory by Katz and Kahn (1978), who argued that open systems can reach the same final state from different initial conditions through different paths. The concept challenges the assumptions of structural contingency theory — that there is a single best organizational form for each environment — by establishing that structural diversity need not imply performance diversity. Multiple structural configurations can be functionally adequate.

Gresov and Drazin (1997) provided the most systematic organizational development of equifinality, examining how multiple design configurations could satisfy the same performance requirements under conditions of task complexity and inter-unit interdependence. Their central contribution was demonstrating that equifinality is not a theoretical concession (admitting that researchers cannot identify the optimal form) but a theoretical prediction: when multiple functionally equivalent configurations exist, organizations will be found distributed across the space of equivalences rather than concentrated at a single optimum.

Doty, Glick, and Huber (1993) provided empirical support for this prediction, showing that organizations occupying hybrid configurational positions — not ideal types in the sense of Miles and Snow (1978) — could achieve equivalent effectiveness to pure-type organizations. Equifinality, in their framing, was a property of the performance landscape: multiple peaks, not a single summit.

Fiss (2011) advanced this program by distinguishing first-order from second-order equifinality using fuzzy-set qualitative comparative analysis. First-order equifinality refers to within-configuration causal heterogeneity: different combinations of conditions producing the same outcome. Second-order equifinality refers to between-configuration equivalence: different configurations producing the same outcome. This distinction maps closely onto the organizational metamerism question, but with a crucial difference. Fiss's second-order equifinality is still a path-equivalence or outcome-equivalence claim at the level of causal configurations. Organizational metamerism adds two further specifications that second-order equifinality lacks: (a) observer-relativity — configurations that are equivalent for one class of evaluative observer need not be equivalent for another, because the equivalence set is a function of the observer's dimensional sensitivities, not a fixed property of the configurations themselves; and (b) a precise mechanism — the expansion and contraction of the equivalence set is driven by coordination cost regime and tacit knowledge intensity, not merely catalogued as a property of the performance landscape. Organizational metamerism is closer to second-order equifinality in its between-configuration scope, but provides a derived theoretical account of when and for whom the equivalence holds. Furnari and colleagues (2021) extend the configurational program by providing heuristics for theorizing causally complex phenomena — including the identification of equifinal solution sets — and explicitly embrace the notion that multiple configurations can produce equivalent outcomes; the metamerism framework provides the missing structural account of the conditions under which such configurational equivalences hold simultaneously rather than emerging through convergent paths.

This body of work establishes two important facts that the organizational metamerism concept inherits: organizational form space is not unimodal in its relationship to performance, and the search for a single optimal structure is theoretically misguided. What the equifinality literature does not establish, and what the present paper argues it cannot establish, is the condition under which distinct configurations are simultaneously equivalent rather than merely convergently equivalent.

The distinction is not semantic. Consider two organizations executing the same software development process: one uses a conventional project-based hierarchy with a vice president, three managers, and fifteen engineers; the other uses a self-organizing team structure with rotating coordination roles. Equifinality, as formalized by Gresov and Drazin, applies if these two organizations arrived at equivalent performance by different developmental paths — the hierarchical firm by scaling from a founding team and the self-organizing firm by deliberately flattening. But this path story is not what is theoretically interesting about the case. What is interesting is that at this moment, in steady state, with the same process, both organizations produce equivalent output. The equivalence is not a historical coincidence; it is a structural property of the process-organization-observer triple.

The equifinality literature does not have a theoretical account of this steady-state simultaneous equivalence. Its central question is: "How many paths lead to the same destination?" The organizational metamerism question is: "How many simultaneously viable configurations produce equivalent output for a given observer evaluating a given process?" The equifinality question is answered by historical analysis — trace the paths. The metamerism question is answered by a structural analysis — characterize the observer's evaluation function and identify the configurations that project to the same value.

There is a further limitation. Gresov and Drazin's (1997) equifinality is performance-equivalence, where performance is typically operationalized as an organization-level outcome (effectiveness, financial performance, survival). The organizational metamerism concept specifies the invariant more precisely: the preserved quantity is value output as evaluated by a particular class of observer, and the equivalence holds relative to a particular underlying process. This triple — observer, process, organizational configuration — is the minimum unit of analysis for assessing metamerism.

This precision matters because it makes the metamerism set tractable: to know which configurations are metamers, one must know what process they are executing and who is evaluating the output. The equifinality literature, operating at the level of organizational effectiveness rather than process output evaluation, lacks this specificity and therefore cannot derive predictions about when the equivalence set expands or contracts.

The present paper's primary proposition about the metamerism set — that it expands as coordination costs fall and contracts as tacit knowledge intensity rises — cannot be derived from the equifinality literature because the equifinality literature treats the set of equivalent configurations as an empirical regularity rather than a derived quantity.

Table 1: Equifinality versus organizational metamerism on key theoretical dimensions.

| Dimension | Equifinality (Gresov and Drazin 1997) | Organizational metamerism |
|-----------|----------------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Type of equivalence | Path equivalence (different histories, same end state) | State equivalence (different configurations, simultaneously equivalent) |
| Observer-relativity | Configuration-independent: equivalence holds across observers | Observer-relative: equivalence depends on the evaluator's value criteria |

### Organizational Routines and the Ostensive-Performative Distinction

The closest theoretical precedent for observer-relative equivalence in organizational behavior is not in the equifinality literature but in the routines literature, and specifically in Pentland and Feldman's (2005) distinction between the ostensive and performative aspects of organizational routines.

Pentland and Feldman proposed that organizational routines have two analytically distinct aspects: the ostensive aspect, which is the abstract, generalized pattern of a routine as participants understand it ("this is how we do things here"), and the performative aspect, which is the specific enactment of the routine in a particular situation by particular people. The ostensive is what routines look like from the outside or from a position of abstraction; the performative is what they look like in action.

This distinction is immediately relevant to organizational metamerism in two ways. First, the ostensive aspect is observer-dependent in a way that the performative aspect is not. Different observers — employees, customers, researchers, regulators — hold different ostensive understandings of what a given routine is. The customer's ostensive understanding of a service delivery routine is built from the outputs she receives; the employee's understanding is built from the coordination actions she performs; the manager's understanding is built from the resource allocations and performance metrics she monitors. These ostensive representations may be systematically different, and they need not converge. What counts as "the same routine" varies by observer.

Second, the ostensive-performative distinction implies that two organizations executing nominally different routines — with different procedures, different role assignments, different authority structures — may produce performative enactments that are identical from the perspective of an observer who evaluates only the output. The output-evaluating observer perceives the two organizations as running the same routine, while an internal observer perceives them as running different routines. This is organizational metamerism at the routine level.

Pentland and Feldman were primarily concerned with the dynamics of routine change — how performative enactments diverge from ostensive understanding, and how this divergence drives endogenous change. Feldman and Pentland (2003) established the foundational mechanism: the performative aspect of routines is itself a source of endogenous change, as actors adapt and improvise, and this generativity means that ostensive patterns evolve through enactment rather than being imposed from outside. They were not seeking to characterize when different routines produce equivalent outputs. But their framework implies the possibility of what we might call routine metamerism: two ostensively different routines can produce performatively equivalent results for a given evaluative observer. The organizational metamerism concept generalizes this from the routine level to the organizational configuration level.

The connection to Pentland and Feldman deepens when we consider the temporal dimension of routines. The ostensive aspect of a routine is more stable than the performative aspect: the abstract pattern — "we run a weekly project review" — persists even as the specific enactments vary from week to week, from person to person, from context to context. This differential stability between the abstracted pattern and its enactments prefigures the temporal stability hierarchy introduced in the Organizational Metamerism section: higher-level abstractions (value outputs) are more stable than lower-level specifications (processes), which are more stable than the organizational configurations (coordination arrangements) that execute them.

The organizational metamerism concept extends Pentland and Feldman in the following specific way. Their ostensive-performative distinction applies to individual routines. The present paper applies the corresponding logic — abstract specification is more stable than configuration, and equivalence is observer-relative — to the full organizational level. What Pentland and Feldman establish for routines, organizational metamerism claims for the aggregate of routines that constitute an organization's productive activity. And just as the ostensive routine is observer-dependent in Pentland and Feldman's framework, the organizational metamerism set is observer-relative in the present paper's framework: which configurations are equivalent depends on what the evaluating observer is sensitive to.

This extension is not trivial. Moving from the routine level to the organizational level introduces a compositional problem: even if individual routines are metrically equivalent across configurations, their coordination may not be. Two organizations that run each process step identically but coordinate those steps differently may produce different aggregate outputs, because the coordination structure introduces errors, delays, or information losses that the individual routines do not. Organizational metamerism holds when the coordination differences between configurations do not produce detectable differences in value output. This is the condition that the temporal stability hierarchy and the metadata mechanism explain: when coordination is fully embedded in the process (as in a pull system or an API-first architecture), coordination differences between configurations approach zero, and the metamerism set expands to encompass configurations that differ only in their coordination overlay.

A further connection: Feldman and Pentland (2003), in the same research program, showed that the performative aspect of routines — the actual enactments — can be a source of endogenous change, as actors adapt, improvise, and deviate from the ostensive pattern. This finding establishes that organizational configurations are not inert executions of stable scripts; they are generative processes that can change their own ostensive patterns. This is consistent with the metadata framing introduced in the Organization as Metadata subsection: organizational form is not a static specification but a continuously re-enacted coordination schema that is both constituted by and constitutive of the processes it coordinates.

Recent work extends the routines program to the dynamics introduced by AI adoption. Mahringer, Danner-Schröder, Müller-Seitz, and Renzl (2024) identify automation and augmentation as the two dimensions along which AI affects routines, showing that AI can simultaneously promote routine change (by capacitating new actions or reframing patterns) and routine stability (by shielding existing actions or enforcing adherence). This finding is consistent with the metamerism framework's prediction: AI-mediated coordination embeds coordination logic in the process layer, stabilizing the ostensive routine specification (the value and process layers) while accelerating change at the organizational configuration layer — precisely the asymmetric volatility that Proposition 3 formalizes. Raisch and Krakowski (2021) sharpen the theoretical picture further: they argue that automation and augmentation are interdependent rather than separable, creating a paradoxical tension in which overemphasizing either fuels reinforcing cycles with negative organizational consequences. For the metamerism framework, this interdependence implies that the expansion of the metamerism set is not a smooth monotonic function of automation adoption — it is shaped by how organizations navigate the automation-augmentation tension, which differs across process codifiability regimes and tacit knowledge intensities.

### Pace Layering and the Distinction from Metamerism

Beane and Leonardi (2025), in their recent Journal of Management Studies paper, applied Stewart Brand's (1999) pace layering framework to organizational theory, arguing that organizations are hierarchies of interdependent processes evolving at different timescales. Their framework identifies multiple "pace layers" within the firm — from the fastest-changing digital coordination tools through intermediate management processes to the slow-changing relational and cultural substrates — and argues that effective organizing in the age of intelligent technologies requires attending to the differential rates of change across these layers.

The pace layering framework is the closest concurrent development to the present paper's temporal stability hierarchy, and the distinction between them requires precise statement. Beane and Leonardi's contribution is about rates of change: their central claim is that different organizational layers evolve at different rates, and that this rate heterogeneity has implications for how organizations should manage technological change. Their argument is temporal and dynamic: the fast layers innovate; the slow layers provide stability and memory; good organizational design manages the coupling between layers at different rates.

The present paper's temporal stability hierarchy makes a related but distinct claim. The hierarchy is not primarily a claim about rates of change (though it implies them); it is a claim about the ontological ordering of three levels of organizational abstraction — value outputs, processes, and configurations — by their characteristic stability. The hierarchy derives a state-equivalence result: because organizational configurations are the most volatile layer, they can change without disturbing the process layer above them, which means that configurations that are different at any given moment can be simultaneously equivalent in their process execution and value production. This is the metamerism claim, and it does not follow from pace layering alone.

To be precise: Beane and Leonardi can explain why organizational configurations change faster than processes (different pace layers, different rates of change). What they cannot explain, without additional theoretical machinery, is why configurations that differ at a given moment produce equivalent outputs at that moment. Rate-of-change heterogeneity implies sequential equivalence — at different points in time, different configurations might be similarly adequate. State-equivalence means simultaneous adequacy: configurations that are currently different are currently equivalent in their output. The former is a temporal claim; the latter is a structural claim.

Furthermore, the metamerism set — the collection of configurations that are equivalent for a given process and observer — is a derived theoretical concept that has no counterpart in pace layering theory. Pace layering describes the organization as a system with layers at different rates; it does not characterize the space of configurations that are substitutable for each other at a given rate. This characterization requires the observer-relative equivalence concept that organizational metamerism provides.

Pace layering's normative prescription — that organizations should manage cross-layer coupling effectively — could in principle generate substitutability claims at the limit, but it does not derive a state-equivalence result from first principles. Where pace layering specifies how layers should evolve at differential rates, organizational metamerism specifies a structural property holding among configurations at any moment.

The relationship between the two frameworks is therefore complementary rather than competitive. Pace layering explains why the organizational layer is the fastest-changing: it is the metadata for processes, and as coordination costs fall (for pace layering: as intelligent technologies accelerate the fast layers), the organizational layer is the first to shift. Organizational metamerism explains what the space of equivalent configurations at any given moment looks like: its size is a function of coordination costs and tacit knowledge, and it is observer-relative. Rate-of-change analysis and state-equivalence analysis are different cuts through the same underlying organizational structure.

One further distinction: Beane and Leonardi (2025) ground their framework in empirical analysis of how organizations actually manage the coupling between pace layers. The present paper is a theoretical contribution that derives state-equivalence from first principles, without claiming empirical grounding beyond the historical illustrations developed in the Organizational Metamerism section. The two papers occupy different positions in the theory-empirics spectrum and are best understood as providing complementary foundations for a research program on the organizational consequences of coordination technology change.

Puranam, Alexy, and Reitzig (2014) ask a related question from yet another angle: when do "new" forms of organizing constitute genuine novelty versus functional equivalence to established forms? The metamerism framework operationalizes this question precisely — genuine novelty appears as non-membership in the metamerism set for a specified observer. Configurations that are new in their structural design but produce identical outputs for the observer in question are metamers, not novel forms. The Puranam et al. framework motivates the question; the metamerism framework provides the formal criterion. A systematic review of the organization design literature by Joseph and Sengul (2024) identifies coordination — alongside configuration, control, and channelization — as one of the four primary research streams in the field; the metamerism framework's focus on the progressive embedding of coordination logic in process specifications represents a direct theoretical extension of this coordination research stream into the AI-era organizational context.

## Organizational Metamerism

### The Temporal Stability Hierarchy

The organizational metamerism concept rests on a prior structural claim about the organization of value creation. We propose that the activities that constitute an organization's productive work are ordered into three levels by their characteristic temporal stability.[^sixtier]

[^sixtier]: The three-layer hierarchy here is the coarse projection appropriate for state-equivalence analysis. A finer six-tier decomposition for acquisition-target transferability analysis — Owner Intent, Business Model, Business Entity, Product, Process, Organization — is developed in Zharnikov (2026ag).

**Definition 1 (Value Layer)**. The value layer consists of the outputs that a productive activity is intended to deliver, defined from the perspective of the evaluative observer who assesses them. Value is what the observer is seeking from the productive activity: safe, reliable transportation; functioning dispute resolution; effective software infrastructure; nourishing food. The value specification defines what constitutes adequate output without prescribing how that output is to be produced.

A necessary qualification is required. The value specification is not exogenous or objectively fixed; it is shaped by institutional logics (Thornton, Ocasio, and Lounsbury 2012) and changes as cultural and technological contexts shift. What the present theory claims is not that value is immutable, but that the rate of change of value specification is slower — by orders of magnitude — than the rate of change of the processes that produce value-compliant outputs or the configurations that execute those processes. The claim is about relative rates of change, not about ontological permanence.

**Definition 2 (Process Layer)**. The process layer consists of the sequences of transformations that convert available inputs into value-layer outputs. Processes are specifiable: they can be described, taught, documented, and transmitted to agents who did not develop them. A process specification identifies the inputs, the sequence of transformations, the acceptance criteria at each step, and the final output standard. The process layer is more stable than the organizational layer because processes are embedded in physical reality, causal mechanisms, and learnable skills that do not change in response to coordination cost reductions.

**Definition 3 (Organizational Layer)**. The organizational layer consists of the configurations of agents, roles, authority relationships, resource allocations, and coordination procedures assembled to execute a given set of processes. Configurations specify who does what with which resources under which authority; they do not contain the process logic itself. The organizational layer is less stable than the process layer because configurations are primarily contingent on the coordination requirements that the process imposes — and coordination requirements are sensitive to the technology available for coordination.

**Proposition 1 (Temporal Stability Hierarchy)** — formalized in the Propositions section with confirming and refuting evidence criteria. Across observable historical timescales and across diverse industries, value outputs change less frequently than the processes that produce them, and processes change less frequently than the organizational configurations that execute them.

Historical evidence for the hierarchy is substantial. The value of reliable long-distance communication has persisted for millennia; the processes of communication shifted from courier systems to optical telegraphy to electrical telegraphy to digital packet switching, each transition spanning decades; the organizational forms — imperial messenger corps, postal guilds, telegraph companies, telecom carriers, internet service providers — have restructured on timescales of years within each process era. The value of freshly prepared food delivered to order has persisted as a human preference; the processes (cooking, sourcing, thermal management) have evolved with technology over decades; but the organizational forms for food delivery have changed dramatically within the last five years alone. The pattern holds across manufacturing, finance, education, and healthcare.

The hierarchy has a structural explanation that does not reduce to contingency. Value goals are anchored in relatively invariant human needs, social arrangements, and institutional logics that change slowly. Processes are contingent on physical and cognitive technology — harder to change than human preferences but faster to change than the needs they serve. Organizational configurations are contingent on coordination technology, which has historically been among the fastest-changing technological domains. The hierarchy is not a description of management preferences; it is a consequence of the differential sensitivity of each layer to different types of technological change.

This stability ordering has been identified implicitly in several adjacent research programs. In organizational theory, Tsoukas and Chia (2002) treat change as ontologically primary and stability as an achieved accomplishment, but their process ontology does not derive a stability ordering across levels of organizational abstraction. The information-processing theory of organizations (March and Simon 1958; Galbraith 1974) models structure as a coordination response to task uncertainty — which implicitly places configuration below process in terms of what is being designed around, but does not formalize the differential stability claim. In process management, the BPM literature (Hammer and Champy 1993; Davenport 1993) implicitly treats processes as more stable than organizational configurations, since process reengineering requires identifying stable value delivery goals before redesigning the configurations that execute them. In the strategy literature, Teece, Pisano, and Shuen (1997) characterize competitive advantage as residing in capabilities — which approximate the process layer — rather than in organizational structure. None of these sources derives the ordering as a formal proposition or derives its AI-era consequences.

The temporal stability hierarchy has a cross-domain structural parallel. Stewart Brand (1994, 1999) identified a nested hierarchy of systems evolving at different timescales, from the geological to the cultural to the commercial to the fashionable. Geologists (Hutton 1788; Lyell 1830) recognized that landform, soil, and surface features evolve at different rates. Evolutionary biologists (Gould 1989; Valentine 2004) identified differential stability between the body plan, the species, and the individual phenotype. The organizational hierarchy proposed here is the specific business-systems instance of a regularity that appears across multiple scales of analysis: nested layers, each more stable than those above and more volatile than those below, with the slow layers providing substrate for the volatility of the fast layers.

**Corollary 1**. Organizational reconfigurations that preserve process integrity will produce no detectable change in value output. Reconfigurations that disrupt processes — by dropping coordination responsibilities, creating ambiguity in role assignments, or breaking information flows — will produce value loss regardless of how elegantly the new organizational configuration is designed.

This corollary is a derived empirical implication of Proposition 1 that requires independent testing; it is taken up in the Discussion section. Its practical significance: the primary failure mode of organizational restructuring is not resistance (Hannan and Freeman 1984), culture clash (Cartwright and Cooper 1993), or leadership failure (Beer and Nohria 2000), but lossy migration — the inadvertent omission of coordination responsibilities in the translation from old configuration to new. The corollary transforms the change management question from a sociological one (how do we get people to accept change?) to a structural one (how do we ensure the migration is lossless?).

### Organization as Metadata: The Explanatory Mechanism

The temporal stability hierarchy explains the ranking of the three layers; the metadata metaphor explains why the ranking takes the specific form it does. We propose that organizational form stands in the same relationship to process that a configuration file stands to an application: it specifies how processes are instantiated and coordinated without containing the productive logic itself.

In software engineering, metadata is data about data — a configuration layer that describes how information is structured, accessed, and processed. A database schema specifies the tables, columns, relationships, and constraints that organize a data store; the schema can be revised — tables restructured, columns renamed, relationships reconfigured — without changing the underlying data, provided the revision correctly maps all existing data to the new structure. A container configuration file specifies the environment in which an application executes; multiple different container configurations can run the same application, and the application's behavior is preserved as long as the interface specifications are satisfied.

We propose an analogous relationship:

**Definition 4 (Organizational Form as Metadata)**. Organizational form is the configuration layer for a set of processes. It specifies how process steps are instantiated (which agents execute which activities), how they are coordinated (what information flows, in what sequence, under what authority), and how they are resourced (what tools and inputs are allocated to what activities). Organizational form does not contain the process; it describes how the process is to be executed in a particular configuration.

The metadata framing has three implications that distinguish it from existing characterizations. First, the configuration can be revised without changing what the process produces, provided the revision correctly maps all process responsibilities to the new configuration. This reframes organizational change as schema migration — a technical operation with correctness criteria — rather than as strategic reconfiguration or cultural transformation, though those dimensions may also be present.

Second, the same process can, in principle, be executed by multiple different organizational configurations that all satisfy the process's coordination requirements. This is the metamerism claim, developed formally below.

Third, the organizational layer is appropriate for AI automation in a specific and bounded sense: AI automates metadata entries (coordination rules, routing decisions, scheduling algorithms, monitoring procedures) rather than value or process. When an AI system assumes a coordination function, the relevant entry in the organizational configuration is updated; the process and its value target are unchanged. This reframes "AI replaces jobs" as "AI updates metadata entries."

The metadata metaphor connects to Pentland and Feldman's (2005) theoretical vocabulary in a precise way. Their ostensive aspect of organizational routines — the abstract, generalized pattern of the routine as participants understand it — is the metadata for the performative enactment. The organizational configuration specifies, ostensively, how coordination will work; the actual coordination events are performative instantiations of that specification. The metadata framing generalizes their insight from the routine level to the organizational level: the whole organizational configuration is the ostensive specification for all the coordination activities the organization performs, and this specification is more abstract, more stable, and more revision-tolerant than the performative enactments it coordinates.

The metadata framing is also consistent with the organizational specification framework developed in Zharnikov (2026i), in which the organizational form is treated as a structured description of how processes are to be instantiated — a schema that can be revised, versioned, and migrated.

A potential objection from process ontology (Tsoukas and Chia 2002) deserves direct engagement. If organizations are ongoing processes of becoming rather than stable configurations, then characterizing organizational form as metadata — a schema, a configuration layer — risks lapsing into entity-based thinking. The objection is well taken as a caution, but it does not undermine the metadata framing. The metadata characterization is not a claim that organizational form is a fixed artifact; it is a claim about the relationship between two processual layers. The process by which coordination arrangements are enacted and reproduced (the meta-process, in Tsoukas and Chia's terms) is ontologically secondary to and more volatile than the value-creating processes it coordinates. In Pentland and Feldman's (2005) terminology, the organizational form is the ostensive specification that coordination enactments reproduce and modify; it is alive, continuously re-enacted, and subject to drift — but it is still a specification, and it is still more volatile than what it coordinates.

### Organizational Metamerism: Definition and Observer-Relativity

We can now state the paper's central concept formally.

In color science, metamerism names the condition under which two physically distinct spectral power distributions produce an identical color percept under a given illuminant for a particular observer (Wyszecki and Stiles 1982). The equivalence depends on three elements: the two spectral distributions (the stimuli), the cone sensitivities of the observer (the evaluation function), and the illuminant (the observation conditions). Change any element and the equivalence may break. The phenomenon of observer metamerism — where different observers with different cone sensitivities disagree about whether two stimuli are equivalent — and metamer mismatch under illuminant change — where stimuli metameric under one light source diverge under another — provide the structural model for how cross-class observer disagreement about organizational equivalence arises (Foster 2011). The same spectral difference that is invisible to a standard observer may be visible to an observer with different cone sensitivities.

The color science metamerism concept has been extended to brand perception as a precise formalization of how distinct signal portfolios can produce identical evaluations in a given observer cohort, with the equivalence holding observer-relative and breaking when the observer's dimensional sensitivities shift (Zharnikov 2026e). The organizational analogue preserves the same tripartite structure:

**Definition 5 (Organizational Metamerism)**. Given an evaluative observer O whose evaluation function f_O is applied consistently across configurations, two organizational configurations A and B are metamers with respect to a given process P if and only if: (i) A and B are structurally distinct configurations both executing P, and (ii) O detects no difference between the value outputs produced by A and those produced by B. The metamerism relation is process-relative and observer-relative: configurations that are metamers for process P and observer O need not be metamers for a different process or a different observer.

The **metamerism set** of a process P under observer O, denoted M(P, O), is the collection of all organizational configurations that are pairwise metamers with respect to P and O. A large metamerism set indicates that O is insensitive to the organizational differences that distinguish the members; a small metamerism set indicates that O's evaluation function is sensitive to organizational form in ways that affect perceived output.

Several features of this definition deserve attention.

First, the equivalence is a state equivalence, not a path equivalence. Both configurations A and B are simultaneously executing P and simultaneously producing value-equivalent outputs. This is what distinguishes organizational metamerism from equifinality. Equifinality says: A and B arrived at equivalent performance via different paths. Metamerism says: A and B are simultaneously equivalent for observer O regardless of how they arrived at their current configurations. The path story is irrelevant to the metamerism claim.

Second, the observer-relativity is not a weakness of the concept but its most productive feature. It generates empirically testable predictions about which classes of observers will disagree about organizational equivalence. A procurement algorithm that evaluates suppliers solely on output quality and price will have a broader metamerism set than a socially embedded institutional buyer who evaluates suppliers on legitimacy, provenance, and organizational identity. An AI system that can only observe process outputs will have a broader metamerism set than a human regulator who can inspect organizational structure. The metamerism framework thus connects organizational theory to the observer-side literatures in institutional theory (DiMaggio and Powell 1983; Meyer and Rowan 1977) in a way that equifinality does not.

Third, the process-relativity of the metamerism set means that the same two configurations may be metamers for some processes but not others. A family-owned manufacturer and a private-equity-backed firm may be metamers for a highly codified manufacturing process (the differences in their coordination arrangements do not affect product quality) but non-metamers for a tacit-knowledge-intensive innovation process (the differences in their relational coordination and incentive structures do affect the quality of new product development). This is a prediction that equifinality cannot generate, because it does not tie the equivalence claim to the specific process being executed.

The relationship to Pentland and Feldman (2005) is now transparent. Their ostensive aspect is observer-relative: different participants and observers construct different ostensive understandings of the same routine. Metamerism extends this observer-relativity from the ostensive understanding to the evaluative output: different observers not only understand routines differently, they evaluate the outputs of different routines differently. Two organizations that appear to be running different routines (from the perspective of an internal observer) may appear to be running the same routine (from the perspective of an output-evaluating external observer). The metamerism concept names this condition and characterizes when it holds.

A note on the relationship to sensemaking (Weick 1995): both frameworks invoke observer-relative interpretation, but they address different phenomena. Sensemaking's observer-relativity concerns meaning construction — how actors retrospectively impose interpretive frames on equivocal experience. The organizational metamerism framework's observer-relativity concerns evaluation function sensitivity — how observers differ in whether they can detect value output differences across configurations. These are orthogonal: the paper concerns evaluation functions applied to outputs, not interpretation frames applied to experience. A configuration can be unambiguously meaningful in Weick's sense yet still be metameric with a structurally different configuration in the metamerism sense.

### Antecedents of Metamerism: What Determines the Set's Size

The metamerism set M(P, O) is a derived quantity, not an empirical fact to be catalogued. Its size depends on properties of the process P, properties of the observer O, and the coordination cost regime. We develop these dependencies in turn.

*Process codifiability*. A process that is fully codified — all steps explicitly specified, all decision rules articulated, all coordination responsibilities formally assigned — can be executed by any configuration that correctly implements the codification. Different configurations are metamers because the process specification is the invariant; the configuration is just a way of assigning agents to steps. A process that is tacit — dependent on embodied knowledge, relational trust, or contextual judgment that cannot be made explicit (Polanyi 1966) — ties value output to specific agents and their relationships, shrinking the metamerism set. Grant's (1996) knowledge-based view of the firm predicts the specific shape of this constraint: the irreducible core of organizational form is the integration of tacit knowledge, and no configuration substitution can preserve this integration unless the tacit knowledge itself is portable across configurations.

Process codifiability and tacit knowledge intensity are conceptually orthogonal: a process can carry both codified structure and tacit improvisational components (e.g., a jazz performance has codified harmonic structure and tacit improvisational knowledge). The metamerism set widens with codifiability and narrows with tacit knowledge intensity, with the two effects entering Proposition 2 separately.

The size of the metamerism set is a derived function of these three antecedents: process codifiability, the prevailing coordination cost regime, and the tacit knowledge intensity of the underlying process. Schematically:

|M(P, O)| = f(codifiability(P), 1/c(P), 1/τ(P))

where c(P) is the coordination cost per coordinating relation in process P and τ(P) is the proportion of P's coordinating work that depends on uncodifiable knowledge. The set grows with codifiability and with falling coordination cost; it shrinks with rising tacit knowledge intensity. The expression is schematic — operationalization of each antecedent is a research program, not a closed-form claim — but it makes the predicted directional dependencies precise enough for empirical testing.

Table 2: Metamerism Set Size as a Function of Process Codifiability and Coordination Cost Regime.

|  | **High coordination cost** | **Low coordination cost** |
|---|---|---|
| **High codifiability** | Moderate set: codified process supports substitution, but expensive coordination limits viable alternatives | Wide set: coordination embedded in process; configurations differ only in organizational overlay |
| **Low codifiability** | Narrow set: tacit knowledge ties output to specific agents and coordination arrangements | Moderate set: falling coordination costs expand substitutability, but tacit knowledge remains a binding constraint |

*Notes*: Set size is a theoretical characterization of the number of configurations in M(P, O) for a technically oriented, output-evaluating observer. Tacit knowledge intensity is treated as the inverse of codifiability here; processes combining high codifiability with residual tacit components (e.g., professional services with documented methodology) occupy intermediate cells.

*Observer evaluation sensitivity*. An observer whose evaluation function is sensitive only to output quality on measurable dimensions will have a wider metamerism set than an observer whose evaluation function is sensitive to organizational form itself (its legitimacy signals, its identity markers, its social affiliations). The observer's inability to directly observe process quality makes organizational form a signal of unobservable process attributes (Spence 1973), narrowing the metamerism set for observers who must infer process quality from structural signals rather than evaluating outputs directly. Signaling theory (Connelly, Certo, Ireland, and Reutzel 2011) characterizes the conditions under which such structural signals are credible and costly — conditions that determine when observer sensitivity to form, rather than output, becomes the binding constraint on the metamerism set. The institutional theory literature (Meyer and Rowan 1977; DiMaggio and Powell 1983; Thornton, Ocasio, and Lounsbury 2012) documents extensively the conditions under which organizational form is evaluated as a signal independently of process output. Observers embedded in institutional fields with strong norms about appropriate organizational form will have narrower metamerism sets than technically oriented observers who evaluate primarily on output.

*Coordination cost regime*. This is the dependency most directly connected to the temporal stability hierarchy and the metadata mechanism. Transaction cost economics (Williamson 1985) characterizes governance choice as a function of asset specificity and transaction frequency — but treats organizational form as the dependent variable responding to coordination costs, not as a metadata layer that can vary freely once coordination is embedded in the process. The metamerism framework extends this logic: when coordination is expensive — when assembling the right agents, communicating effectively, monitoring progress, and resolving exceptions requires dense human attention — the specific coordination arrangement matters. Two organizations executing the same process may produce different outputs because their coordination arrangements differ in quality. The quality of coordination is itself an output variable, and differences in organizational form that translate to differences in coordination quality will register as differences in value output.

When coordination costs fall — when technology embeds coordination in the process itself, as in pull production systems; or in digital interfaces, as in API-first architectures; or in AI agents, as in fully automated coordination — the coordination quality difference between organizational configurations shrinks. Configurations that would have been non-metamers under high coordination costs become metamers as coordination costs fall, because the coordination arrangement is no longer a significant source of variance in value output. The metamerism set expands monotonically with the extent to which coordination has been embedded in the process rather than imposed by the organizational overlay. This compression mechanism has become particularly salient in the current AI deployment cycle: Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb (2018) identify the fundamental contribution of AI as reducing the cost of prediction — a framing that subsumes many coordination functions, since coordination under uncertainty is inherently a prediction problem. Choudary (2026) extends this insight, arguing that AI's primary organizational payoff is not task automation but coordination compression — AI's capacity to extract structure from fragmented inputs and continuously reconcile them across teams and tools, reducing the translation costs that previously made coordination a bottleneck on viable organizational form.

This mechanism can be seen in historical sequence. The Toyota Production System (Ohno 1988) embedded coordination in the physical flow of materials: the kanban card is simultaneously a material artifact and a coordination signal, and its presence in the flow eliminates the need for a production scheduler to make the same decision. Once coordination is embedded in the physical process, different organizational forms that both implement pull production are metamers with respect to the scheduling coordination function — the configuration's scheduling arrangement is irrelevant, because the process itself handles scheduling. API-first architectures (Malone, Yates, and Benjamin 1987 anticipated the logic; Baldwin and Clark's 2000 design-rules framework established modularity as the general principle; modern platform architectures have realized it) embed coordination in digital interface contracts: a subservice that exposes a well-specified API is coordinated by the API, not by the organizational overlay. Different organizational forms that both implement the same API contracts are metamers with respect to the interfacing coordination functions. AI-mediated coordination generalizes this compression to the residual coordination that neither physical pull systems nor digital interfaces can absorb: exception resolution, cross-domain judgment, and dynamic resource reallocation. Each step in this trajectory expands the metamerism set by removing a category of coordination from the organizational configuration and embedding it in the process.

The mechanism by which coordination costs determine the size of the metamerism set can be stated in terms of the structure of coordination networks.

Coordination in a human organization scales with the number of interdependencies among roles. Galbraith (1974) modeled organizational design as an information-processing problem: the organizational structure routes information so that decisions can be made where relevant information resides. Simon's (1962) architecture of complexity establishes the deeper principle: in hierarchically organized systems, the number of interactions that must be managed grows with decomposability, and near-decomposable hierarchies dominate because they suppress cross-subsystem coordination to a manageable level. The number of potential coordination interactions in a fully connected organization of n roles is O(n²), and hierarchies exist to compress this to a manageable tree structure — at the cost of information loss and decision delay at each hierarchical junction. The human hierarchy is, in this framing, a compression algorithm for an otherwise intractable coordination problem.

The key observation is that this compression algorithm — the hierarchy — is part of the organizational configuration, not part of the process. Different configurations solve the same coordination problem differently (different hierarchy designs, different committee structures, different escalation rules) and introduce different patterns of information loss. These information-loss differences translate to output differences that make the two configurations non-metamers.

As coordination technology changes this picture, it does so by moving the coordination logic from the organizational configuration into the process specification. A pull production system's kanban rules are part of the process specification; a digital platform's API contracts are part of the process specification; an AI agent's coordination logic is part of the process specification. In each case, the coordination problem that previously required a configuration-specific organizational solution now has a process-level solution that is invariant across configurations. The specification impossibility result in Zharnikov (2026h) adds a complementary structural observation: in high-dimensional organizational specification spaces, exhaustive characterization of all possible configurations is geometrically intractable. The implication for the metamerism framework is that any empirically observed metamerism set is a sample from a larger theoretical set; the theoretically derived determinants of set size — process codifiability, observer sensitivity, and coordination cost regime — are therefore the appropriate targets for empirical research.

## Propositions

The paper's four propositions are stated in testable form, specifying for each the evidence that would confirm or refute the claim.

**Proposition 1 (Temporal Stability Hierarchy)**. Value outputs change less frequently than the processes that produce them, and processes change less frequently than the organizational configurations that execute them. This holds across industries, technological eras, and national contexts. Illustratively, Toyota's commitment to defect-free, customer-pull vehicle production has persisted across decades of process redesign and multiple organizational restructurings without the value specification itself changing.

*Confirming evidence*: Historical analysis showing that, across a sample of industries and time periods, the mean time between material changes in value specification exceeds the mean time between material changes in production processes, which in turn exceeds the mean time between material changes in organizational form (headcount, reporting structure, role definition). The pattern should hold at the industry level; individual firm exceptions are consistent with the proposition.

*Falsification*: A systematic finding that organizational form is more stable than production process across industries and time periods. Or a theoretically motivated class of organizations (for example, ideologically driven organizations where value redefinition is frequent) in which the stability ordering inverts.

*Operationalization*: longitudinal panel data tracking the change frequency of value-output specifications, process specifications, and organizational configurations across multi-year windows; the predicted ordering should hold within firms holding industry constant.

**Proposition 2 (Metamerism Set Expansion)**. The metamerism set M(P, O) for a given process P and evaluative observer O grows as the proportion of P's coordination logic embedded in the process specification increases, and shrinks as the tacit knowledge intensity of P increases. Process codifiability and tacit knowledge intensity enter the proposition as independent variables: their effects on set size are additive and separable. Illustratively, API-first software firms — where coordination is encoded in interface contracts — exhibit broader metamerism sets than legal practices, where relational tacit knowledge binds output quality to specific attorney-client relationships.

This claim does not contradict the contingency-fit record (Donaldson 1996): SARFIT documents the narrow-fit, high-coordination-cost regime in which misfit imposes performance penalties, while the metamerism framework predicts how the equivalence set expands when coordination costs fall — precisely the AI-era condition under analysis. The two frameworks operate at different points on the coordination-cost dimension rather than generating contradictory predictions.

*Confirming evidence*: Controlled comparison of firms executing comparable processes under different organizational forms, in industries with different degrees of process codification and coordination automation, showing no significant difference in value output quality among configurations in high-codification industries (the metamerism set is wide) but significant differences in high-tacit-knowledge industries (the metamerism set is narrow). Longitudinal evidence showing that the variance in organizational form across firms executing similar processes is higher in industries that have adopted AI coordination tools than in industries that have not.

*Falsification*: Evidence that value output quality is systematically associated with specific organizational forms across processes regardless of codification level. Or evidence that process codification and coordination automation do not predict increased variance in viable organizational forms.

*Operationalization*: cross-sectional fsQCA on matched-process firms, with codifiability and tacit knowledge intensity coded from process documentation and expert ratings, and the metamerism set proxied by the cluster of structurally distinct firms producing observer-equivalent outputs.

**Proposition 3 (Coordination Cost and Organizational Volatility)**. As AI reduces the marginal cost of coordination, the rate of change of organizational form increases while the rate of change of process remains stable. The ratio of organizational change frequency to process change frequency increases with the extent of AI coordination adoption. Illustratively, the wave of organizational restructuring at AI-early-adopter technology firms since 2022 — involving repeated headcount reductions and reporting-line changes without corresponding changes in core software delivery processes — exemplifies the decoupling this proposition predicts. The organizational change literature characterizes change as episodic and concentrated in punctuated equilibria (Romanelli and Tushman 1994); the metamerism framework predicts that as the cost of organizational reconfiguration falls, the equilibrium periods shorten and restructuring becomes continuous rather than episodic — a prediction that extends and partially revises the punctuated equilibrium model for the AI-coordination-cost regime.

*Confirming evidence*: Time-series evidence showing that the frequency of organizational restructuring events (layoffs, reorganizations, M&A, spin-offs, role-definition changes) has increased in AI-early-adopter industries since the widespread adoption of AI coordination tools, while the frequency of production process changes has not increased proportionally. Cross-sectional evidence showing that firms with higher AI adoption rates exhibit higher organizational restructuring frequency, controlling for firm size, industry, and environmental dynamism. Klein and Wieczorek's (2026) analysis of "headless firm" architectures — in which AI agents absorb coordination roles that previously required a human management layer — provides a contemporary organizational form that this prediction encompasses.[^klein]

[^klein]: Klein and Wieczorek (2026) is an arXiv working paper; empirical claims should be treated as provisional pending peer review.

*Falsification*: Evidence that AI adoption is associated with equal increases in both organizational restructuring frequency and process change frequency. Or evidence that organizational restructuring frequency has not increased in AI-adopting firms, suggesting that AI is not functioning as a coordination cost reducer in practice.

*Operationalization*: panel of firms varying in AI adoption, measuring the year-over-year frequency of organizational restructuring (cap-table changes, reporting-line revisions, role redefinitions) against the frequency of process change (workflow redesigns, SOP revisions); the prediction is restructuring frequency rises faster than process change frequency under AI adoption.

**Proposition 4 (Tacit Knowledge Constraint)**. The expansion of the metamerism set M(P, O) as coordination costs fall is bounded by the tacit knowledge intensity of P. Firms in high-tacit-knowledge industries will show smaller reductions in organizational coordination overhead than firms in high-codification industries, even at equivalent levels of AI coordination adoption. Illustratively, management consulting firms — whose differentiation depends on relational knowledge that cannot be reduced to process specifications — maintain organizational depth and partnership structures despite high AI adoption, while logistics firms at equivalent adoption levels reduce hierarchical layers.

*Confirming evidence*: Cross-industry comparison showing that the correlation between AI adoption and organizational form variance is significantly stronger in industries with highly codifiable processes (logistics, manufacturing, data processing) than in industries with highly tacit processes (creative services, strategic consulting, scientific research). Case study evidence showing that AI-adopting firms in high-tacit domains maintain organizational depth while AI-adopting firms in codifiable domains reduce it.

*Falsification*: Evidence that AI adoption expands the metamerism set at equivalent rates across industries regardless of tacit knowledge intensity. Or evidence that LLMs and other AI tools are progressively codifying previously tacit coordination processes at a rate that eliminates the tacit boundary as a meaningful constraint.

*Operationalization*: matched-pair comparison of firms with similar coordination embedding but high vs. low tacit knowledge intensity (e.g., AI-coordinated logistics platforms vs. AI-assisted boutique law firms); the prediction is metamerism set size remains narrower for high-tacit firms regardless of coordination embedding.

## Discussion

### Theoretical Implications

The organizational metamerism concept contributes to organizational theory in three specific ways.

*Reframing equifinality*. The paper does not contest the empirical finding that multiple organizational configurations can achieve equivalent performance. It reframes the finding: equifinality is a path-equivalence claim about historical trajectories; metamerism is a state-equivalence claim about simultaneous structural alternatives. Researchers interested in organizational diversity should distinguish between these two phenomena, because they have different causal structures and different empirical signatures. Path equifinality is evidenced by heterogeneity in organizational histories leading to similar current states. State equivalence (metamerism) is evidenced by heterogeneity in current organizational configurations producing equivalent current outputs.

*Extending the routines program*. Pentland and Feldman (2005) established observer-relativity at the routine level. The present paper extends this observation to the organizational level and generates a structural prediction: the metamerism set is wider for observers who can only evaluate process outputs (customers, algorithmic buyers, performance management systems) and narrower for observers who evaluate organizational form directly (institutional actors, regulators, legitimacy-granting bodies). This prediction opens an empirical research program at the intersection of routine theory, institutional theory, and organizational design.

*Reframing institutional decoupling*. The metadata framing reframes Meyer and Rowan's (1977) decoupling positively: where decoupling describes form-process divergence as a tension to be managed for legitimacy, organizational metamerism describes the same condition as structural flexibility for technically oriented observers. Institutional embeddedness operates on legitimacy-evaluating observers — narrowing the metamerism set for those observer classes — without contradicting the technical-observer claim. The organizational metamerism and institutional frameworks thus complement rather than contradict each other: each characterizes a different observer class and its attendant equivalence set.

*Grounding organizational change theory*. If organizational form is the metadata layer for processes, and if metadata revisions are only as disruptive as their lossiness, then the central question in organizational change theory is not how to manage resistance or align culture, but how to ensure that the migration from one configuration to another preserves all process responsibilities. This reformulation is not less sociological than the standard account — people's resistance to change is real and consequential — but it identifies a technical condition for successful reorganization that is prior to and logically independent of the sociological conditions. An organization can successfully manage cultural resistance to a restructuring while still losing value output, if the new configuration drops a coordination responsibility. The metadata view predicts that many reorganizations that "succeed" in sociological terms — adoption, acceptance, reduced resistance — fail in output terms, and vice versa.

### Methodological Implications

The metamerism framework generates a specific methodological recommendation for organizational researchers: operationalize the observer before measuring equivalence. Studies of equifinality typically measure organizational effectiveness or financial performance as the outcome variable without specifying whose evaluation function is being applied. Metamerism analysis requires specifying: which class of evaluative observer, evaluating which process output, under which observation conditions? Different specifications will produce different metamerism sets, and the variation across observer types is itself theoretically informative.

For example, a study of organizational form diversity in the restaurant industry should distinguish: Are the diverse organizational forms (sole proprietorship, chain franchise, platform-coordinated dark kitchen, worker cooperative) equivalent for the customer who evaluates food quality and service? For the investor who evaluates financial returns? For the food safety regulator who evaluates compliance procedures? These are different observers with different evaluation functions, and the theory predicts they will disagree about which configurations are equivalent.

Zharnikov (2026e) formalizes this point in the brand perception context, showing that metamerism in brand evaluation is a function of the observer's perceptual weights across evaluation dimensions. The organizational analogue is that configurations are metamers not in the abstract but relative to specific evaluation functions. This is a constraint on research design — the observer must be specified — but it is also a source of theoretical richness, because the variation in metamerism sets across observer types is itself a variable that the theory predicts and that empirical research can measure.

The specification impossibility result in Zharnikov (2026h) adds a complementary methodological implication: in high-dimensional organizational specification spaces, exhaustive characterization of the metamerism set is geometrically intractable. Researchers should expect the empirically observable metamerism set to be a sample from a larger theoretical set, and should design studies accordingly — focusing on the theoretically predicted determinants of set size rather than on cataloguing equivalences.

### Practical Implications

For practitioners, the organizational metamerism framework produces two practical insights.

*Restructuring as schema migration*. If organizational form is the configuration layer for processes, then restructuring is a schema migration — analogous to a database schema upgrade. Schema migrations fail not because change is inherently disruptive but because migrations can be lossy: responsibilities assigned in the old configuration are ambiguated or dropped in the new one. The practical prescription is to treat every restructuring as a technical operation with correctness criteria: before changing reporting lines, map all existing coordination responsibilities; after designing the new configuration, verify that every responsibility has a clear owner in the new structure; monitor for coordination gaps in the transition period. This shifts the primary management task from culture management to process responsibility accounting.

*Configuration choice as observer management*. The observer-relativity of the metamerism set means that selecting an organizational configuration is simultaneously a value-production decision and an observer-management decision. When multiple configurations are metamers with respect to process output (production equivalents), the differentiating criterion is how different classes of evaluative observers respond to the configurations' visible characteristics. An organization choosing between a worker cooperative structure and a corporate subsidiary structure for an e-commerce operation may face a wide metamerism set in production terms — both can execute the same fulfillment process equivalently — but a narrow set in institutional terms, because workers, labor advocates, and certain customer cohorts will evaluate the configurations differently. The practical task is to identify which observer classes are consequential for the organization's competitive environment and to select configurations that produce favorable evaluations from those observers without sacrificing production equivalence.

---

### Limitations

The theory assumes that value output can be measured and compared across organizational configurations — an assumption satisfied for outputs with clear quality metrics (software reliability, food safety scores, on-time rates) but problematic for multi-dimensional, contested, or highly observer-relative outputs. The theory is also focused on economic organizations in competitive environments; its extension to non-profit organizations, public agencies, and identity-bound organizations (Selznick 1957; King, Felin, and Whetten 2010) requires additional analysis, as these organizations face selection pressures where the relationship between coordination cost changes and organizational form volatility may be mediated by regulatory, political, and legitimacy factors. Tacit knowledge intensity is the primary boundary condition on the metadata thesis: the metamerism set expansion that follows from coordination cost reduction is bounded in any process where relational and embodied knowledge cannot be codified, and the location of that boundary is itself an empirical question that AI capability trajectories will continue to shift.

Finally, this paper does not attempt an empirical demonstration of the propositions it derives. Organization Science's tradition of theoretical contributions — represented by Tsoukas and Chia (2002), Pentland and Feldman (2005), and Michel (2014) — establishes that precisely stated theoretical contributions with testable propositions constitute independent scholarly contributions. The propositions derived here are intended as a starting point for empirical research programs, not as established empirical facts.

### Future Research

Three empirical strategies follow naturally from the framework. First, a configurational analysis using fsQCA (Fiss 2011) on matched samples of high- and low-codifiability processes — comparing, for example, software firms operating under API-first architectures with consulting practices retaining high relational tacit knowledge — would test Proposition 2's prediction that codifiability widens the metamerism set while tacit intensity narrows it. Second, longitudinal network analyses tracking the embedding of coordination work into process specifications — the migration from authority relationships to interface contracts and AI-mediated orchestration — would test Proposition 3's prediction that AI-era organizations exhibit decoupled organizational restructuring frequency from process change frequency. Third, experimental designs that systematically vary the evaluative observer's sensitivity profile across configurations — an observer-evaluation-function manipulation — would test the observer-relativity claim directly, by measuring whether evaluators with different sensitivity profiles disagree about which configurations are metameric. Each design targets a different antecedent in the schematic expression for set size; together they would calibrate the framework against organizational data. Zharnikov (2026ah) develops one downstream application of the metamerism framework: brand portfolios as multiple Tier-4 instances riding on shared Tier-3/5/6 substrate, where the cross-brand metamerism set is bounded by tier-level separability — a natural extension from the intra-firm equivalence studied here to the multi-brand, multi-unit case.
