# Dual Hierarchies of Organizational Transferability: A Six-Tier Ontology and Theory of Acquisition Failure Propagation

*Dmitry Zharnikov*

DOI: [10.5281/zenodo.19895813](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19895813)

Working Paper v1.0.0 -- April 2026

---

## Abstract

Mergers and acquisitions continue to destroy value at high rates because scholars and practitioners lack a shared ontology specifying which organizational layers are transferred and how failures propagate across them. This paper develops a six-tier ontology of the acquisition target — Owner Intent, Business Model, Business Entity, Product, Process, and Organization — each characterized by a distinct governor, specification surface, and transferability mode. The tiers form dual oppositely directed hierarchies: a service hierarchy running upward from Organization to Intent and a constraint hierarchy running downward from Intent to Organization. The constraint hierarchy determines integration sequencing; shocks propagate bidirectionally, generating seven falsifiable propositions about failure cascades. The framework is form-invariant across for-profit, NGO, and cooperative entities via explicit substitution rules, with Tier 2, 4, and 5 substitutions provisional. The ontology connects previously siloed streams — cultural distance, executive turnover, business-model misfit, and integration capabilities — under a single transferability logic, explaining residual variance documented in meta-analyses (King et al. 2004, 2021). The primary contribution is a generalizable theory of organizational separability under ownership change, with implications for theory of the firm and dynamic capabilities. A Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic is derived as a theoretical implication; propositions carry confirming and falsifying criteria.

**Keywords**: mergers and acquisitions; organizational ontology; transferability; business model; integration failure; form-invariance; separability diagnostic

---

Twenty years after King, Dalton, Daily, and Covin (2004) documented that strategic, financial, and organizational antecedents explain only a modest fraction of post-acquisition performance variance, subsequent meta-analyses confirm the same pattern: failure remains modal and integrative theory scarce (King et al. 2021; Paumen et al. 2024). The field has generated sophisticated single-layer explanations — cultural distance (Stahl and Voigt 2008), executive turnover (Bilgili et al. 2017), strategic misfit, and business-model misalignment (Christensen et al. 2011) — yet lacks a vocabulary for how these layers relate, constrain one another, or propagate failure. A cultural scholar and a strategy scholar examining the same deal are effectively analyzing different organizational tiers without a shared map of their interdependence. This ontological deficit is not merely semantic: it leaves the mechanisms by which a shock at one level reverberates across the entity underspecified, undermining both predictive accuracy and prescriptive guidance.

Cartwright and Schoenberg (2006) observe, reviewing thirty years of M&A research, that the field's explanatory frameworks remain discipline-internal — human resource scholars examine organizational resistance, strategy scholars examine strategic fit, legal scholars examine entity structure — with no integrative framework connecting these layers into a transferability theory. Angwin et al. (2023) call explicitly for theoretical rejuvenation of the field, identifying conceptual innovation and theoretical cross-fertilisation across domains as two of five priority directions. Haleblian et al. (2009), in a systematic stock-taking of M&A research, document that the field has accumulated domain-specific findings across strategy, finance, and human resources without theoretical integration of the layers those domains represent. Graebner et al. (2017) explicitly call for cross-level process models connecting strategic, sociocultural, and operational dimensions of integration. The present paper answers these calls by providing a unified cross-tier transferability ontology with formal propagation mechanisms.

This paper makes three primary theoretical contributions. First, it supplies the missing integrative ontology — a six-tier framework in which Owner Intent, Business Model, Business Entity, Product, Process, and Organization each carry a distinct governor, specification surface, and transferability mode. Bauer et al. (2024) and Bauer and Friesl (2024) establish that integration capabilities must span organizational domains; Strobl, Bauer, Shepherd, Lou, Tarba, and Dao (2025) document how contradictory HR signals propagate through the acquiring organization — but none of these contributions specify which tier generates which constraint, or how a misspecification at one tier propagates to tiers above and below it. The six-tier ontology supplies that specification. Second, it formalizes cross-tier propagation via dual hierarchies, extending Simon's (1962) near-decomposability and Penrose's (1959) productive services into a predictive failure theory. Third, it derives seven falsifiable propositions with explicit confirming and falsifying criteria, converting an empirical anomaly into a theoretical program. The practical implication — a Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic (STSD) for pre-close risk profiling — is presented strictly as a derivative of the theory, not its primary objective.

To our knowledge, this is the first framework in the M&A literature that formalizes cross-tier propagation as a set of falsifiable propositions organized around a dual-hierarchy mechanism, providing the theoretical architecture that King et al. (2004) and King et al. (2021) document has been missing for two decades. By reframing ownership transfer as hierarchical perturbation, the model offers a generalizable contribution to organizational theory: separability is never costless because constraint architectures are sticky.

The paper proceeds as follows. The next section establishes theoretical foundations, positioning the ontology against existing enterprise-architecture frameworks, addressing the ontological-mixing concern, reviewing the M&A failure literature, and situating the paper relative to recent dynamic-capabilities work. The third section presents the full six-tier framework. The fourth section develops the dual hierarchy and derives seven failure-propagation propositions. The fifth section demonstrates form-invariance across organizational types, develops the flip case, and derives integration implications including network and platform boundary conditions. The sixth section presents the STSD and a compact future-research agenda. The seventh section discusses limitations and contribution.

## Theoretical Foundations

### *Layered Ontologies of the Firm*

Prior layered frameworks of the firm provide partial precedents but lack the specific structure this paper requires. Zachman's (1987) 6×6 enterprise-architecture matrix organizes stakeholder perspectives against interrogatives but carries no M&A framing, no transferability axis, and collapses Owner Intent and Business Model into a single Owner row — the very distinction this paper argues is diagnostic for acquisition outcomes. Osterwalder's (2004) Business Model Ontology formalizes Tier 2 with precision but represents Tiers 4–6 only as undifferentiated "Key Activities" and "Key Resources" and contains no Tier 1, no Tier 3, and no transferability axis. The Business Architecture Body of Knowledge (Business Architecture Guild 2024) excludes Process and omits Tiers 1 and 3. Dietz's (2006) DEMO ontology addresses organizational design via a different axis with no M&A application. To our knowledge, no existing framework combines Owner Intent as a distinct tier above the Business Model, Business Entity as a distinct legal tier, per-tier transferability modes, dual service and constraint hierarchies, and an M&A-specific application.

The six-tier ontology unifies objects of heterogeneous ontological type — a latent psychological state (Tier 1), an abstract projection (Tier 2), a legal artifact (Tier 3), and operational realities (Tiers 4–6) — under a single instrumental axis: each tier is an object that must be specified, transferred, and integrated when one entity acquires another. The question is not "are they the same kind of thing?" but "what happens to each kind of thing when it crosses a new-owner boundary?" The unifying axis is transferability mode. Coase (1937) and Williamson (1985) ground Tier 3 as the boundary object of the firm; this paper extends that move by identifying the five other objects a buyer also acquires (or fails to acquire) when the entity changes hands. The dual hierarchy developed below extends Simon's (1962) near-decomposability principle — that complex systems retain stability through hierarchical modularity — by adding tier names, governors, transferability modes, and an M&A-specific diagnostic that makes near-decomposability actionable for pre-close risk assessment.

### *The M&A Failure Literature and Its Missing Layer*

Haspeslagh and Jemison (1991) established the foundational post-merger integration distinction between capability transfer and administrative integration; the present paper extends it by specifying tier-level transferability modes. The scale of the failure problem is substantial: Cronqvist and Pély (2026) report that 46% of S&P 500 M&A deals are eventually unwound; Paumen et al. (2024) confirm failure as the modal outcome across the peer-reviewed empirical literature; Lev and Gu (2024) distill 43 deal-level failure predictors to a 10-factor model — yet none of those factors is tier-level. Two systematic stock-takings frame the gap. Haleblian et al. (2009) document that M&A research has accumulated domain-specific findings across strategy, finance, and human resources without theoretical integration of the layers those domains represent. Graebner et al. (2017) explicitly call for cross-level process models connecting strategic, sociocultural, and operational dimensions of integration. The dual-hierarchy framework answers both calls by specifying which tier constrains which and at what sequencing.

The dominant failure explanations are each anchored in a single organizational layer. Cultural distance — Stahl and Voigt (2005, 2008), Brede et al. (2025) — addresses Tier 6 in isolation, treating culture as a property of the people layer with no specification of how cultural disruption propagates to Tier 4 Product or Tier 2 Model. Executive turnover (Bilgili et al. 2017) similarly addresses Tier 6 without asking which tier the departing executive's tacit knowledge was encoding. Strategic misfit (King et al. 2004) is a Tier 2 diagnosis that leaves transmission to Tiers 4–6 unspecified. Business-model misalignment (Christensen et al. 2011) addresses the Tier 2–Tier 4 boundary but not the upstream Tier 1 Intent constraint or the downstream Tier 5 Process implications. The common structure of these explanations is the feature that makes them collectively inadequate: each diagnoses a single layer and cannot explain how failure propagates to adjacent tiers. King et al. (2004, 2021) explicitly call for the integrative theory two decades of meta-analyses have not produced. Klok et al. (2023) and Smeulders et al. (2023) provide adjacent multi-layer evidence — emotional dynamics distributed across levels and integration controls bridging Tiers 5–6 — without supplying the propagation logic.

A parallel recent stream — Bauer et al. (2024) on cross-domain integration capabilities, Bauer and Friesl (2024) on attentional crowding-out during integration, and Strobl et al. (2025) on contradictory HR signals — establishes the *phenomenology* of integration difficulty: capabilities must span domains, attention is scarce, signals are contradictory. What this stream does not supply is the ontological vocabulary for specifying *which* tier carries which difficulty, which tier is causally prior to which, and how a failure at one tier propagates to the next. The present paper supplies that vocabulary, positioning itself as the ontological complement to capabilities research.

## The Six-Tier Ontology

The six-tier ontology specifies each tier of an acquisition target by its governor — the agent or force that determines the tier's configuration — its specification surface — the set of observable artifacts that describe the tier's state — and its transferability mode — the mechanism by which the tier does or does not cross an ownership boundary. Table 1 presents the full structure.

**Table 1: The Six-Tier Ontology of an Acquisition Target.**

| Tier | Name | Governor | Specification Surface | Transferability Mode |
|------|------|----------|-----------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | Owner Intent | Owner psyche / board / charter | Stated purpose + time horizon + exit criteria | Imprint on lower tiers only; no direct transfer |
| 2 | Business Model | Market reality + owner choice | Value proposition + customer segments + channels + unit economics | Conceptually replicable; not legally owned |
| 3 | Business Entity | Law / regulator / tax authority | Charter + cap table + contracts + audited financials | By document (equity or asset transfer) |
| 4 | Product | Customer / beneficiary acceptance | Acceptance criteria + tolerance bands + dimensional specification | If written; tacit if not |
| 5 | Process | Management | SOPs + workflow maps + ERP configuration + supply-chain architecture | If codified; tacit if not |
| 6 | Organization | People and culture | Roles + employment contracts + tacit knowledge + relational networks | Partial only (retention contracts at best) |

*Notes*: Tiers are ordered by position in the constraint hierarchy (Tier 1 highest, Tier 6 lowest). SBT = Spectral Brand Theory (Zharnikov 2026a); the SBT eight-dimension framework is one example of a precision specification instrument for Tier 4. The service hierarchy runs in the opposite direction — upward from Tier 6 to Tier 1. The "Service Hierarchy and Constraint Hierarchy" subsection develops both hierarchies formally.

### *Tier 1 — Owner Intent*

Owner Intent is the psychic and strategic commitment of the controlling principal — the answer to "why does this exist, and when would it be rational to stop?" It is topmost in the constraint hierarchy because it constrains every downstream choice. Its specification surface is stated purpose, time horizon, and exit criteria, observable in founding documents, investor communications, and interview data. Theoretically, Schumpeter (1934) grounds the entrepreneurial function as domain commitment, not financial optimization; Jensen and Meckling (1976) supply the principal-agent framing in which Intent is the principal's objective function; Selznick's (1957) "infusion with value" supplies the structural precursor to the flip case below; Marquis and Tilcsik (2013) establish imprinting as the propagation mechanism.

*Vignette:* A sixty-two-year-old regional manufacturer has operated in the same trade for forty years. Their Intent, probed across several conversations, produces no sentence that survives without the company's name. Tier 1 is Fused.

### *Tier 2 — Business Model*

The Business Model is the mechanism by which value flows from the market into organizational resources — value proposition, customer segments, channels, unit economics, cost structure, revenue model. Its governor is market reality interacting with owner choice. Its transferability mode distinguishes it from every other tier: a Business Model is conceptually replicable — a competitor can copy it without acquiring the entity — but it is not legally owned and does not transfer in a share deal. Teece (2010) formalizes business models as the commercialization side of dynamic capabilities; Osterwalder (2004) supplies the current specification standard. The critical distinction from Tier 1 is direction: the Business Model is the chosen answer to the market; the Owner Intent is the purpose for which that answer was chosen.

*Vignette:* A private equity sponsor manages a portfolio of three business-services companies under a shared back-office model, written as an investment thesis with explicit CAC/LTV targets and a defined exit timeline. Tier 2 is Independent: the model could be replicated by a successor management team without the sponsor's participation.

### *Tier 3 — Business Entity*

The Business Entity is the legal and regulatory construct that holds rights, obligations, and property — what actually transfers in a share deal. Its specification surface is the charter, cap table, debt structure, counterparty contracts, and audited financials. Coase (1937) grounds the firm boundary economically; Williamson (1985) extends this to vertical-integration governance. The critical analytical point is that a clean Tier 3 is necessary but not sufficient: financial due diligence is designed to examine Tier 3 — and Tier 3 only. A financially healthy Entity can hold a business whose Tier 4 Product is tacit, whose Tier 1 Intent is Fused to the founder, and whose Tier 6 will fracture within twelve months of close.

*Vignette:* A clean LLC with a three-year audit history passes financial due diligence without exception. What the data room cannot show is that the product specification lives entirely in the founder's head. The Entity is Independent; Tier 4 is Fused. The gap is the value the buyer will discover missing on day sixty.

### *Tier 4 — Product*

The Product is the specific outcome a customer or beneficiary is paying for, governed by customer acceptance. Its specification surface includes acceptance criteria, tolerance bands, and dimensional specifications. Its transferability mode is conditional: a written specification transfers; a tacit one does not. Penrose (1959) grounds productive resources as the core from which competitive advantage is derived; the Product is the external expression of that bundle. Zharnikov (2026a) provides one example of a precision specification instrument for brand-anchored Products; Zharnikov (2026ah) develops the brand-portfolio implications of identifying brand with Tier 4. The critical distinction from Tier 5 is that Tier 4 defines what is produced, while Tier 5 defines how: Process documentation anchored to an unspecified Product is coordination machinery without a destination.

*Vignette:* A SaaS company with $40M ARR has its Product codified in API contracts, SLA documents, and a roadmap with acceptance criteria. A third party could reconstruct the testing regime without the founding team. Tier 4 is Independent — the acquisition commands a premium multiple precisely because this independence removes the most common source of post-close value degradation.

### *Tier 5 — Process*

The Process is the coordinated activity that produces the Tier 4 Product, governed by management. Its specification surface covers standard operating procedures, workflow maps, ERP configuration, and supply-chain architecture. Its transferability mode parallels Tier 4: codified Process transfers; tacit Process does not. Mintzberg (1979) provides the foundational coordination-mechanism taxonomy; Pentland and Feldman (2005) distinguish the ostensive aspect of a routine (its abstract, codifiable pattern) from its performative enactment, with only the former transferring across owners. Porter's (1985) value chain decomposes Tier 5 into primary and support activities. Puranam, Singh, and Chaudhuri (2009) establish that structural integration is necessary when tasks are highly interdependent and common ground is low, but destroys value when imposed on loosely coupled processes — empirical grounding for the STSD's Partial classification as a tractable middle ground. Felker et al. (2024) find that routinization and codification of integration processes improve M&A outcomes when strategic intent is stable but not otherwise — direct support for the constraint-hierarchy sequencing principle that Tier 1 clarity must precede Tier 5 codification. Zollo and Singh (2004) demonstrate that deliberate learning through codification generates superior post-acquisition performance, establishing Tier 5 codification as an active transferability investment rather than mere documentation.

*Vignette:* A holding company imposed a shared back-office Process over eighteen months. Twelve months in, the three operating teams continue to run legacy workflows in parallel with the mandated shared systems. Tier 5 is Partial: the buyer inherits a gap requiring active harmonization before any cross-sell synergy thesis can execute.

### *Tier 6 — Organization*

The Organization is the set of people, roles, cultural norms, tacit knowledge, and relational networks that execute the Tier 5 Process. Its transferability mode is partial by structural necessity: employment contracts transfer as legal obligations but not as motivational commitments; culture cannot be acquired by document; tacit knowledge exits with the individual who holds it. Hannan and Freeman (1984) establish Tier 6 as the site of highest institutional resistance to change. Polanyi (1966) identifies the specific component retention contracts cannot capture: the judgment and relational trust encoded in individuals through years of practice. Grant (1996) establishes this irreplaceable tacit knowledge as the source of the firm's most defensible competitive advantage. Du and Tsolmon (2026) show that what transfers via people is not merely skill but positional knowledge of where information flows — the component the constraint hierarchy cannot reconstruct once it exits. Stahl and Voigt (2008) and Strobl et al. (2025) supply Tier 6 as the most thoroughly studied tier in the M&A literature and, paradoxically, the least connected to the upstream configurations that produced its current state.

*Vignette:* A regional NGO has fourteen paid staff plus a volunteer base managed by a program coordinator. The board simultaneously occupies Tier 1 (charter custodian) and Tier 6 (organizational actor) — a dual role that is the structural source of nonprofit governance complexity. Tier 6 is Partial: paid-staff successors are partially identified, but volunteer coordination and board succession are underdeveloped.

## The Dual Hierarchy and Seven Propositions

### *Service Hierarchy and Constraint Hierarchy*

The six tiers form two overlapping hierarchies with opposite directionality. The *service hierarchy* runs upward: Organization serves Process; Process serves Product; Product funds Entity; Entity implements Model; Model realizes Intent. Each tier contributes instrumentally to the tier above it, in Penrose's (1959) sense that the value of organizational resources is not in their stock but in the services they render. The *constraint hierarchy* runs downward: Intent constrains the admissible set of Business Models (a Mission Founder cannot adopt a Model that treats program outcomes as negotiable); Model constrains admissible Entity forms (a cooperative Model cannot inhabit a C-corp structure without contradiction); Entity constrains admissible Products (a regulated pharmaceutical Entity cannot deliver Products outside its license); Product constrains Process architecture (zero-defect acceptance requires defect-detecting Process); Process constrains required Organization (24-hour service Process requires shift coverage). Williamson (1985) grounds governance-as-constraint propagating downward; Thompson (1967) supplies the organizational-theory anchor in his pooled, sequential, and reciprocal interdependence types — directional flows that constrain feasible coordination structures, formalized here as the dual hierarchy.

Service and constraint are the same relationships described from opposite directions. A shock at any tier propagates in both: a Tier 6 fracture reduces the productive capacity Tier 5 requires, which transmits through the service chain to Tier 4 drift even when the Product specification was never altered (the structural basis for P6). The integration sequencing implication is direct: because upper tiers constrain lower tiers, specification clarity at upper tiers is logically prior to integration execution at lower tiers. A buyer who reorganizes Tier 6 before resolving Tier 1 compatibility is redesigning a team without knowing what the team is supposed to produce for whom. Bauer et al. (2024) establish empirically that integration capabilities must span multiple domains — consistent with the dual-hierarchy implication that integration is a cross-tier problem.

### *Failure-Propagation Propositions*

The following seven propositions formalize the failure-propagation pathways implied by the dual hierarchy. Each is presented with statement, mechanism, and confirming/falsifying criteria. P1–P5 and P7 are theoretical — derived from the dual-hierarchy structure and consistent with adjacent single-layer findings, but not yet traced end-to-end in a single empirical study. P6 is partially documented across Stahl and Voigt (2008) and Bilgili et al. (2017) but not as an end-to-end chain. The contribution is the theoretical architecture; empirical validation is the agenda described in "Validation and Future Research Agenda."

Each proposition takes the canonical conditional form: *if antecedent A holds in target/acquirer dyad D, then consequent C is observed in D over horizon H, conditional on mechanism M operating.* Mechanism M for every proposition is the constraint hierarchy described above: each tier rules out inadmissible configurations for the tier below it, so a misalignment at tier *n* forces revision at tier *n+1*, which propagates by the same logic.

**P1 — Intent–Model Misalignment.** When an acquirer's Owner Intent differs structurally from the target's (e.g., financial-return-primary buyer of a mission-primary target), the target's Business Model will require fundamental revision, generating downstream friction at Tier 4 and Tier 5 not visible in pre-close due diligence. *Mechanism:* the new Tier 1 is incompatible with a Model designed to optimize impact rather than return on capital; Bauer and Friesl (2024) document the attentional crowding-out that deprives the revision process of bandwidth. *Confirming:* studies measuring Tier 1 configuration at close and tracing forced Model revisions to Tier 4 or Tier 5 disruption at 12–36 months, controlling for pre-close financials. *Falsifying:* a systematic comparison of for-profit acquisitions of mission-primary targets showing no greater rate of Model revision or downstream disruption than for-profit-to-for-profit acquisitions of equivalent financial profile. Christensen et al. (2011) frame deal failure in terms of business-model fit and warn that conflating absorption from leverage-as-platform causes mispricing — the AMR-tradition empirical anchor closest to this proposition.

**P2 — Business Model–Product Friction.** When an acquirer imposes a Business Model revision on the target, the Product specification will drift toward the new Model's optimization criteria, and customers whose purchase decision was anchored in the prior dimensional configuration will exhibit higher churn than pre-acquisition baselines. *Mechanism:* the Product optimized for a mission-primary Model is not the Product that maximizes margin in a return-primary Model; Product revision changes the dimensional configuration and reduces overlap between the Product the customer chose and the Product they now receive. *Confirming:* attribute-stability studies of acquired brands at close versus 12 and 36 months post-close, with churn disaggregated by customer segment. *Falsifying:* no systematic association between Model revision and product attribute drift across completed acquisitions with matched pre-/post-close data.

**P3 — Entity–Operations Disruption.** When Entity restructuring — subsidiary consolidation, re-domiciling, change of legal form — follows close, operational continuity at Tiers 5 and 4 will be disrupted by contract renegotiation, regulatory re-certification, and counterparty uncertainty disproportionate to the restructuring's legal scope. *Mechanism:* the Entity is the contract- and license-holding construct through which Products are delivered and Processes operated; restructuring it does not automatically assign counterparty relationships to the new vehicle, and Tier 3 constrains admissible Products via regulatory scope. *Confirming:* event studies comparing operational-continuity metrics for acquisitions with and without Entity restructuring in the 90 days post-close, controlling for deal size and industry. *Falsifying:* no significant association between Entity restructuring and operational-continuity disruption.

**P4 — Product–Process Disruption.** When an acquirer revises the target's Product specification, the Process architecture designed for the prior Product will exhibit misalignment, generating quality failures or cost overruns until the Process is rebuilt around the new specification. *Mechanism:* a zero-defect acceptance criterion requires defect-detecting Process; a high-throughput specification requires high-volume Process. Specification revision without simultaneous Process revision creates a mismatch the impossibility of fully specifying Product requirements (Zharnikov 2026h) compounds with tacit residuals documentation cannot eliminate. *Confirming:* case comparisons of acquisitions with and without documented Product-specification revisions, measuring quality-defect rates and cost variances at 6 and 18 months. *Falsifying:* no systematic association between Product revision and quality/cost variance, controlling for baseline Process maturity.

**P5 — Process–Organization Fracture.** When Process integration is imposed on the acquired Organization before Tier 4 specification clarity is established, employee resistance and attrition will exceed rates when Process integration follows Product confirmation. *Mechanism:* the staffing structure, role design, and decision allocation were built to execute a specific Process in service of a specific Product; imposing Process changes without establishing the new Product's acceptance criteria deprives the Organization of the purpose context in which those changes make sense. Klok et al. (2023) document the emotional dynamics underestimated in integration planning; Smeulders et al. (2023) supply integration-control evidence bridging Tier 5 to Tier 6 without tracing the full sequencing effect. *Confirming:* quasi-experimental comparisons of Product-first versus Process-first sequencing, measuring executive turnover and employee attrition at 12 and 36 months. *Falsifying:* no systematic difference in attrition rates between Product-first and Process-first sequencings in matched samples.

**P6 — Organization Fracture to All-Layer Degradation.** When key Organization members — executives, technical specialists, relationship holders — exit following acquisition, the tacit knowledge they carried (uncodified Product specification, Process exception-handling, customer relationships) will degrade across all six tiers at rates that retention bonuses or post-exit documentation cannot recover. *Mechanism:* Tier 6 is the sole repository of tacit specification knowledge never externalized to Tier 4 or Tier 5 surfaces; once the holder exits, the knowledge exits with them. The all-layer degradation follows from the service hierarchy: Organization serves Process, Process serves Product, Product funds Entity, Entity implements Model. Strobl et al. (2025) establish that contradictory HR signals accelerate exit; Bauer et al. (2024) show integration-capability deficits produce the conditions of severest tacit-knowledge loss. *Confirming:* longitudinal studies that (a) measure pre-close tacit-knowledge codification by tier, (b) track executive turnover at 6, 12, 36 months, and (c) measure tier-specific value preservation. *Falsifying:* acquisitions with high pre-close codification show the same all-layer degradation rate following executive turnover as acquisitions with low codification. Stahl and Voigt (2008) trace cultural differences through sociocultural integration to shareholder value; Bilgili et al. (2017) establish the executive-turnover-to-performance link meta-analytically — together making P6 the best-supported pathway, though no single study traces the full chain.

**P7 — Bidirectional Feedback.** Sustained lower-tier failure forces upward revision of upper tiers — specifically, sustained Tier 5 or Tier 6 fractures of duration ≥ 12 months and severity sufficient to depress reported Tier 3 revenue ≥ 10% from deal-thesis projection will trigger documented Tier 2 Business Model revision within 24 months and, conditional on Model revision being insufficient to restore the deal thesis, documented Tier 1 Intent revision (write-down, divestiture decision, or strategic-review announcement) within an additional 12 months. *Mechanism:* sustained Process failure reduces the Product quality the Model promised; sustained Product degradation erodes Entity revenue; sustained revenue erosion forces Model revision because the prior Model has lost the productive base it was specified against. The feedback loop amplifies because upper-tier revisions generate new lower-tier constraints via the constraint hierarchy, restarting the cascade. The feedback is asymmetric: upward revision is observed only when the lower-tier failure crosses the duration-and-severity threshold; below that threshold, lower-tier failures are absorbed within the original Model without forcing upper-tier revision. Bauer and Friesl (2024) document the sequential cost-anchor failures consistent with the mechanism. *Confirming:* panel data documenting sequential tier revisions in the 18–48 months post-close where lower-tier severity meets the threshold and exogenous market shocks are controlled. *Falsifying:* no systematic instances of lower-tier failures meeting the threshold precipitating Model or Intent revision across a sample with controlled exogenous shocks; or, observation that Model/Intent revisions occur as frequently below threshold as above, indicating that the upward revisions are not driven by the constraint cascade.

## Form-Invariance and Integration Implications

### *Substitution Rules Across Organizational Forms*

The six-tier structure does not require a for-profit assumption. Each tier has a structurally equivalent form across for-profit, NGO/charity, and mutual/cooperative organizational types. Table 2 presents the substitution matrix.

**Table 2: Form-Invariant Six-Tier Ontology Across Organizational Types.**

| Tier | For-Profit | NGO / Charity | Mutual / Cooperative |
|------|------------|---------------|----------------------|
| 1 Intent | Owner goal (return in domain X) | Charter mission + board fiduciary duty | Member benefit (collective intent) |
| 2 Model | Value capture: revenue, margin, unit economics | Theory of change + funding model (grants / fees / endowment) | Member-benefit model (cost-recovery + dividends to members) |
| 3 Entity | LLC, C-corp, GmbH | 501(c)(3), CIC, foundation, charity, association | Cooperative, mutual society, credit union |
| 4 Product | Customer-paid output | Beneficiary-received impact | Member-received service |
| 5 Process | Activity producing output | Activity producing impact | Activity producing member service |
| 6 Organization | Employees | Employees + volunteers + board | Member-employees + officers |

*Notes*: Tier 1 and Tier 3 substitutions are anchored in Hansmann (1996) for for-profit, NGO, and cooperative forms; Tier 1 and Tier 6 NGO/hybrid substitutions draw additionally on Battilana and Lee (2014). The Tier 2, Tier 4, and Tier 5 substitutions are theoretical deductions from the tier definitions adapted to each form's value logic — answers to the same defining question ("how does value flow to and from this entity?") under different governance regimes — and remain provisional pending the cross-form comparative design described in "Validation and Future Research Agenda." The cooperative Tier 1 entry is not a scalar individual intent but a collective mandate governable only through governance structure. Privatization (state-owned to for-profit) and other public-sector cross-form cases require an additional Tier 1 substitution (politically constituted authority) that this version of the table does not specify; Bruton et al. (2015) supply the closest precedent.

### *The Flip Case: Intent Equals Product*

In standard for-profit transactions, Tier 1 and Tier 4 are distinct: the owner's purpose is to capture return in a domain, and the Product is the specific mechanism through which that return is generated. In identity-bound cases — the Domain Craftsman, the Family Steward, the Mission Founder — Intent is not separable from Product. The founder does not have an Intent to which the business happens to be the answer; the business is the Intent's only possible expression. Structurally: Intent ≡ Product. The chain collapses to Intent ≡ Product ← (Model = funding wrapper) ← Entity (legal cover) ← Process ← Organization. Tiers below Intent remain separately transferable in limited forms — assets can be sold, IP can be licensed — but the separability of Intent from Product is destroyed. Selznick (1957) provides the closest prior formulation in "infusion with value"; Marquis and Tilcsik (2013) establish imprinting as the mechanism; Whetten (2006) grounds organizational identity as central, enduring, distinctive attributes; Pecis et al. (2025) trace family-business goal entanglement through rhetorical history; Berrone et al. (2012) establish the SEW consequence: identity-bound principals systematically overvalue the firm relative to its financial utility, producing a reservation price above rational valuation that makes the firm effectively unsellable to pure financial buyers. The categorical M&A consequence is that firms in the flipped chain are unsellable as going concerns to a buyer who does not share the founder's identity commitment.

### *Network and Platform Boundary Conditions*

Table 2 does not extend to network and platform businesses, which are a distinct boundary condition the current tier definitions do not fully accommodate. In a platform business, Tier 4 (Product) is a market interface generating value by connecting participants on multiple sides, with acceptance criteria held by two or more participant populations rather than a single customer. Tier 6 (Organization) extends beyond payroll to ecosystem participants — developers, third-party vendors, marketplace sellers — whose participation constitutes the platform's value, whose loyalty is not contractually held in standard employment terms, and whose exit following an acquisition may constitute Tier 6 fragmentation financial due diligence would not detect. Tier 2 sits structurally closer to Tier 4 than in unit-good businesses, because the multi-sided pricing architecture and cross-subsidization logic are not separable from the interface design itself. The full extension of the ontology to platform and network businesses — specifying governors, specification surfaces, and transferability modes under multi-sided conditions — is flagged here as a natural next step; the current propositions and diagnostic apply to platform businesses with modification, not without it.

### *The Integration Sequencing Principle*

The constraint hierarchy derives a sequencing principle for M&A integration that is formal rather than prescriptive: because upper tiers constrain the admissible configurations of lower tiers, specification clarity at upper tiers is logically prior to integration execution at lower tiers. The sequencing implication is a structural necessity — attempting to finalize lower-tier configurations before upper-tier specifications are resolved is building on a foundation that the constraint hierarchy has not yet established.

The formal sequence is: (1) resolve Tier 1 (Intent) compatibility before Tier 2 (Business Model) revision; (2) resolve Tier 2 revision before Tier 4 (Product) specification; (3) confirm Tier 4 specification before Tier 5 (Process) integration; (4) confirm Tier 5 architecture before Tier 6 (Organization) role redesign. Tier 3 (Entity) integration is legally required immediately post-close; the framework implies that Tier 3 integration should be designed to minimize disruption to Tiers 4 through 6 while upper-tier specification clarity is being established.

**Table 3: Integration Sequencing Derived from the Constraint Hierarchy.**

| Phase | Tier Priority | Address Before | Theoretical Basis |
|-------|---------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Pre-close | Tier 1 compatibility | Any Tier 2 revision | Constraint hierarchy; P1 |
| Pre-close or Day 1 | Tier 4 specification | Tier 5 integration | P4; Tier 4 tacit knowledge loss risk (P6) |
| Months 1–6 | Tier 2 revision (if required) | Tier 5 harmonization | P2; P4 chaining |
| Months 1–6 | Tier 5 codification | Tier 6 role redesign | P5; Tier 6 resistance without purpose context |
| Months 6–18 | Tier 6 transition | Founder departure | P6; tacit knowledge transfer requires time |

*Notes*: The sequencing principle is derived from the constraint hierarchy, not from empirical observation of integration practice. Deviations from this sequence — the modal failure mode in the literature (Larsson and Finkelstein 1999) — produce the cascade pathways formalized in P1–P7. Tier 3 integration is omitted from the sequencing table because it is legally mandated immediately post-close; the design imperative is to minimize its disruption to Tiers 4–6.

The modal failure mode in practice inverts this sequence: acquirers begin with Tier 6 (Organization) — immediately visible, urgent, and socially demanding — and Tier 5 (Process) — operationally critical in the first weeks — while Tiers 1 through 4 remain unresolved. Larsson and Finkelstein (1999) identify inadequate organizational integration as the dominant predictor of synergy failure in their case-survey methodology; in tier-level vocabulary, their finding maps to Tier 5 forced-merge producing Tier 4 drift — a P4 cascade — whose upstream origin in an unresolved Tier 1 compatibility question the authors do not observe because their unit of analysis is the organizational layer, not the full tier stack. Jemison and Sitkin (1986) provide the foundational AMR framing of this pathology as the "activity trap" — operational urgency crowding out the strategic-intent resolution that the constraint hierarchy requires. Van Oorschot et al. (2023) find that post-M&A reorganization requires simultaneous integration in some sub-elements (product harmonization) and differentiation in others (structural autonomy), a cross-tier complexity that the dual-hierarchy framework accommodates naturally: the constraint hierarchy specifies which sub-elements must converge (Tiers 1–2) while the service hierarchy identifies which sub-elements can retain productive differentiation (Tiers 5–6). Bauer et al. (2024) establish that integration-capability deficits at any domain reduce synergy realization across the full deal, consistent with the dual-hierarchy framework's claim that integration is a cross-tier problem rather than a within-tier problem.

### *The Separability-as-Screening Principle*

The Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic (STSD) — described fully in "The Six-Question Diagnostic" — produces a Fused/Partial/Independent profile for each of the six tiers before close, enabling acquirers to price risk by tier rather than by aggregate financial performance.

A profile with Fused at Tier 1 signals the flip case (see "The Flip Case: Intent Equals Product"): no post-close integration sequence can resolve this condition because the flip case is a structural feature of the target, not a remediable information asymmetry. A profile with Fused at Tier 4 signals that the Product specification lives in the founder's judgment and will degrade post-close regardless of retention efforts; pre-close codification is the only intervention that changes the Tier 4 separability state. A profile with Partial at Tier 5 signals process-maturity gaps that are tractable: codification can close the gap, but must be scoped explicitly as integration cost rather than assumed synergy. A profile with Partial at Tier 6 signals key-person dependency: retention structures should be conditioned on knowledge-transfer milestones, not on calendar milestones.

The framework's primary theoretical contribution to integration theory is the disaggregation of due-diligence risk by tier rather than production of an aggregate pass/fail verdict. Standard financial due diligence produces a Tier 3 verdict: the Entity is clean or it is not. The STSD produces a six-cell profile that maps risk to tier, allowing acquirers to design tier-specific integration interventions. Bauer and Friesl (2024) provide complementary support from an attention-based view: when integration attention is allocated by aggregate severity rather than by tier-specific risk, attentional crowding-out causes high-severity tiers to consume cognitive resources that causally upstream tiers require — the STSD profile makes this allocation problem explicit by surfacing which tiers carry the binding integration risk. Current due diligence examines Tier 3; the STSD examines all six.

### *The Form-Mismatch Principle*

Cross-form acquisitions — a for-profit acquirer targeting a cooperative or NGO — face Tier 1 misspecification risk that no standard due-diligence checklist captures, because standard financial due diligence examines Tier 3 and does not examine Tier 1 compatibility. The form-invariance table (Table 2) reveals the specific substitution that must be negotiated in cross-form deals: the acquiring entity's Tier 1 (Intent) must be compatible with the target's Tier 1, or the downward constraint chain will generate forced revisions at every lower tier.

The cooperative presents a distinctive structural challenge: Tier 1 Intent is collectively held and democratically governed. No single principal can unilaterally authorize a sale; the member assembly must consent. A credit union cannot be acquired by a bank without member consent not because of regulatory friction alone but because the Intent itself — the collective member-benefit mandate — cannot be transferred without being replaced by a fundamentally different Intent. The STSD applied to a cooperative requires a collective-governance version of the diagnostic in which the question "Can the owner state their Intent in one sentence that does not contain the company's name?" becomes "Can the member assembly articulate a purpose that survives the transaction without changing the economic logic of membership?" Battilana and Lee (2014) establish that hybrid organizations at the for-profit/NGO boundary face mission-drift risk when acquired by non-mission buyers — an empirical instantiation of the form-mismatch cascade that the present framework formalizes.

Privatization transactions are the most visible cross-form case and the most tractable natural experiment for empirical testing of the form-mismatch principle: the Tier 1 substitution is explicit, documented in government records, and associated with large-scale post-acquisition operational changes that can be retrospectively coded against the tier-level predictions.

## The Separability Diagnostic and Validation Agenda

### *The Six-Question Diagnostic*

The Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic (STSD) is best understood as a theoretical implication of the dual-hierarchy framework rather than its primary deliverable. If the ontology is valid — if each tier is governed by a distinct force, exposes a distinct transferability mode, and generates distinct downstream constraints — then pre-close tier profiling should predict post-acquisition trajectories better than aggregate financial or strategic fit measures. The STSD operationalizes this theoretical claim as a structured judgment instrument that produces a six-cell profile — one state per tier, three possible states: Fused, Partial, or Independent. Table 4 presents the full diagnostic. Pablo (1994) establishes that integration-level decisions are made prior to integration execution and depend on task, cultural, and political target characteristics — providing direct AMJ-tradition precedent for the STSD's pre-close-decision framing: the instrument is designed to produce a tier-disaggregated risk profile at the point in the acquisition process where integration-level choices are still open.

**Table 4: The Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic.**

| Tier | Diagnostic Question | Fused | Partial | Independent |
|------|---------------------|-------|---------|-------------|
| 1 Intent | Can the owner state Intent in one sentence not naming the company? | No — the company IS the answer | Named but tied to company | Yes — purpose survives without this entity |
| 2 Business Model | Is the model articulated as a written canvas independent of habit? | No — implicit, inarticulable | Some components explicit, others tacit | Yes — fully written; logic independently testable |
| 3 Business Entity | Are debts and obligations decoupled from the owner's balance sheet? | No — personal guarantees, commingled accounts | Partial decoupling; residual obligations remain | Yes — fully decoupled; clean separation |
| 4 Product | Is there a written WHAT-specification a third party could test against? | No — spec lives in founder's judgment | Partial codification; key criteria implicit | Yes — written spec; survives founder exit |
| 5 Process | Does the process run a continuous month without the owner present? | No — owner present for key decisions | Owner required for exceptions only | Yes — runs fully; owner presence optional |
| 6 Organization | Does every owner role have a named, trained successor? | No — owner occupies irreplaceable roles | Some successors named; key roles thin | Yes — all roles covered by named successors |

*Notes*: The STSD produces an ordinal state per tier (Fused / Partial / Independent), not a continuous score. Inter-rater reliability evidence is the first validation priority. Identity-bound owners (Domain Craftsman, Family Steward, Mission Founder) will always show Fused at Tier 1 — a structural state, not a remediable condition.

How to read the profile: a profile of Independent / Independent / Independent / Independent / Partial / Partial indicates a highly transferable business with integration risk concentrated in Tiers 5 and 6 — the SaaS company profile described in the Product tier vignette. A profile of Fused / Fused / Partial / Fused / Partial / Fused indicates a business that presents as a standard transaction from the financial-due-diligence seat but will not survive the transition intact — the regional manufacturer profile described in the Owner Intent vignette. The six-cell structure makes visible what aggregate financial performance measures cannot: the tier-level distribution of value at risk.

### *Validation and Future Research Agenda*

The STSD is currently a structured judgment instrument with no inter-rater reliability data and no published predictive-validity evidence. The roadmap to empirical validation has four priorities.

First, translate the six diagnostic questions into a semi-structured interview protocol with probes for each response type. Two independent assessors conduct the protocol on the same target without contact before scoring. Cohen's κ ≥ .70 is the target threshold per tier, consistent with Landis and Koch's (1977) standard for acceptable inter-rater agreement on ordinal clinical instruments. Second, test predictive validity: the primary hypothesis is that Fused-tier count at close will be negatively associated with acquisition performance at 36 months after controlling for deal size, industry, and financial leverage — the most tractable operationalization of the framework's core claim. Secondary hypotheses follow from individual propositions: Fused Tier 4 at close predicts higher executive churn in the first 18 months (P6 pathway); Fused Tier 1 with a for-profit acquirer predicts higher rate of Business Model revision within 24 months (P1 pathway). Third, validate the form-invariance substitution rules for Tiers 2, 4, and 5 through a cross-form comparative design: a minimum of ten completed acquisitions per organizational form (thirty total across for-profit, NGO, and cooperative), with privatization and other public-sector cross-form transactions held as a separate extension once the Tier 1 substitution for politically constituted authority is specified. Fourth, extend the framework to platform and network businesses: specify governors, specification surfaces, and transferability modes for each tier under multi-sided market conditions, then validate that the modified STSD produces reliable classifications for platform-business acquisitions.

### *Companion Computation Script*

A reference implementation of the STSD scoring step described above is published at the paper's public mirror under `six-tier-ontology/code/stsd_scorer.py`. The script encodes Table 4 exactly, takes a six-cell ordinal response, and returns the separability profile, Fused-tier count, flip-case flag, and constraint-hierarchy-ordered integration-priority ranking. It is deterministic and depends only on the Python standard library; the two example profiles described in the "How to read the profile" paragraph above can be reproduced via `python stsd_scorer.py --example`. The script computes the scoring step only and is not a substitute for structured-interview administration; it does not encode any of the four validation steps in the agenda above.

## Discussion

### *Theoretical Contribution*

The six-tier ontology and its dual-hierarchy architecture offer a theoretical explanation for why ownership transfer so frequently destroys value. By making explicit the governors, specification surfaces, transferability modes, and bidirectional propagation pathways that single-layer models leave implicit, the framework converts the "unidentified moderators" of King et al. (2004, 2021) into tier-contingent mechanisms. The primary contribution is not another antecedent variable but an architecture — a constraint-based view of the firm that complements capability, attention, and identity perspectives. Where dynamic-capabilities research has shown integration requires spanning domains (Bauer et al. 2024), the dual-hierarchy framework specifies which domains, in what order, and why misalignment at one necessarily perturbs others. The seven propositions formalize cross-tier failure predictions with explicit confirming and falsifying criteria — a theoretical move predicting cross-layer cascades rather than within-layer outcomes. Corley and Gioia (2011) argue that high-contribution theory advances both originality and utility; the six-tier ontology aims at both, grounding cross-tier cascade claims in an instrument (the STSD) whose predictive-validity program constitutes the utility dimension.

The dual-hierarchy construct has implications beyond M&A. The same hierarchical modularity that permits near-decomposability (Simon 1962) creates leverage points for cascading failure when ownership boundaries are crossed. In institutional theory, imprinting (Marquis and Tilcsik 2013) is the mechanism by which Tier 1 propagates downward during founding — a constraint cascade the framework makes formally tractable. In routine dynamics (Pentland and Feldman 2005), the ostensive-performative distinction maps onto the Tier 4–Tier 5 transferability boundary: ostensive specifications transfer; performative enactments do not. In hybrid organizing (Battilana and Lee 2014), Tier 1 substitution under cross-form acquisition generates the identity and mission-drift dynamics the dual hierarchy explains as a constraint cascade rather than a cultural friction. P7 is relevant to paradox theory: the upward-revision loop formalizes the simultaneity of stability and change demands paradox scholars describe.

### *Practical Implications*

The STSD translates the framework directly into due-diligence practice and yields tier-specific actions before letter-of-intent submission. A Fused Tier 1 signals the flip case and should trigger price renegotiation or deal exit, not integration planning. A Fused Tier 4 signals that pre-close codification of the Product specification is the single highest-return integration investment available, because that investment becomes unavailable on day one of ownership. A Partial Tier 5 signals tractable process-maturity gaps whose codification cost should be scoped as explicit integration cost rather than assumed synergy. The integration sequencing principle provides the actionable corollary: buyers who resolve Tier 1 compatibility before committing to Tier 2 Model retention, and who extract Tier 4 specifications before imposing Tier 5 changes, structure their integration around the constraint hierarchy rather than against it. The practical value is not only in predicting failure but in providing a sequenced decision architecture that directs attention to causally upstream tiers before the pre-close codification window closes.

### *Limitations*

**Cascade claim is theoretical.** P1–P5 and P7 have no end-to-end empirical support. They are derived from the dual hierarchy and consistent with adjacent single-layer findings, but not yet traced in a single integrated study. This is the appropriate framing for an AMR conceptual contribution: the framework provides a theoretical architecture whose value is in the testable predictions it generates.

**Form-invariance is provisional.** Hansmann (1996) anchors the Tier 1 and Tier 3 substitutions for for-profit, NGO, and cooperative forms; Battilana and Lee (2014) anchors the hybrid Tier 1 and Tier 6. The Tier 2, 4, and 5 substitutions are logical deductions from the tier definitions and require empirical validation in the cross-form design described above before they can be treated as established.

**Multi-principal Intent configurations.** The framework assumes a dominant principal whose Intent is characterizable as a relatively stable stance. When ownership is dispersed — public companies, VC-backed startups with multiple investor classes, sibling co-owners — Intent is a vector, not a scalar. The framework applies most cleanly to closely-held businesses; extension to publicly-traded targets and dispersed-ownership structures requires the multi-principal Intent adaptation and remains a future-research priority.

**Platform and network boundary conditions.** As detailed above, tier definitions require modification for two-sided markets and ecosystem orchestrators; the propositions apply to platforms with modification, not without it.

Nevertheless, by supplying a shared ontological vocabulary and a propagation logic, this theory reframes the M&A failure problem from an empirical puzzle into a predictable consequence of hierarchical constraint architecture. Ownership transfer necessarily perturbs the acquired firm's constraint hierarchy; the extent to which acquirers can specify and manage that perturbation before the pre-close codification window closes determines whether value is preserved or destroyed. Future work that tests, extends, or challenges this architecture will advance not only M&A scholarship but our understanding of how organizations cohere across ownership changes.
