## Abstract

Mergers and acquisitions continue to fail at high rates despite decades of research, with meta-analyses attributing the residual variance to unidentified moderators (King et al. 2004; Paumen et al. 2024). Extant explanations remain anchored at single organizational layers — cultural distance, executive turnover, strategic misfit, or business-model misalignment — leaving cross-layer propagation mechanisms underspecified. This paper develops a six-tier ontology of the acquisition target: Owner Intent, Business Model, Business Entity, Product, Process, and Organization. Each tier is specified by one governor, one specification surface, and one distinct transferability mode. The tiers form dual overlapping hierarchies — a service hierarchy running upward and a constraint hierarchy running downward — that jointly determine integration sequencing and generate seven falsifiable propositions on failure cascades. The framework applies across for-profit, NGO, cooperative, and state-owned forms through explicit substitution rules; the substitution mappings for Tiers 2, 4, and 5 are framed as provisional theoretical deductions awaiting empirical validation. The paper contributes (1) the first unified transferability ontology, (2) a formal derivation of cross-tier propagation pathways, and (3) a structured Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic for pre-close risk assessment. Propositions are distinguished from the partially documented empirical record throughout. The paper offers a new theoretical vocabulary and diagnostic instrument for M&A scholars and practitioners while opening a validation agenda centered on longitudinal tier-specific performance metrics.

---

M&A transactions fail at rates that have proved remarkably resistant to both improved deal screening and decades of scholarly attention. Meta-analyses consistently document post-acquisition performance below benchmarks — King, Dalton, Daily, and Covin (2004) analyzed 93 studies and concluded that strategic, financial, and organizational antecedents collectively explain only a small fraction of performance variance, with the residual attributed to "unidentified moderators." More than two decades later, Paumen, Kroon, and Khapova (2024) confirmed the pattern: failure remains the modal outcome, and integrative theoretical frameworks remain scarce. King, Wang, Samimi, and Cortés (2021), synthesizing a further generation of empirical work, found no improvement in aggregate predictive accuracy, documenting persistent performance underperformance even as data quality and methodological sophistication advanced.

The present paper argues that a structural source of this explanatory gap has been overlooked: the field has no shared vocabulary for specifying which tier of an acquisition target is being diagnosed. A buyer who determines that cultural distance is high (Stahl and Voigt 2008) and a buyer who determines that the business model is misaligned (Meirovich 2017) are diagnosing different organizational layers — but neither framework tells the analyst how those layers relate to each other, how a failure at one propagates to others, or which layers can transfer to a new owner and which cannot. The six-tier ontology proposed here provides that vocabulary.

The gap is not merely definitional. Cartwright and Schoenberg (2006) observe, reviewing thirty years of M&A research, that the field's explanatory frameworks remain discipline-internal — human resource scholars examine organizational resistance, strategy scholars examine strategic fit, legal scholars examine entity structure — with no integrative framework connecting these layers into a transferability theory. The field has accumulated insight about individual tiers without asking whether those tiers constrain one another, how a failure at one tier propagates to adjacent tiers, or what it would mean to specify the full acquisition target before close rather than after. This integration deficit is the gap the present paper addresses.

Recent work in the capabilities and processual traditions reinforces the urgency of cross-layer integration. Bauer, King, Friesl, Schriber, and Weng (2024) show that acquisition integration capabilities require organizational design choices that span multiple analytic domains; Bauer and Friesl (2024) document that attention allocation during integration is a scarce resource whose distribution across layers determines synergy realization. Yet the capability-based literature lacks the ontological vocabulary to specify which layers require which attention at which sequencing. This paper supplies that vocabulary.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it develops the six-tier ontology of the acquisition target — Owner Intent, Business Model, Business Entity, Product, Process, and Organization — each specified by one governor, one specification surface, and one distinct transferability mode. Second, it formalizes the dual service and constraint hierarchies that structure the tiers' relationships and derive integration sequencing implications from first principles. Third, it presents the Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic (STSD), a structured pre-close instrument that profiles each tier as Fused, Partial, or Independent, and a validation roadmap for this instrument.

The paper proceeds as follows. The next section establishes theoretical foundations, positioning the ontology against existing enterprise-architecture frameworks, grounding each tier in its intellectual lineage, and directly addressing the ontological-mixing concern that a referee might raise. The third section presents the full six-tier framework with Table 1. The fourth section develops the dual hierarchy and derives seven failure-propagation propositions. The fifth section demonstrates form-invariance across organizational types and develops the flip case for identity- and mission-bound principals. The sixth section derives implications for M&A integration theory. The seventh section presents the STSD and its validation roadmap. The eighth section discusses limitations and future research directions.

## Theoretical Foundations

### *Layered Ontologies of the Firm*

Prior frameworks have layered organizational phenomena without producing the specific structure the present paper requires. The closest structural precedent is Zachman's (1987) 6×6 matrix, which organizes enterprise architecture across six stakeholder perspectives (Planner, Owner, Designer, Builder, Subcontractor, Worker) against six interrogatives (What, How, Where, Who, When, Why). Zachman includes an "Owner's View" row and achieves a systemic cross-perspective representation that influenced a generation of enterprise-architecture practice. However, the Zachman framework carries no M&A framing, provides no transferability axis, and collapses Owner Intent and Business Model into the same Owner row — precisely the distinction the present paper argues is diagnostic for acquisition outcomes.

Osterwalder's (2004) Business Model Ontology formalizes Tier 2 of the present framework with considerable precision, decomposing the business model into nine building blocks (customer segments, value proposition, channels, customer relationships, revenue streams, key resources, key activities, key partnerships, cost structure). The BMO has achieved broad adoption as a specification standard for business models. Its limitation, from the present paper's perspective, is topological: it addresses Tier 2 almost exclusively, represents Tiers 4 through 6 only as undifferentiated "Key Activities" and "Key Resources," and contains no Tier 1 (Owner Intent), no Tier 3 (Business Entity), and no transferability axis. The nine-block model was designed to characterize business models, not acquisition targets.

The Business Architecture Body of Knowledge (Business Architecture Guild 2024), a practitioner framework with considerable deployment across large enterprises, explicitly excludes Process from its scope and focuses on value streams, business capabilities, and information mapping. BIZBOK omits both Tier 1 and Tier 3, providing no instrument for distinguishing the principal's psychic commitment from the legal entity that holds the assets. Dietz's (2006) DEMO ontology addresses organizational design through ontological, infological, and datalogical layers tied to transaction structures — a different axis from the six named tiers — and carries no M&A application.

The cumulative gap across these frameworks is precise: no existing framework combines Owner Intent as a distinct tier above the Business Model, Business Entity as a distinct legal tier, per-tier transferability modes, dual service and constraint hierarchies, and an M&A-specific application. The six-tier ontology fills this gap by combining elements from enterprise architecture (the notion of layered views of the firm), agency theory (the principal's Intent as the governing variable), transaction cost economics (the Entity as the boundary object), and the M&A integration literature (transferability as the organizing axis).

### *Tier Ontologies and the Unifying Axis of Transferability*

The six-tier ontology unifies objects of heterogeneous ontological type under a single framework. Tier 1 (Owner Intent) is a latent psychological and motivational state — not directly observable, inferred from stated purpose, revealed preference, and behavioral history. Tier 2 (Business Model) is an abstract projection — a theory of value creation and capture that exists as a conceptual object, replicable without legal title. Tier 3 (Business Entity) is a legal and regulatory artifact — a contract-holding, tax-filing, regulator-facing construct that transfers by document. Tiers 4 through 6 (Product, Process, Organization) are operational realities — things that exist in the world and produce outcomes for customers and beneficiaries.

A referee applying strict ontological homogeneity as a framework criterion would object that these four categories cannot be legitimately combined in a single tiered structure. The objection is structurally analogous to the objection that Zachman's (1987) framework cannot legitimately combine stakeholder perspectives (social ontology) with technical interrogatives (logical ontology) in a single 6×6 matrix. Zachman's framework did combine them, and it became a foundational instrument precisely because the combination revealed relationships that discipline-internal ontologies cannot capture.

The present framework rests on a different justification: not ontological homogeneity but shared instrumental relationship to the M&A specification act. All six tiers are objects that must be specified, transferred, and integrated when one entity acquires another. The question is not "are they the same kind of thing?" but "what happens to each kind of thing when it crosses a new-owner boundary?" The answer differs by tier, and the differences are the framework's theoretical contribution. The unifying axis is transferability mode — the mechanism by which each tier crosses, or fails to cross, the ownership boundary. Tier 1 (Owner Intent) does not transfer by any legal instrument; it transfers, if at all, as imprinted constraint on the tiers below it. Tier 3 (Business Entity) transfers by document. Tier 6 (Organization) transfers only partially, via retention contracts that convert prior commitments into new obligations without guaranteeing the motivational states that made those commitments productive.

This is a feature of the framework, not a defect. Frameworks that restrict to one ontological category — legal frameworks that see only Tier 3, brand-measurement frameworks that see only Tier 4, HR-integration frameworks that see only Tier 6 — are precisely the frameworks that produce single-layer diagnoses of multi-layer failures. The six-tier ontology explains what they miss by design. Coase (1937) and Williamson (1985) provide the economic foundation for Tier 3 as the boundary object of the firm — the governance structure that the transaction cost tradition predicts will emerge where market transactions are costly. The present paper extends that foundational move by identifying the five other objects that a buyer also acquires (or fails to acquire) when the entity changes hands.

### *The M&A Failure Literature and Its Missing Layer*

The M&A performance literature has identified four dominant failure explanations, each anchored in a single organizational layer. Cultural distance — the degree to which acquirer and target differ on dimensions of national or organizational culture — has accumulated the largest empirical base, with Stahl and Voigt (2005) providing an early integrative model of how cultural differences propagate through integration outcomes, Stahl and Voigt (2008) providing a meta-analytic examination across 46 studies (n = 10,710), and Brede, Gerstel, Wöhrmann, and Bausch (2025) extending that evidence using Glassdoor review data as a cultural-distance proxy. Both address Tier 6 (Organization) in isolation: culture is treated as a property of the people and cultural norms layer, with no specification of how cultural disruption propagates to Product specification (Tier 4) or Business Model viability (Tier 2). Executive turnover — studied by Bilgili, Calderon, Allen, and Kedia (2017) in a meta-analytic design — also addresses Tier 6 in isolation, treating executive exit as a post-acquisition performance predictor without asking which tier the departing executive's tacit knowledge was encoding. Strategic misfit — the finding by King et al. (2004) that strategic antecedents explain only a small fraction of variance — is a Tier 2 diagnosis that leaves the transmission mechanism to Tiers 4 through 6 unspecified. Business-model misalignment — identified by Meirovich (2017) as an underexamined culprit in M&A failure — addresses the Tier 2 to Tier 4 boundary but does not connect it to the constraint imposed by Tier 1 (Owner Intent) above or the execution implications for Tier 5 (Process) below.

The common structure of these explanations is the feature that makes them collectively inadequate: each diagnoses a single layer and cannot explain how failure at that layer propagates to adjacent tiers or to overall deal value. King et al. (2004) explicitly call for theory development to explain the residual variance — a call that King et al. (2021) document has not been answered at the integrative level two decades later. Klok, Kroon, and Khapova (2022), reviewing the emotional dimensions of M&A, note that integration outcomes are shaped by emotional processes distributed across multiple organizational levels, further implicating cross-layer dynamics that single-layer models cannot capture.

The present paper's theoretical gap claim is precise: no existing framework connects the layers into a transferability ontology that explains why misspecifying which tier is being acquired cascades into failure across all others. Smeulders, Dekker, and Van den Abbeele (2023) come closest in the empirical literature, showing that integration controls affect cultural-difference management and employee resistance — a finding that bridges Tier 5 (Process/integration management) to Tier 6 (Organization/resistance) — but their analysis remains anchored within the organizational layer rather than tracing the cascade to Tier 4 Product disruption or Tier 2 Model revision.

## The Six-Tier Ontology

The six-tier ontology specifies each tier of an acquisition target by its governor — the agent or force that determines the tier's configuration — its specification surface — the set of observable artifacts that describe the tier's state — and its transferability mode — the mechanism by which the tier does or does not cross an ownership boundary. Table 1 presents the full structure.

**Table 1: The Six-Tier Ontology of an Acquisition Target.**

| Tier | Name | Governor | Specification Surface | Transferability Mode |
|------|------|----------|-----------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | Owner Intent | Owner psyche / board / charter | Stated purpose + time horizon + exit criteria | Imprint on lower tiers only; no direct transfer |
| 2 | Business Model | Market reality + owner choice | Value proposition + cohorts + channels + unit economics | Conceptually replicable; not legally owned |
| 3 | Business Entity | Law / regulator / tax authority | Charter + cap table + contracts + audited financials | By document (equity or asset transfer) |
| 4 | Product | Customer / beneficiary acceptance | Acceptance criteria + tolerance bands + dimensional specification | If written; tacit if not |
| 5 | Process | Management | SOPs + workflow maps + ERP configuration + supply-chain architecture | If codified; tacit if not |
| 6 | Organization | People and culture | Roles + employment contracts + tacit knowledge + relational networks | Partial only (retention contracts at best) |

*Notes*: Tiers are ordered by position in the constraint hierarchy (Tier 1 highest, Tier 6 lowest). SBT = Spectral Brand Theory (Zharnikov 2026a); the SBT eight-dimension framework is one example of a precision specification instrument for Tier 4. The service hierarchy runs in the opposite direction — upward from Tier 6 to Tier 1. Section 4.1 develops both hierarchies formally.

### *Tier 1 — Owner Intent*

Owner Intent is the psychic and strategic commitment of the controlling principal — the answer to "why does this exist, and when would it be rational to stop?" It is the topmost tier in the constraint hierarchy because it constrains every downstream choice: the Business Model chosen, the Products authorized, the Entity structures permitted, the Processes built, and the culture shaped over time. Intent is governed by the owner's psyche, or in governance-structured organizations by the board mandate or charter mission. Its specification surface is the owner's stated purpose, time horizon, and exit criteria — observable, though imperfectly, in founding documents, investor communications, and interview data.

Schumpeter (1934) grounds the entrepreneurial function as an economy-transforming commitment to a domain, not merely an optimization problem over financial returns. Jensen and Meckling's (1976) agency theory provides the principal-agent framing: Owner Intent is the principal's objective function, and every lower tier is an agent executing in its service. Selznick (1957) adds the temporal dimension: institutions that survive long enough undergo "infusion with value," transitioning from expendable tools to entities whose existence is considered inherently valuable — the structural origin of the flip case developed in Section 5.2. Marquis and Tilcsik (2013) establish imprinting as the mechanism by which Intent propagates to lower tiers during the founding period, encoding the founder's commitments in organizational structure, hiring standards, and product choices long after the founding context has changed.

*Vignette:* A sixty-two-year-old regional manufacturer has operated in the same trade for forty years. When asked to describe the business, he describes the craft — "doing this work properly" — not a financial return objective. His Intent, probed across several conversations, produces no sentence that survives without the company's name. Tier 1 is Fused: the owner and the business are the same thing.

### *Tier 2 — Business Model*

The Business Model is the mechanism by which value flows from the market into organizational resources — a formal account of the value proposition, the observer cohorts served, the channels through which value is delivered, the unit economics governing each transaction, the cost structure, and the revenue model. Its governor is market reality in interaction with owner choice: market conditions constrain the set of viable models, while the owner selects among them in service of Tier 1 Intent. Its transferability mode distinguishes it from every other tier: the Business Model is conceptually replicable — a competitor can observe and copy it without acquiring the legal entity — but it is not legally owned and does not transfer in a share deal. What transfers is the accumulated track record of the model's operation, encoded in financial statements that are Tier 3 artifacts.

Teece (2010) formalizes business models as the commercialization side of dynamic capabilities, distinguishing the model (the theory of value creation and capture) from the strategy (the competitive positioning within a market). Osterwalder's (2004) nine-block ontology provides the current specification standard for Tier 2. The critical distinction from Tier 1 is that the Business Model is the chosen answer to the market; the Owner Intent is the purpose for which that answer was chosen. A Domain Craftsman and a Financial Sponsor may adopt structurally identical Business Models — same unit economics, same channel structure, same value proposition — while holding opposite Tier 1 Intent configurations. The downstream implications are radically different: the Domain Craftsman's Model cannot be revised to optimize return on capital without violating the Tier 1 constraint; the Financial Sponsor's Model was designed for precisely that revision.

*Vignette:* A private equity sponsor manages a portfolio of three business-services companies under a shared back-office model. The Business Model is written as an investment thesis with explicit CAC/LTV targets, fund return benchmarks, and a defined exit timeline. Tier 2 is Independent: the model is documented, the logic is explicit, and it could be replicated by a successor management team without the sponsor's participation.

### *Tier 3 — Business Entity*

The Business Entity is the legal and regulatory construct that holds rights, obligations, and property — what actually transfers in a share deal. Its governor is the legal and regulatory environment: the Entity is what law sees when it looks at the business. Its specification surface is the charter, the cap table, the debt structure, the counterparty contracts, and the audited financials. Its transferability mode is by document: equity interests or assets change hands through written instruments, and the legal act is complete on execution.

Coase (1937) grounds the firm boundary as the locus at which internal coordination is cheaper than market coordination; Williamson (1985) extends this to a theory of vertical integration as a governance choice. The Entity is the governance structure that these theories predict: the vehicle through which the Business Model's economic logic operates in the market, holding the contracts that make the value proposition credible to counterparties. The critical analytical point is that a clean Tier 3 is a necessary but not sufficient condition for a successful acquisition. Financial due diligence is designed to examine Tier 3 — and Tier 3 only. A financially healthy Entity with clean documentation can hold a business whose Tier 4 Product specification is entirely tacit, whose Tier 1 Intent is Fused to the founder, and whose Tier 6 Organization will fracture within twelve months of close. The Entity will transfer; the business will not.

*Vignette:* A clean LLC with a three-year audit history and no personal guarantees passes financial due diligence without exception. The data room shows healthy revenue, normalized EBITDA, and a well-structured cap table. What the data room cannot show is that the product specification — the thing generating that EBITDA — lives entirely in the founder's head and will dissolve at exit. The Entity is Independent; Tier 4 is Fused. The gap between them is the value that the buyer will discover missing on day sixty of the integration.

### *Tier 4 — Product*

The Product is what the business is hired to deliver — the specific outcome a customer or beneficiary is paying for, as determined by customer acceptance. Its governor is the customer or beneficiary: the Product is specified not by the producer's intention but by the acceptance criteria that the market enforces. Its specification surface includes acceptance criteria, tolerance bands, and dimensional specifications — the full set of observable criteria against which delivery quality is assessed. Its transferability mode is conditional: a written Product specification transfers; a tacit one does not.

Penrose (1959) grounds the productive resources of the firm as the core from which competitive advantage is derived; the Product is the external expression of the resource bundle, the specific output that the market values. Zharnikov (2026a) provides one example of a precision specification instrument for Tier 4 in the form of the SBT eight-dimension framework — Semiotic, Narrative, Ideological, Experiential, Social, Economic, Cultural, Temporal — which can serve as a dimensional specification surface where the Product is a brand-anchored offering. The critical distinction from Tier 5 is that Tier 4 defines what is produced, while Tier 5 defines how. Process documentation anchored to an unspecified Product is coordination machinery without a destination: it can run, but no one can determine whether it is running correctly.

*Vignette:* A SaaS company with $40M ARR has its Product codified in API contracts, SLA documents, and a formal roadmap with acceptance criteria. A third party could read the documentation and reconstruct the testing regime without the founding team's input. Tier 4 is Independent. The acquisition commands a premium multiple precisely because this independence removes the most common source of post-close value degradation.

### *Tier 5 — Process*

The Process is the coordinated activity that produces the Tier 4 Product, governed by management. Its specification surface covers standard operating procedures, workflow maps, ERP configuration, and supply-chain architecture — the full set of codified coordination instructions that tell the Organization how to produce the Product. Its transferability mode parallels Tier 4: codified Process transfers; tacit Process does not.

Mintzberg (1979) provides the foundational taxonomy of coordination mechanisms — direct supervision, standardization of work, standardization of outputs, standardization of skills, mutual adjustment — whose applicability to a given business determines the degree to which Tier 5 is transferable independently of the people who built it. Pentland and Feldman (2005) refine this view by distinguishing the ostensive aspect of a routine (its abstract, codifiable pattern) from its performative aspect (the situated enactment by specific individuals); a Process whose ostensive specification is fully codified can transfer across owners, while a Process whose value resides in its performative enactment cannot. Porter's (1985) value chain decomposes Tier 5 into primary and support activities, providing the horizontal structure that complements the vertical tier structure of the present framework. The constraint relationship between Tier 5 and Tier 6 is tight: the staffing structure, role design, and decision-allocation of the Organization were built to execute a specific Process. Imposing Process changes without first establishing the new Product's acceptance criteria deprives the Organization of the purpose context in which those changes make sense.

*Vignette:* A holding company with three commercial-services subsidiaries imposed a shared back-office Process over eighteen months. Twelve months in, the three operating teams continue to run legacy workflows in parallel with the mandated shared systems. Process documentation exists but describes mandated procedures; actual practice has diverged. Tier 5 is Partial: the buyer inherits a gap between documented Process and operational reality that will require active harmonization before any cross-sell synergy thesis can be executed.

### *Tier 6 — Organization*

The Organization is the set of people, roles, cultural norms, tacit knowledge, and relational networks that execute the Tier 5 Process, governed by the people and culture themselves. Its specification surface covers formal roles and employment contracts, organizational charts, and — critically — the tacit knowledge and relational networks that formal documents cannot capture. Its transferability mode is partial by structural necessity: employment contracts transfer as legal obligations but not as motivational commitments; culture cannot be acquired by document; tacit knowledge exits with the individual who holds it.

Hannan and Freeman's (1984) structural inertia framework establishes Tier 6 as the site of highest institutional resistance to change — the tier most likely to respond to new ownership by defending prior configurations rather than adapting to new ones. Wilensky's (1964) treatment of tacit organizational intelligence identifies the specific component of Tier 6 that retention contracts cannot capture: the judgment, relational trust, and contextual knowledge encoded in individuals through years of practice. Zhao (2023) establishes that CEO contract design on the acquirer side shapes what kinds of acquisition risk are tolerated, which in turn determines what degree of Tier 6 fragility the acquiring organization will accept in its targets. Stahl and Voigt (2008) and Strobl, Bauer, Shepherd, Lou, Tarba, and Dao (2024) provide the empirical grounding for Tier 6 as the tier most thoroughly studied in the M&A literature and, paradoxically, the least connected to the upstream tier configurations that produced its current state.

*Vignette:* A regional NGO has fourteen paid staff plus a volunteer base managed by a program coordinator and a governing board. The board simultaneously occupies Tier 1 (charter custodian) and Tier 6 (organizational actor) — a dual role that is the structural source of nonprofit governance complexity. Organization includes volunteers who are not on employment contracts, a feature invisible in standard financial due diligence. Tier 6 is Partial: paid-staff successors are partially identified, but the volunteer coordination function and board succession are underdeveloped.

## The Dual Hierarchy and Seven Propositions

### *Service Hierarchy and Constraint Hierarchy*

The six tiers are not merely a classification; they form two overlapping hierarchical structures with opposite directionality. Understanding both hierarchies is necessary for deriving the framework's integration-sequencing implications and for grounding the failure-propagation propositions that follow.

The service hierarchy runs upward: each tier contributes instrumentally to the tier above it. Organization serves Process — without the people and culture, the production system cannot operate. Process serves Product — without the production mechanism, the customer promise cannot be fulfilled. Product funds Entity — the revenue the Entity reports is generated by the Product the customer accepted. Entity implements Model — the legal construct is the vehicle through which the Business Model's economic logic operates in the market. Model realizes Intent — the Business Model is the owner's chosen answer to the market, selected in service of their purpose. This is an instrumental hierarchy: each tier's value is derived from its contribution to the tier above it; a Tier 6 disruption that reduces Process execution capacity reduces the Product quality that the Business Model promised to deliver.

The constraint hierarchy runs downward: each tier constrains the admissible set of configurations for the tier below it. Intent constrains the admissible set of Business Models — a Mission Founder whose Intent is identical to their Product cannot adopt a Business Model that treats program outcomes as negotiable. Business Model constrains admissible Entity forms — a cooperative member-benefit Model cannot be held in a C-corp structure without fundamental contradiction. Entity constrains admissible Products — a regulated pharmaceutical Entity cannot deliver a Product that its license does not cover. Product constrains required Process architecture — a zero-defect acceptance criterion requires a Process that detects defects before they reach the customer. Process constrains required Organization — a continuous 24-hour service Process requires an Organization with shift coverage, not a Monday-through-Friday team.

The service and constraint hierarchies are not two separate structures imposed on the same six tiers; they are the same set of relationships described from opposite directions. The service hierarchy asks, "What does each tier make possible for the tier above it?" The constraint hierarchy asks, "What does each tier rule out for the tier below it?" Together, they establish a system of mutual dependency that has a specific implication for failure analysis: a shock at any tier propagates in both directions. A Tier 6 (Organization) fracture reduces the productive capacity that Tier 5 (Process) requires; simultaneously, because Organization is the execution surface for Process, and Process is the production mechanism for Product, the downstream effect of a sustained Tier 6 fracture is Tier 4 (Product) drift — even if the Product specification itself was never altered. This is the structural basis for Proposition P6 below.

Penrose (1959) grounds the productive services framing: the value of organizational resources is not in their stock but in the services they render, and those services flow upward through the service hierarchy. Williamson (1985) grounds the governance-as-constraint perspective: governance structures exist to reduce opportunism and uncertainty in the specification of productive activity, and this reduction propagates downward through the constraint hierarchy. Bauer et al. (2024) establish empirically that acquisition integration capabilities must span multiple organizational domains — a finding consistent with the dual-hierarchy structure's implication that integration must address all six tiers, not just the most visible ones.

The integration sequencing implication follows directly from the constraint hierarchy: because upper tiers constrain lower tiers, specification clarity at upper tiers is logically prior to integration execution at lower tiers. A buyer who reorganizes Tier 6 (Organization) before resolving Tier 1 (Intent) compatibility is redesigning a team without knowing what the team is supposed to produce for whom. This sequencing principle is formalized in Section 6 and illustrated in Figure 1.

### *Failure-Propagation Propositions*

The following seven propositions formalize the failure-propagation pathways implied by the dual hierarchy. Each proposition is presented with its statement, mechanism, empirical anchor, confirming criteria, and falsifying criteria. The empirical anchor for each proposition reflects the current state of the literature as established in the deep-research synthesis conducted prior to this paper's drafting. Propositions P1 through P5 and P7 are theoretical — derived from the dual-hierarchy structure and consistent with adjacent single-layer findings, but not yet traced end-to-end in a single empirical study. Proposition P6 is partially empirically documented. The paper's contribution is the theoretical architecture; empirical validation is the agenda described in Section 7.

**P1 — Intent–Model Misalignment**

*Statement:* When an acquirer's Owner Intent differs structurally from the target's Owner Intent (for example, a financial-return-primary acquirer acquires a mission-primary target), the target's Business Model will require fundamental revision to conform to the new Tier 1 constraint, and this revision will generate downstream friction at Tier 4 (Product) and Tier 5 (Process) that is not visible in pre-close due diligence.

*Mechanism:* Owner Intent constrains the admissible set of Business Models via the downward constraint hierarchy. A mission-primary target's Business Model — theory of change plus grant funding model — was designed to optimize for impact metrics, not return on capital. A financial-return-primary acquirer's Tier 1 constraint is incompatible with this Model. The acquirer therefore faces a forced Model migration — a revision of the target's value proposition, funding sources, and unit economics — that propagates downward through the constraint hierarchy to disrupt Tier 4 product-market positioning and Tier 5 operational priorities. Bauer and Friesl (2024) establish that attentional crowding-out during integration — when acquirer attention is absorbed by financial and legal integration tasks — systematically deprives the Model-revision process of the managerial bandwidth needed to translate Tier 2 changes into coherent Tier 4 and Tier 5 adjustments.

*Empirical anchor:* No end-to-end empirical study traces Intent-level misalignment to Business Model migration to downstream tier failure in an M&A context. King et al. (2004) document that strategic-intent antecedents explain only a small fraction of post-acquisition performance variance. Meirovich (2017) identifies business-model misalignment as an underexamined failure mode (SSRN preprint, grey literature).

*Confirming criteria:* Studies that (a) measure Tier 1 Intent configuration at close and (b) trace forced Business Model revisions to specific Tier 4 or Tier 5 disruptions in the 12–36 months post-close, controlling for pre-close financial performance.

*Falsifying criteria:* P1 is falsified if a systematic comparison of for-profit acquisitions of mission-primary organizations shows no greater rate of Business Model revision or downstream tier disruption than comparable for-profit-to-for-profit acquisitions of equivalent financial profile.

**P2 — Business Model–Product Friction**

*Statement:* When an acquirer imposes a Business Model revision on the target — following P1 or independently — the target's Product specification will drift toward the new Model's optimization criteria, and customers whose decision to purchase was anchored in the prior Product's dimensional configuration will exhibit higher churn rates than pre-acquisition baselines.

*Mechanism:* The Business Model governs the admissible set of Products via the downward constraint hierarchy: the Product optimized for a mission-primary Model (beneficiary impact) is not the Product that maximizes margin in a return-primary Model. Product revision to match the new Model changes the dimensional configuration of the offering — affecting the Experiential, Ideological, and Social dimensions of the customer's or beneficiary's perception — and reduces the perceptual overlap between the Product the customer chose and the Product they now receive. Bauer and Friesl (2024) document synergy-evaluation failures in which acquirers mis-project Tier 4 demand stability under revised Business Models, overestimating the loyalty of customers whose purchase decisions were anchored in the prior Product's configuration.

*Empirical anchor:* No peer-reviewed study traces Business Model revision to Product-dimension drift to customer churn in an M&A context. Post-merger product-portfolio rationalization is documented in practitioner literature as a common integration activity, but downstream demand effects are not quantified in peer-reviewed evidence.

*Confirming criteria:* Studies measuring dimensional stability of the acquired brand's perceptual profile at close versus 12 and 36 months post-close, with churn disaggregated by customer cohort and benchmarked against pre-acquisition cohort retention rates.

*Falsifying criteria:* P2 is falsified if Business Model revisions imposed by acquirers show no statistically significant association with Product-dimensional drift in a sample of completed acquisitions with matched pre- and post-close perceptual data.

**P3 — Entity–Operations Disruption**

*Statement:* When Entity restructuring — subsidiary consolidation, re-domiciling, or change of legal form — follows close, operational continuity at Tier 5 (Process) and Tier 4 (Product) will be disrupted by contract renegotiation, regulatory re-certification, and counterparty uncertainty at rates disproportionate to the legal scope of the restructuring.

*Mechanism:* The Business Entity is the contract-holding, license-carrying construct through which the target's Products are delivered and Processes are operated. Restructuring the Entity — changing the legal vehicle through which counterparty relationships are held — does not automatically assign those relationships to the new vehicle; many contracts include change-of-control clauses or regulatory certifications that are Entity-specific. The operational disruption is not a consequence of the legal act itself but of the downstream dependencies on the specific Entity that the acquirer did not enumerate in due diligence. The constraint relationship explains why: Tier 3 constrains admissible Products via regulatory scope, and restructuring that scope disrupts the previously acceptable Product without producing a new specification anchor.

*Empirical anchor:* No peer-reviewed evidence documents Entity restructuring as an isolated antecedent to operational disruption in a traced causal design. Legal-complexity variables are included in some M&A performance models but are not disaggregated to the Entity-restructuring event.

*Confirming criteria:* Event studies comparing operational-continuity metrics — service levels, production output — for acquisitions with and without Entity restructuring in the 90 days post-close, controlling for deal size and industry.

*Falsifying criteria:* P3 is falsified if Entity-restructuring events show no significant association with operational-continuity disruption across a sample of acquisitions with documented Entity changes.

**P4 — Product–Process Disruption**

*Statement:* When an acquirer revises the target's Product specification — following P2 or independently — the Process architecture that was designed to produce the prior Product will exhibit misalignment with the new specification's requirements, generating quality failures or cost overruns that persist until the Process architecture is rebuilt around the new specification.

*Mechanism:* Process is constrained by Product via the downward constraint hierarchy: a zero-defect Product acceptance criterion requires a Process with defect-detection capacity; a high-throughput Product specification requires a Process with high-volume capacity. Revising the Product specification without simultaneously revising the Process creates a mismatch between what the production machinery was designed to produce and what the new specification requires. This mismatch produces quality failures when the old Process cannot meet the new Product's acceptance criteria, or cost overruns when the old Process is over-engineered for the new specification's lower standards. The impossibility of fully specifying Product requirements to arbitrary resolution (Zharnikov 2026h) means that the mismatch accumulates tacit residuals that cannot be eliminated by documentation alone.

*Empirical anchor:* No peer-reviewed M&A evidence traces Product-specification revision to Process-architecture mismatch. SKU-rationalization literature documents process disruption in manufacturing contexts outside M&A settings but does not establish a causal chain in acquisition-specific designs.

*Confirming criteria:* Case comparisons of acquisitions with and without documented Product-specification revisions, measuring quality-defect rates and process-cost variances at 6 and 18 months post-close.

*Falsifying criteria:* P4 is falsified if Product-specification revisions following acquisition show no systematic association with quality-failure or cost-overrun rates, controlling for baseline Process maturity.

**P5 — Process–Organization Fracture**

*Statement:* When Process integration — ERP harmonization, SOP standardization, workflow reengineering — is imposed on the acquired Organization before Tier 4 (Product) specification clarity is established, employee resistance and attrition will exceed rates observed when Process integration follows Product specification confirmation.

*Mechanism:* Organization is constrained by Process via the downward constraint hierarchy: the staffing structure, role design, and decision-allocation of the Organization were built to execute a specific Process in service of a specific Product. Imposing Process changes without establishing the new Product's acceptance criteria deprives the Organization of the purpose context in which the changes make sense. Employees who understand what the business is supposed to produce and for whom can adapt to Process changes as rational adjustments in service of a known goal; employees whose Product context has been removed by an unresolved Tier 1 through Tier 4 cascade experience Process changes as arbitrary administrative disruption. Klok et al. (2022) establish that emotional dynamics during integration — employees' uncertainty about what they are working toward — are systematically underestimated in integration planning, consistent with the mechanism above. Stahl and Voigt (2008) show that integration approach moderates sociocultural integration outcomes; Smeulders et al. (2023) show that integration controls affect employee resistance, providing adjacent empirical links without tracing the full Process-first sequencing effect.

*Empirical anchor:* Adjacent links partially documented. No study traces the Process-first versus Product-first sequencing effect specifically.

*Confirming criteria:* Quasi-experimental comparisons of integrations sequenced Product-first versus Process-first, measuring executive turnover and employee attrition rates at 12 and 36 months.

*Falsifying criteria:* P5 is falsified if Process-integration sequencing — before versus after Product specification clarity — shows no significant difference in attrition rates across a matched sample of acquisitions.

**P6 — Organization Fracture to All-Layer Collapse**

*Statement:* When key Organization members — executives, technical specialists, or relationship holders — exit following acquisition, the tacit knowledge they carried, including uncodified Product specification, Process exception-handling, and customer relationship maintenance, will degrade across all six tiers at rates that cannot be recovered by retention bonuses or documentation efforts initiated after exit.

*Mechanism:* Tier 6 (Organization) is the sole repository of tacit specification knowledge that was never externalized into the Tier 4 or Tier 5 specification surfaces. This knowledge is irreversible: once the person who holds it exits, the knowledge exits with them. Financial incentives can delay exit; they cannot transfer tacit knowledge to a new holder. The all-layer degradation follows from the service hierarchy: Organization serves Process, so Tier 5 execution degrades; Process serves Product, so Tier 4 quality degrades; Product funds Entity, so Tier 3 revenue degrades; Entity implements Model, so Tier 2 viability degrades. Strobl et al. (2024) establish that contradictory HR signals during integration accelerate exit decisions, amplifying the mechanism above. Bauer et al. (2024) show that integration-capability deficits produce precisely the conditions in which tacit knowledge loss is most severe.

*Empirical anchor:* This is the best-supported pathway. Stahl and Voigt (2008) trace cultural differences to sociocultural integration to synergy realization to shareholder value across 46 studies. Bilgili et al. (2017) establish executive turnover to post-acquisition performance in a meta-analytic design. The chain from Organization fracture to all-layer value degradation is partially documented across these two sources; no single study traces the full pathway end-to-end. Smeulders et al. (2023) provide supporting evidence that integration controls moderate the resistance effects that precede exit decisions.

*Confirming criteria:* Longitudinal studies that (a) measure pre-close tacit knowledge codification rates by tier, (b) track executive and key-specialist turnover at 6, 12, and 36 months, and (c) measure tier-specific value preservation — Product dimensional stability, Process efficiency, customer retention — as outcome variables.

*Falsifying criteria:* P6 is falsified if acquisitions with high pre-close tacit knowledge codification — written Tier 4 and Tier 5 specifications — show the same rate of all-layer value degradation following executive turnover as acquisitions with low pre-close codification.

**P7 — Bidirectional Feedback**

*Statement:* Tier failure does not propagate unidirectionally from upper to lower tiers; sustained lower-tier failures — Process breakdown or Organization fracture — force upward revision of Business Model and, in extreme cases, Owner Intent, creating feedback loops that amplify initial shocks and accelerate total deal-value destruction.

*Mechanism:* The service hierarchy runs upward: sustained Process failure reduces the Product quality that the Business Model promised to deliver; sustained Product degradation erodes the revenue that the Entity reports; sustained revenue erosion requires Business Model revision to restore viability. In extreme cases — where the Business Model was predicated on the target's tacit Organization knowledge being preserved — Tier 6 fracture forces a de facto Tier 1 Intent revision on the acquirer: the deal thesis collapses because the asset the acquirer bought was not the asset received. The feedback loop amplifies because upper-tier revisions generate new lower-tier constraints via the constraint hierarchy, restarting the cascade. Bauer and Friesl (2024) document synergy-evaluation failures in which initial integration-cost underestimates — driven by underestimating Tier 5 and Tier 6 integration difficulty — force sequential Business Model revision as each cost anchor fails, consistent with the feedback mechanism above.

*Empirical anchor:* No peer-reviewed evidence documents bidirectional feedback loops in M&A contexts. All four DR engines consulted during literature development returned NOT FOUND for empirical evidence of lower-layer failures forcing upward revision of Business Model or Intent in a traced M&A setting. This is a purely theoretical proposition derived from the dual-hierarchy structure.

*Confirming criteria:* Case studies or panel data documenting sequential tier revisions — upward from Process and Organization fracture to Business Model restructuring — in the 18–48 months post-close, with explicit elimination of concurrent exogenous shocks as alternative explanations.

*Falsifying criteria:* P7 is falsified if a systematic case comparison shows no instances of lower-tier failures precipitating Business Model or Intent revision in acquisitions where exogenous market shocks are controlled for.

## Form-Invariance

### *Substitution Rules Across Organizational Forms*

The six-tier structure does not require a for-profit assumption. Every tier has a structurally equivalent form across four organizational types: for-profit, NGO/charity, mutual/cooperative, and state-owned enterprise. Table 2 presents the full substitution matrix.

**Table 2: Form-Invariant Six-Tier Ontology Across Organizational Types.**

| Tier | For-Profit | NGO / Charity | Mutual / Cooperative | State-Owned |
|------|------------|---------------|----------------------|-------------|
| 1 Intent | Owner goal (return in domain X) | Charter mission + board fiduciary duty | Member benefit (collective intent) | Government mandate (public service + fiscal contribution) |
| 2 Model | Value capture: revenue, margin, unit economics | Theory of change + funding model (grants / fees / endowment) | Member-benefit model (cost-recovery + dividends to members) | Public-service model + treasury contribution |
| 3 Entity | LLC, C-corp, GmbH | 501(c)(3), CIC, foundation, charity, association | Cooperative, mutual society, credit union | SOE, public-law entity, agency |
| 4 Product | Customer-paid output | Beneficiary-received impact | Member-received service | Citizen-received service |
| 5 Process | Activity producing output | Activity producing impact | Activity producing member service | Activity producing public service |
| 6 Organization | Employees | Employees + volunteers + board | Member-employees + officers | Civil servants + appointees |

*Notes*: Tier 1 and Tier 3 substitutions are anchored in Hansmann (1996) for for-profit, NGO, and cooperative forms and in Bruton, Peng, Ahlstrom, Stan, and Xu (2015) for the state-owned form. Tier 1 and Tier 6 NGO/hybrid substitutions draw additionally on Battilana and Lee (2014). The Tier 2, Tier 4, and Tier 5 substitutions across all four forms are theoretical deductions from the tier definitions, not empirically validated cross-form mappings; they are explicitly provisional (see Section 5.3). The cooperative Tier 3 entry applies to democratically governed entities requiring member consent for ownership transfer; Tier 1 is not a scalar individual intent but a collective mandate governable only through governance structure.

Hansmann (1996) provides the primary peer-reviewed anchor for the Tier 1 and Tier 3 substitutions across for-profit, NGO, and cooperative forms, establishing that ownership structure — the right to receive residual earnings and the right to exercise control — systematically varies across organizational types in ways that determine the feasibility and terms of ownership transfer. The state-owned enterprise form presents a Tier 1 that is held not by an individual or a membership collective but by a politically constituted authority with its own succession dynamics: the government mandate does not die with a particular minister and does not transfer by individual consent.

The privatization case — state-owned to for-profit acquisition — is the most visible cross-form transaction and the most instructive application of the form-invariance table. A privatization is, in this framework, a Tier 1 substitution: the Intent moves from public-service mandate to private-return-primary. Product and Process tiers do not automatically follow. Privatizations that fail characteristically do so because the acquirer assumes Tiers 4 and 5 will adapt to the new Tier 1 without deliberate extraction and documentation — the same mistake that kills lower-mid-market founder transactions. The constraint hierarchy explains why the assumption is wrong: a Process built to produce a citizen service under a public-service mandate cannot be repurposed to produce a private product for a fee without explicitly revising the Tier 4 acceptance criteria and rebuilding the Tier 5 quality architecture around those new criteria. Bruton et al. (2015) establish that SOEs are hybrid organizations whose governance complexity — the coexistence of public-service and commercial-return objectives — mirrors the form-mismatch challenge that the framework identifies.

### *The Flip Case: Intent Equals Product*

In standard for-profit transactions, Tier 1 (Owner Intent) and Tier 4 (Product) are distinct: the owner's purpose is to capture return in a domain, and the Product is the specific mechanism through which that return is generated. The owner could, in principle, sell the business and apply their Intent elsewhere — acquire a different company in the same domain, or reinvest the proceeds in an adjacent domain. Intent survives the divestiture.

In identity-bound cases — the Domain Craftsman, the Family Steward, the Mission Founder — Intent is not separable from Product. The founder does not have an Intent to which the business happens to be the answer; the business is the Intent's only possible expression. The craft studio, the family winery, the regional literacy program — in each case, removing the product would remove the reason for existence. Structurally: Intent ≡ Product.

The chain collapses: Intent ≡ Product ← (Model = funding wrapper) ← Entity (legal cover) ← Process ← Organization. The tiers below Intent still exist and remain separately transferable in limited forms: assets can be sold, IP can be licensed, employees can be hired. What collapses is the separability of Intent from Product — the two cannot be split without destroying the thing that made the business what it is.

Selznick (1957) provides the closest prior formulation: institutionalization as "infusion with value" — the transition from organization-as-expendable-tool to organization-as-organic-entity — and his "test of expendability" (would the organization be readily dissolved or redirected to serve other ends?) is the structural precursor to the flip case. Marquis and Tilcsik (2013) establish imprinting as the mechanism: the founder's Identity is encoded in organizational structure during the founding period, creating a persistent constraint on the Product and Process configurations that will be recognized as legitimate by the founding cohort. Whetten (2006) grounds organizational identity as the claim that certain attributes are central, enduring, and distinctive; the flip case is the limiting condition in which the founder's personal identity and the organization's identity are co-constituted, such that a change in Tier 1 governor is simultaneously a change in organizational identity.

The family-steward case extends the flip case through intergenerational transmission. Pecis, Ge, and Bauer (2025), in a rhetorical history analysis of family businesses, trace how family and business goals become entangled through narrative articulation across generations — each generation inheriting not merely the business assets but the rhetorical construction of what the business is for. This longitudinal goal-entanglement process produces precisely the structural condition the flip case describes: a business whose Tier 1 Intent and Tier 4 Product have been co-narrated into identity equivalence over decades. Berrone, Cruz, and Gomez-Mejia (2012) establish the static consequence through the socioemotional wealth (SEW) lens: identity-bound principals systematically overvalue the firm relative to its financial utility, producing a reservation price above rational financial valuation that makes the firm effectively unsellable to pure financial buyers.

The consequence for M&A practice is categorical: firms in the flipped chain are unsellable as going concerns to a buyer who does not share the founder's identity commitment. They can sell assets. They can sell IP. They can merge with an entity that shares their Intent. They cannot be acquired by a financial buyer without changing what the business fundamentally is — a change that will be detected by the customer cohort, whose purchase decision was anchored in the prior Product's dimensional configuration.

### *Provisional Anchoring of Tiers 2, 4, and 5 Substitutions*

Hansmann (1996) provides peer-reviewed support for the Tier 1 and Tier 3 substitutions across for-profit, NGO, and cooperative forms. Bruton et al. (2015) anchor the state-owned Tier 1 substitution. Battilana and Lee (2014) anchor the NGO and hybrid Tier 1 and Tier 6 substitutions.

The Tier 2, Tier 4, and Tier 5 substitutions across all four forms are theoretical deductions from the tier definitions, not empirically validated cross-form mappings. "Theory of change + funding model" is the NGO's functional equivalent of "value capture + unit economics" at Tier 2 because both constitute the answer to "how does value flow to the organization from the external environment?" — the defining question of the Business Model tier — adapted to the form-specific value logic. "Citizen-received service" is the state-owned equivalent of "customer-paid output" at Tier 4 because both are defined by the acceptance criteria of the Tier's governor (customer or beneficiary acceptance) adapted to the form's specific service recipient. These are logically derived from the tier definitions and structurally coherent, but they are not empirically validated across forms. The validation gap is a specific future research priority addressed in Section 7.3.

## Implications for M&A Integration Theory

### *The Integration Sequencing Principle*

The constraint hierarchy derives a sequencing principle for M&A integration that is formal rather than prescriptive: because upper tiers constrain the admissible configurations of lower tiers, specification clarity at upper tiers is logically prior to integration execution at lower tiers. The sequencing implication is not a consulting recommendation but a structural necessity — attempting to finalize lower-tier configurations before upper-tier specifications are resolved is building on a foundation that the constraint hierarchy has not yet established.

The formal sequence is: (1) resolve Tier 1 (Intent) compatibility before Tier 2 (Business Model) revision; (2) resolve Tier 2 revision before Tier 4 (Product) specification; (3) confirm Tier 4 specification before Tier 5 (Process) integration; (4) confirm Tier 5 architecture before Tier 6 (Organization) role redesign. Tier 3 (Entity) integration is legally required immediately post-close and cannot be sequenced into this flow; the framework implies that Tier 3 integration should be designed to minimize disruption to Tiers 4 through 6 while upper-tier specification clarity is being established — a design choice that standard M&A legal practice does not currently specify.

**Table 3: Integration Sequencing Derived from the Constraint Hierarchy.**

| Phase | Tier Priority | Address Before | Theoretical Basis |
|-------|---------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Pre-close | Tier 1 compatibility | Any Tier 2 revision | Constraint hierarchy; P1 |
| Pre-close or Day 1 | Tier 4 specification | Tier 5 integration | P4; Tier 4 tacit knowledge loss risk (P6) |
| Months 1–6 | Tier 2 revision (if required) | Tier 5 harmonization | P2; P4 chaining |
| Months 1–6 | Tier 5 codification | Tier 6 role redesign | P5; Tier 6 resistance without purpose context |
| Months 6–18 | Tier 6 transition | Founder departure | P6; tacit knowledge transfer requires time |

*Notes*: The sequencing principle is derived from the constraint hierarchy, not from empirical observation of integration practice. Deviations from this sequence — the modal failure mode in the literature (Larsson and Finkelstein 1999) — produce the cascade pathways formalized in P1–P7. Tier 3 integration is omitted from the sequencing table because it is legally mandated immediately post-close; the design imperative is to minimize its disruption to Tiers 4–6.

The modal failure mode in practice inverts this sequence: acquirers begin with Tier 6 (Organization) — immediately visible, urgent, and socially demanding — and Tier 5 (Process) — operationally critical in the first weeks — while Tiers 1 through 4 remain unresolved. This sequencing inversion is the structural origin of the cascade pathways formalized in P1 through P7. Larsson and Finkelstein (1999) identify inadequate organizational integration as the dominant predictor of synergy failure in their case-survey methodology; in tier-level vocabulary, their finding maps to Tier 5 forced-merge producing Tier 4 drift — a P4 cascade — whose upstream origin in an unresolved Tier 1 compatibility question the authors do not observe because their unit of analysis is the organizational layer, not the full tier stack.

Bauer et al. (2024) establish that acquisition integration capabilities must span multiple organizational domains and that capability deficits at any domain reduce synergy realization across the full deal. This finding is consistent with the dual-hierarchy framework's claim that integration is a cross-tier problem, not a within-tier problem, and that capability at any single tier cannot compensate for misspecification at the tiers above it.

### *The Separability-as-Screening Principle*

The Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic (STSD) — described fully in Section 7 — produces a Fused/Partial/Independent profile for each of the six tiers before close. This profile has a specific implication for integration theory: the profile forecasts integration risk by tier-binding density before the deal closes, enabling acquirers to price risk by tier rather than by aggregate financial performance.

A profile with Fused at Tier 1 signals the flip case (Section 5.2): no post-close integration sequence can resolve this condition because the flip case is a structural feature of the target, not a remediable information asymmetry. The deal thesis itself requires reconsidering — the buyer is not acquiring a going concern whose Intent can be redirected; they are acquiring an asset bundle whose value as a going concern depends on the Tier 1 governor's continued commitment.

A profile with Fused at Tier 4 signals that the Product specification lives in the founder's judgment and will degrade post-close regardless of retention efforts, because the tacit specification exits with the person who holds it. Pre-close codification — a structured product-specification extraction conducted as a closing condition — is the only intervention that changes the Tier 4 separability state before the founder's departure removes the specification from the organization. A profile with Partial at Tier 5 signals process-maturity gaps that are tractable: codification can close the gap, but must be scoped explicitly as integration cost rather than assumed synergy. A profile with Partial at Tier 6 signals key-person dependency: retention structures should be conditioned on knowledge-transfer milestones verified by the new management team, not on calendar milestones that neither measure nor incentivize knowledge transfer.

The framework's primary theoretical contribution to integration theory is the disaggregation of due-diligence risk by tier rather than production of an aggregate pass/fail verdict. Standard financial due diligence produces a Tier 3 verdict: the Entity is clean or it is not. The STSD produces a six-cell profile that maps risk to tier, allowing acquirers to design tier-specific integration interventions rather than applying generic integration playbooks that are optimized for no particular tier configuration. Bauer and Friesl (2024) provide complementary support from an attention-based view: when integration attention is allocated by aggregate severity rather than by tier-specific risk, attentional crowding-out causes high-severity tiers to consume cognitive resources that lower-severity but causally upstream tiers require — the STSD profile makes this allocation problem explicit by surfacing which tiers actually carry the binding integration risk.

### *The Form-Mismatch Principle*

Cross-form acquisitions — a for-profit acquirer targeting a cooperative or NGO — face Tier 1 misspecification risk that no standard due-diligence checklist captures, because standard financial due diligence examines Tier 3 and does not examine Tier 1 compatibility. The form-invariance table (Table 2) reveals the specific substitution that must be negotiated in cross-form deals: the acquiring entity's Tier 1 (Intent) must be compatible with the target's Tier 1, or the downward constraint chain will generate forced revisions at every lower tier.

The cooperative presents a distinctive structural challenge: Tier 1 Intent is collectively held and democratically governed. No single principal can unilaterally authorize a sale; the member assembly must consent. A credit union cannot be acquired by a bank without member consent not because of regulatory friction alone — although regulatory friction exists — but because the Intent itself, the collective member-benefit mandate, cannot be transferred without being replaced by a fundamentally different Intent. The STSD applied to a cooperative requires a collective-governance version of the diagnostic in which the question "Can the owner state their Intent in one sentence that does not contain the company's name?" becomes "Can the member assembly articulate a purpose that survives the transaction without changing the economic logic of membership?" Battilana and Lee (2014) establish that hybrid organizations at the for-profit/NGO boundary face mission-drift risk when acquired by non-mission buyers — an empirical instantiation of the form-mismatch cascade that the present framework formalizes.

Privatization transactions, as noted in Section 5.1, are the most visible cross-form case and the most tractable natural experiment for empirical testing of the form-mismatch principle: the Tier 1 substitution is explicit, documented in government records, and associated with large-scale post-acquisition operational changes that can be retrospectively coded against the tier-level predictions.

## The Separability Diagnostic and Validation Roadmap

### *The Six-Question Diagnostic*

The Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic (STSD) is a structured judgment instrument that produces a six-cell profile — one state per tier, three possible states: Fused, Partial, or Independent. Table 4 presents the full diagnostic.

**Table 4: The Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic.**

| Tier | Diagnostic Question | Fused | Partial | Independent |
|------|---------------------|-------|---------|-------------|
| 1 Intent | Can the owner state their Intent in one sentence that does NOT contain the company's name? | No — the company IS the answer | Named but tied to company's continued existence | Yes — stated purpose survives without this specific entity |
| 2 Business Model | Is the model articulated as a written canvas — value proposition, addressable cohorts, unit economics, channels — independent of "what we have always done"? | No — implicit, inarticulable without reference to history | Partial — some components explicit, others tacit | Yes — fully written; anyone could test the logic |
| 3 Business Entity | Is the Entity's cap table, debt structure, and counterparty obligations decoupled from the owner's personal balance sheet? | No — personal guarantees, commingled accounts | Some decoupling; residual personal obligations remain | Yes — fully decoupled; clean separation between personal and corporate |
| 4 Product | Is there a written WHAT-specification — acceptance criteria a third party could read and reconstruct testing against? | No — specification lives in founder's judgment | Partial codification; key acceptance criteria implicit | Yes — written spec, team-held, survives founder exit |
| 5 Process | Does the process run for a continuous month without the owner physically present? | No — owner must be present for key decisions | Owner required for exceptions; routine operations self-running | Yes — process runs fully; owner presence optional |
| 6 Organization | Does every role the owner occupies have a named, trained successor? | No — owner occupies irreplaceable roles | Some successors identified; key roles underbacked | Yes — all owner roles covered by named successors |

*Notes*: The STSD is a structured judgment instrument, not a scored psychometric scale. It produces an ordinal state per tier (Fused / Partial / Independent), not a continuous score. Scoring rules and inter-rater reliability data are the first priority for validation (Section 7.2). Identity-bound owners (Domain Craftsman, Family Steward, Mission Founder) will always show Fused at Tier 1; this is a structural state, not a remediable condition. The diagnostic describes reality; it does not prescribe action. A Fused Tier 1 profile tells the buyer what deal they are actually making, which may differ from the deal they think they are making.

The STSD is named for citability and to enable the instrument to accumulate an empirical record. "Separability" is the key theoretical construct: the profile's value lies in making tier-level binding density observable before close, not in producing a single summary score that would collapse the tier-level distinction the framework requires.

How to read the profile: a profile of Independent / Independent / Independent / Independent / Partial / Partial indicates a highly transferable business with integration risk concentrated in Tiers 5 and 6 — the acquirer inherits a clean entity, a documented model, a specified product, and must manage process and organizational continuity through deliberate retention and documentation. This is the SaaS company profile described in Section 3.4. A profile of Fused / Fused / Partial / Fused / Partial / Fused indicates a business that presents as a standard transaction from the financial-due-diligence seat but will not survive the transition intact — the regional manufacturer profile described in Section 3.1. The six-cell structure makes visible what aggregate financial performance measures cannot: the tier-level distribution of value at risk.

### *Validation Protocol*

The STSD is currently a structured judgment instrument with no inter-rater reliability data and no published predictive-validity evidence. The following validation roadmap identifies the research designs required to move the instrument from a theoretical proposal to an empirically validated diagnostic.

*Inter-rater reliability.* The six diagnostic questions should be translated into a semi-structured interview protocol with probes for each response type — specifically, examples that distinguish Fused from Partial at each tier. Two independent assessors conduct the protocol on the same acquisition target without contact before scoring. Cohen's κ ≥ .70 is the target threshold for each tier-level classification, consistent with Landis and Koch's (1977) standard for acceptable inter-rater agreement on ordinal clinical instruments. Preliminary testing on archival acquisition cases with known post-close outcomes would establish the protocol before prospective deployment.

*Predictive validity.* The primary predictive hypothesis: the number of Fused tiers at close will be negatively associated with acquisition performance at 36 months — more Fused tiers, lower performance — after controlling for deal size, industry, and financial leverage. This is the most tractable operationalization of the framework's core claim. Secondary hypotheses follow from individual propositions: (a) Fused Tier 4 at close predicts higher executive churn in the first 18 months (P6 pathway); (b) Fused Tier 1 with a for-profit acquirer predicts higher rate of Business Model revision within 24 months (P1 pathway); (c) Partial Tier 5 at close predicts higher operational-disruption scores at 6 months (P4 pathway). The validation study requires pre-close due-diligence data and post-close performance data from the same acquisition — a design requiring either a practitioner partner with access to deal records or a retrospective case-archival methodology using publicly reported M&A disclosures.

### *Sampling Design*

The form-invariance claim (Section 5) requires that the STSD produce reliable classifications for for-profit, NGO, cooperative, and state-owned targets. A minimum sampling frame of ten completed acquisitions per organizational form — forty total — drawn from publicly reported deals or practitioner case archives, provides a basis for cross-form reliability comparison. Priority sampling should focus on state-owned privatization transactions as the most tractable natural experiment for the form-mismatch principle (Section 6.3): the Tier 1 substitution is explicit and documented in government records, the post-privatization operational record is often publicly reported, and the deal size makes retrospective data collection feasible.

The validation of the Tier 2, Tier 4, and Tier 5 form-invariance substitutions (Table 2) requires the cross-form comparison design specifically: if the STSD produces consistent profiles across organizational forms — including the provisionally deduced substitutions for Tiers 2, 4, and 5 — this constitutes provisional empirical support for the substitution rules. If classifications diverge systematically by form at those tiers, the substitution rules require revision.

## Discussion, Limitations, and Future Research

### *Theoretical Contribution*

The six-tier ontology offers three contributions to organizational and M&A theory. First, it provides the first unified vocabulary for specifying the full acquisition target across organizational forms — a vocabulary in which the distinction between Tier 1 (Intent) and Tier 4 (Product) is not a semantic nicety but a structural diagnostic: confusing the two is the mechanism behind the most costly category of founder-bound acquisition failures. Second, the dual service and constraint hierarchies formalize the constraint-sequencing logic that integration theory has described anecdotally as "get the cultural integration right before the operational integration" without deriving the sequencing from first principles. The dual hierarchy supplies that derivation. Third, the seven propositions provide a falsifiable theoretical architecture for a literature that King et al. (2004) and King et al. (2021) document has been unable to explain residual variance with single-layer models — the propositions are not empirical findings but explicit testable claims whose confirmation or falsification will discipline the next generation of M&A research.

### *Limitations*

**Cascade claim is theoretical.** Propositions P1 through P5 and P7 have no end-to-end empirical support. They are derived from the dual hierarchy and are consistent with adjacent single-layer findings, but they have not been traced in a single integrated study. The paper is explicit throughout about this status. This is the appropriate framing for an AMR conceptual contribution: the framework provides a theoretical architecture whose value is in the testable predictions it generates, not in empirical findings it has yet to produce. The framing of limitations as defects mistakes the genre of theoretical work for the genre of empirical work.

**Form-invariance Tiers 2, 4, and 5 substitutions are provisional.** The Tier 2, Tier 4, and Tier 5 substitutions in Table 2 are theoretical deductions from the tier definitions, not empirically validated cross-form mappings. Hansmann (1996) anchors Tiers 1 and 3; Bruton et al. (2015) and Battilana and Lee (2014) anchor the state-owned and hybrid Tier 1 substitutions. The Tier 2, 4, and 5 substitutions across forms — the NGO's "theory of change + funding model" as Tier 2 equivalent, the state-owned enterprise's "citizen-received service" as Tier 4 equivalent — are logically derived from the tier definitions and structurally coherent, but they require empirical validation in the cross-form comparative design described in Section 7.3 before they can be treated as established.

**No primary empirical data.** The paper contributes a theoretical framework and a structured diagnostic instrument. It does not present primary data. This choice is deliberate: the contribution is ontological and theoretical, not empirical. A theory paper that rushes to validation risks the error of testing a framework whose definitions are not yet stable; stabilizing the definitions — which is what the present paper does — is the necessary precondition for valid empirical testing. The validation agenda in Section 7 is the appropriate sequel, not a deficit of the current contribution.

**Multi-principal Intent configurations.** The thirteen-archetype map in the source framework assumes a dominant principal whose Intent can be characterized as a relatively stable stance. When ownership is dispersed — public companies, VC-backed startups with multiple investor classes, family businesses with sibling co-owners — Intent is a vector, not a scalar. The STSD Tier 1 question requires adaptation for multi-principal cases: the relevant diagnostic unit shifts from individual to governance structure. The framework is tractable for multi-principal cases in principle but requires explicit extension beyond the scope of the current paper.

### *Future Research*

**P6 at scale.** The most tractable first test: a longitudinal panel study of acquisitions measuring pre-close tacit knowledge codification (Tier 4 and Tier 5 specification completeness, scored by independent assessors using the STSD), executive turnover rates at 6, 12, and 18 months, and deal-value realization at 36 months. Pathway 6 has the strongest existing empirical support (Stahl and Voigt 2008; Bilgili et al. 2017); a well-designed study could move it from "partially documented" to "confirmed" and provide the evidential anchor for the full cascade framework.

**STSD validation.** Develop the semi-structured interview guide from the six diagnostic questions, compute inter-rater reliability across independent assessors on the same targets at κ ≥ .70, and test predictive validity against 12- and 36-month outcomes. Multi-method validation — combining interview-based STSD scoring with archival data on deal terms, retention structures, and post-close operational metrics — would establish the instrument's construct validity alongside its predictive validity.

**Cross-form comparative case sampling.** Apply the STSD retrospectively to a matched sample of for-profit, NGO, cooperative, and state-owned acquisitions to validate the form-invariance substitution rules for Tiers 2, 4, and 5. Privatization transactions are the priority sampling frame: explicit Tier 1 substitutions, documented policy rationales, and often publicly reported post-privatization operational outcomes provide a natural experiment that other cross-form acquisitions rarely offer at comparable scale and documentation depth.

**Network and platform businesses.** In businesses where customers are structural components of the asset — network-effect platforms, two-sided markets, professional associations — the clean distinction between Tier 4 (what is delivered to the customer) and the customer as external agent breaks down. The customer base is simultaneously the recipient of the Product and part of the Product itself. An extension or sub-tier for "the user base as transferable property" would address this boundary condition and is the most important structural extension the current framework requires.

## Online Appendix: Extended Worked Examples

*Note: The following four worked examples are provided as supplementary material. The vignettes in the main text (Sections 3.1–3.6) are the primary illustrative vehicles; these extended examples are intended for readers seeking additional operational detail.*

**Example A — Regional Manufacturing Services Firm, $18M Revenue**

The founder is sixty-two years old, forty years in the trade. Owner Intent, when probed, produces sentences that describe the craft — "doing this work properly" — not a financial return objective. The Business Model has never been written; when asked, the founder says "we serve our clients well," which is a mission statement, not a model. The Entity is a clean LLC with a three-year audit history, but the revolving credit line carries a personal guarantee, decoupling the Tier 3 balance sheet from Tier 1 psyche in the legal sense while keeping them financially entangled. The Product is specified tacitly: "quality" is a word the founder uses often and cannot fully externalize. Two hundred pages of operational SOPs exist but describe the coordination machinery without any document specifying what that machinery is producing. The Organization is a hub-and-spoke system with the founder at the center.

Separability profile: Fused / Fused / Partial / Fused / Partial / Fused.

M&A implication: this business requires Product specification extraction before the deal progresses, a written Tier 4 specification as a closing condition, and a retention structure conditioned on knowledge-transfer milestones.

**Example B — SaaS Company, $40M ARR**

Owner Intent is Builder/Compounder — financial return and the satisfaction of having built durable software, neither dominating. The Business Model is written with explicit CAC/LTV tracking, churn monitored by product tier, and unit economics that survive stress-testing. The Entity has a clean cap table with VC participation structured as preferred equity and no personal guarantees. The Product is codified in API contracts, SLA documents, and a formal roadmap with acceptance criteria. The Process has most workflows codified at L2; some senior IC-level decisions remain tacit, particularly around technical debt prioritization. The Organization has rotating leadership but key engineers hold tacit architectural judgment.

Separability profile: Independent / Independent / Independent / Independent / Partial / Partial.

M&A implication: highly transferable, integration risk concentrated in Tiers 5 and 6. The earn-out structure should be light on financial conditions and focused on top-engineer retention milestones and process documentation delivery within 12 months of close.

**Example C — Holdco/Roll-Up Target (Three Commercial-Services Subsidiaries)**

Owner Intent is Financial Sponsor — portfolio play, explicit investment horizon, pure return orientation. The Business Model is a shared back-office efficiency model imposed across all three entities. The Entity structure is layered: a holding entity above three subsidiaries, each with its own legal existence. The Products are three distinct offerings with an overlapping customer base. The Process at the back-office level was imposed over the last eighteen months with uneven adoption; three operating teams still run legacy workflows. Founding management teams have retired or are in transition; institutional memory is fragmentary.

Separability profile: Independent / Partial / Independent / Independent / Partial / Partial.

M&A implication: the acquirer buying this holdco is buying primarily a legal efficiency play. The operational integration work — aligning Tier 5 across three legacy systems, clarifying the Tier 4 relationship between the three Products for the shared customer base — is not yet done and should be scoped as integration cost, not assumed synergy.

**Example D — Regional NGO, $2M Annual Budget, 14 Staff**

The Executive Director is the Mission Founder. Intent is identical to Product — tutoring and educational outcomes cannot be separated from the reason the organization exists. The chain is flipped. The Business Model is 60% grants, 30% government contracts, 10% individual donations, with a written theory of change and cost-per-student metrics reported quarterly. The Entity is a 501(c)(3) with an asset distribution clause that prevents private conversion. The Product — tutoring outcomes — is measurable and reported quarterly; acceptance criteria exist and are held by the program team, not only by the Executive Director. The Process is codified in the program delivery manual and the volunteer-onboarding pipeline is documented. The Organization includes the Executive Director plus 14 staff plus a volunteer base, with the board simultaneously Tier 1 governor and Tier 6 actor.

Separability profile: Fused (intentionally — Intent ≡ Product) / Independent / Independent / Independent / Independent / Partial.

M&A implication: not for sale as a going concern, but structurally ready to merge with a compatible peer. Tier 1 alignment — mission compatibility check — comes first; if that fails, no merger is possible. Tiers 2 through 5 are already independent and can be merged operationally. Tier 6 requires deliberate succession planning for the Executive Director role.
