# Dual Hierarchies of Organizational Transferability: A Six-Tier Ontology and Theory of Acquisition Failure Propagation

*Dmitry Zharnikov*

DOI: [10.5281/zenodo.19895813](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19895813)

Working Paper v1.0.0 -- April 2026

---

## Abstract

Mergers and acquisitions continue to destroy value at high rates because scholars and practitioners lack a shared ontology specifying which organizational layers are transferred and how failures propagate across them. This paper develops a six-tier ontology of the acquisition target — Owner Intent, Business Model, Business Entity, Product, Process, and Organization — each characterized by a distinct governor, specification surface, and transferability mode. The tiers form dual oppositely directed hierarchies: a service hierarchy running upward from Organization to Intent and a constraint hierarchy running downward from Intent to Organization. The constraint hierarchy determines integration sequencing; shocks propagate bidirectionally, generating seven falsifiable propositions about failure cascades. The framework is form-invariant across for-profit, NGO, cooperative, and state-owned entities via explicit substitution rules, with Tier 2, 4, and 5 substitutions provisional. The ontology connects previously siloed streams — cultural distance, executive turnover, business-model misfit, and integration capabilities — under a single transferability logic, explaining residual variance documented in meta-analyses (King et al. 2004, 2021). The primary contribution is a generalizable theory of organizational separability under ownership change, with implications for theory of the firm and dynamic capabilities. A Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic is derived as a theoretical implication; propositions carry confirming and falsifying criteria.

**Keywords**: mergers and acquisitions; organizational ontology; transferability; business model; integration failure; form-invariance; separability diagnostic

---

Twenty years after King, Dalton, Daily, and Covin (2004) documented that strategic, financial, and organizational antecedents explain only a modest fraction of post-acquisition performance variance, subsequent meta-analyses confirm the same pattern: failure remains modal and integrative theory scarce (King et al. 2021; Paumen et al. 2024). The field has generated sophisticated single-layer explanations — cultural distance (Stahl and Voigt 2008), executive turnover (Bilgili et al. 2017), strategic misfit, and business-model misalignment (Christensen et al. 2011) — yet lacks a vocabulary for how these layers relate, constrain one another, or propagate failure. A cultural scholar and a strategy scholar examining the same deal are effectively analyzing different organizational tiers without a shared map of their interdependence. This ontological deficit is not merely semantic: it leaves the mechanisms by which a shock at one level reverberates across the entity underspecified, undermining both predictive accuracy and prescriptive guidance.

Cartwright and Schoenberg (2006) observe, reviewing thirty years of M&A research, that the field's explanatory frameworks remain discipline-internal — human resource scholars examine organizational resistance, strategy scholars examine strategic fit, legal scholars examine entity structure — with no integrative framework connecting these layers into a transferability theory. Angwin et al. (2023) call explicitly for theoretical rejuvenation of the field, identifying conceptual innovation and theoretical cross-fertilisation across domains as two of five priority directions. Haleblian et al. (2009), in a systematic stock-taking of M&A research, document that the field has accumulated domain-specific findings across strategy, finance, and human resources without theoretical integration of the layers those domains represent. Graebner et al. (2017) explicitly call for cross-level process models connecting strategic, sociocultural, and operational dimensions of integration. The present paper answers these calls by providing a unified cross-tier transferability ontology with formal propagation mechanisms.

This paper makes three primary theoretical contributions. First, it supplies the missing integrative ontology — a six-tier framework in which Owner Intent, Business Model, Business Entity, Product, Process, and Organization each carry a distinct governor, specification surface, and transferability mode. Bauer et al. (2024) and Bauer and Friesl (2024) establish that integration capabilities must span organizational domains; Strobl, Bauer, Shepherd, Lou, Tarba, and Dao (2025) document how contradictory HR signals propagate through the acquiring organization — but none of these contributions specify which tier generates which constraint, or how a misspecification at one tier propagates to tiers above and below it. The six-tier ontology supplies that specification. Second, it formalizes cross-tier propagation via dual hierarchies, extending Simon's (1962) near-decomposability and Penrose's (1959) productive services into a predictive failure theory. Third, it derives seven falsifiable propositions with explicit confirming and falsifying criteria, converting an empirical anomaly into a theoretical program. The practical implication — a Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic (STSD) for pre-close risk profiling — is presented strictly as a derivative of the theory, not its primary objective.

To our knowledge, this is the first framework in the M&A literature that formalizes cross-tier propagation as a set of falsifiable propositions organized around a dual-hierarchy mechanism, providing the theoretical architecture that King et al. (2004) and King et al. (2021) document has been missing for two decades. By reframing ownership transfer as hierarchical perturbation, the model offers a generalizable contribution to organizational theory: separability is never costless because constraint architectures are sticky.

The paper proceeds as follows. The next section establishes theoretical foundations, positioning the ontology against existing enterprise-architecture frameworks, addressing the ontological-mixing concern, reviewing the M&A failure literature, and situating the paper relative to recent dynamic-capabilities work. The third section presents the full six-tier framework. The fourth section develops the dual hierarchy and derives seven failure-propagation propositions. The fifth section demonstrates form-invariance across organizational types, develops the flip case, and derives integration implications including network and platform boundary conditions. The sixth section presents the STSD and a compact future-research agenda. The seventh section discusses limitations and contribution.

## Theoretical Foundations

### *Layered Ontologies of the Firm*

Prior frameworks have layered organizational phenomena without producing the specific structure the present paper requires. The closest structural precedent is Zachman's (1987) 6×6 matrix, which organizes enterprise architecture across six stakeholder perspectives (Planner, Owner, Designer, Builder, Subcontractor, Worker) against six interrogatives (What, How, Where, Who, When, Why). Zachman includes an "Owner's View" row and achieves a systemic cross-perspective representation that influenced a generation of enterprise-architecture practice. However, the Zachman framework carries no M&A framing, provides no transferability axis, and collapses Owner Intent and Business Model into the same Owner row — precisely the distinction the present paper argues is diagnostic for acquisition outcomes. The dual hierarchy developed in this paper extends Simon's (1962) near-decomposability principle — the insight that complex systems retain stability through hierarchical modularity — to the M&A specification problem; what this paper adds beyond Simon's foundational analysis is the specific tier names, governors, transferability modes, and M&A-specific diagnostic instrument that make the near-decomposability principle actionable for pre-close risk assessment.

Osterwalder's (2004) Business Model Ontology formalizes Tier 2 of the present framework with considerable precision, decomposing the business model into nine building blocks. The BMO has achieved broad adoption as a specification standard for business models. Its limitation, from the present paper's perspective, is topological: it addresses Tier 2 almost exclusively, represents Tiers 4 through 6 only as undifferentiated "Key Activities" and "Key Resources," and contains no Tier 1 (Owner Intent), no Tier 3 (Business Entity), and no transferability axis. The Business Architecture Body of Knowledge (Business Architecture Guild 2024) explicitly excludes Process from its scope and omits both Tier 1 and Tier 3, providing no instrument for distinguishing the principal's psychic commitment from the legal entity that holds the assets. Dietz's (2006) DEMO ontology addresses organizational design through ontological, infological, and datalogical layers tied to transaction structures — a different axis from the six named tiers — and carries no M&A application.

To our knowledge, the cumulative gap across these frameworks is precise: no existing framework combines Owner Intent as a distinct tier above the Business Model, Business Entity as a distinct legal tier, per-tier transferability modes, dual service and constraint hierarchies, and an M&A-specific application. The six-tier ontology fills this gap by combining elements from enterprise architecture (layered views of the firm), agency theory (the principal's Intent as the governing variable), transaction cost economics (the Entity as the boundary object), and the M&A integration literature (transferability as the organizing axis).

### *Tier Ontologies and the Unifying Axis of Transferability*

The six-tier ontology unifies objects of heterogeneous ontological type under a single framework. Tier 1 (Owner Intent) is a latent psychological and motivational state. Tier 2 (Business Model) is an abstract projection — a theory of value creation and capture, replicable without legal title. Tier 3 (Business Entity) is a legal and regulatory artifact that transfers by document. Tiers 4 through 6 (Product, Process, Organization) are operational realities that produce outcomes for customers and beneficiaries.

A referee applying strict ontological homogeneity as a framework criterion would object that these four categories cannot be legitimately combined in a single tiered structure. The objection is structurally analogous to the objection that Zachman's (1987) framework cannot legitimately combine stakeholder perspectives (social ontology) with technical interrogatives (logical ontology) in a single 6×6 matrix. Zachman's framework did combine them, and it became a foundational instrument precisely because the combination revealed relationships that discipline-internal ontologies cannot capture.

The present framework rests on a different justification: not ontological homogeneity but shared instrumental relationship to the M&A specification act. All six tiers are objects that must be specified, transferred, and integrated when one entity acquires another. The question is not "are they the same kind of thing?" but "what happens to each kind of thing when it crosses a new-owner boundary?" The unifying axis is transferability mode — the mechanism by which each tier crosses, or fails to cross, the ownership boundary. Tier 1 (Owner Intent) does not transfer by any legal instrument; it transfers, if at all, as imprinted constraint on the tiers below it. Tier 3 (Business Entity) transfers by document. Tier 6 (Organization) transfers only partially, via retention contracts that convert prior commitments into new obligations without guaranteeing the motivational states that made those commitments productive.

Frameworks that restrict to one ontological category — legal frameworks that see only Tier 3, brand-measurement frameworks that see only Tier 4, HR-integration frameworks that see only Tier 6 — are precisely the frameworks that produce single-layer diagnoses of multi-layer failures. The six-tier ontology explains what they miss by design. Coase (1937) and Williamson (1985) provide the economic foundation for Tier 3 as the boundary object of the firm. The present paper extends that foundational move by identifying the five other objects that a buyer also acquires (or fails to acquire) when the entity changes hands.

### *The M&A Failure Literature and Its Missing Layer*

Haspeslagh and Jemison (1991) established the foundational post-merger integration framework, distinguishing capability transfer from administrative integration; the present paper extends that distinction by specifying tier-level transferability modes. The scale of the failure problem is substantial: Cronqvist and Pély (2026) report that 46% of S&P 500 M&A deals are eventually unwound, with 50% of divested deals destroying shareholder value and an average of ten years required to unwind a failed transaction; Paumen, Kroon, and Khapova's (2024) systematic review confirms that failure remains the modal outcome across the peer-reviewed empirical literature. Lev and Gu (2024) identify 43 deal-level factors that predict failure and distill them to a 10-factor model, providing the most comprehensive recent statistical inventory of M&A failure antecedents — yet none of those factors is tier-level, which is precisely the gap the present paper addresses. Haleblian, Devers, McNamara, Carpenter, and Davison (2009), in a systematic stock-taking of M&A research, document that the field has accumulated domain-specific findings across strategy, finance, and human resources without theoretical integration of the layers those domains represent — the exact ontological gap the present paper fills. Graebner, Heimeriks, Huy, and Vaara (2017), reviewing post-merger integration processes in the Academy of Management Annals, explicitly call for cross-level process models connecting strategic, sociocultural, and operational dimensions of integration — a call the dual-hierarchy framework directly answers by specifying which tier constrains which and at what sequencing.

The M&A performance literature has identified four dominant failure explanations, each anchored in a single organizational layer. Cultural distance has accumulated the largest empirical base, with Stahl and Voigt (2005) providing an early integrative model of how cultural differences propagate through integration outcomes, Stahl and Voigt (2008) providing a meta-analytic examination across 46 studies (n = 10,710), and Brede, Gerstel, Wöhrmann, and Bausch (2025) extending that evidence using Glassdoor review data as a cultural-distance proxy. Both Stahl and Voigt papers address Tier 6 (Organization) in isolation: culture is treated as a property of the people and cultural norms layer, with no specification of how cultural disruption propagates to Product specification (Tier 4) or Business Model viability (Tier 2). Executive turnover — studied by Bilgili, Calderon, Allen, and Kedia (2017) in a meta-analytic design — also addresses Tier 6 in isolation, treating executive exit as a post-acquisition performance predictor without asking which tier the departing executive's tacit knowledge was encoding. Strategic misfit — the finding by King et al. (2004) that strategic antecedents explain only a small fraction of variance — is a Tier 2 diagnosis that leaves the transmission mechanism to Tiers 4 through 6 unspecified. Business-model misalignment — identified by Christensen et al. (2011) as a leading cause of mispricing and post-deal value destruction — addresses the Tier 2 to Tier 4 boundary but does not connect it to the constraint imposed by Tier 1 (Owner Intent) above or the execution implications for Tier 5 (Process) below.

The common structure of these explanations is the feature that makes them collectively inadequate: each diagnoses a single layer and cannot explain how failure at that layer propagates to adjacent tiers or to overall deal value. King et al. (2004) explicitly call for theory development to explain the residual variance — a call that King et al. (2021) document has not been answered at the integrative level two decades later. Klok, Kroon, and Khapova (2023), reviewing the emotional dimensions of M&A, note that integration outcomes are shaped by emotional processes distributed across multiple organizational levels, further implicating cross-layer dynamics that single-layer models cannot capture.

The present paper's theoretical gap claim is precise: no existing framework connects the layers into a transferability ontology that explains why misspecifying which tier is being acquired cascades into failure across all others. Smeulders, Dekker, and Van den Abbeele (2023) come closest in the empirical literature, showing that integration controls affect cultural-difference management and employee resistance — a finding that bridges Tier 5 (Process/integration management) to Tier 6 (Organization/resistance) — but their analysis remains anchored within the organizational layer rather than tracing the cascade to Tier 4 Product disruption or Tier 2 Model revision.

### *Recent Capabilities and Attention-Based Work*

A distinct stream of recent integration research has substantially advanced understanding of the capability requirements and attentional demands of M&A — and made the need for a tier-level vocabulary more acute. Bauer et al. (2024), writing in Long Range Planning, demonstrate that integration capabilities must span multiple organizational domains simultaneously, and that mismatches in organizational design choices across those domains systematically reduce synergy realization. Their finding implies that integration is a cross-domain problem — but the domains are not named at a level of precision that would permit pre-close diagnosis or sequenced intervention design.

Bauer and Friesl (2024), writing in the Journal of Management Studies, establish that managerial attention during integration is a scarce resource, and that attentional crowding-out — when integration teams are consumed by visible urgent tasks — systematically deprives causally prior integration challenges of the bandwidth they require. Their attention-based analysis implies that some integration challenges are more causally upstream than others and therefore warrant earlier attention. What the framework does not supply is a principled specification of which challenges are upstream: the present paper's constraint hierarchy provides that specification. Strobl et al. (2025), writing in the British Journal of Management, document that contradictory HR signals during integration — acquirer messages that simultaneously communicate stability and disruption — accelerate voluntary exit decisions in the target organization, with implications for the tacit-knowledge loss pathways formalized in P5 and P6.

What these three recent contributions collectively establish — capability requirements, attention scarcity, and HR-signal contradictions — is the phenomenology of integration difficulty. What they do not supply is the ontological vocabulary for specifying which tier carries which type of difficulty, which tier is causally prior to which, and how a failure at one tier propagates to the next. The present paper supplies that vocabulary, positioning itself as the ontological complement to a stream of capabilities research that has established the mechanisms of integration failure without yet having a map of the terrain on which those mechanisms operate.

## The Six-Tier Ontology

The six-tier ontology specifies each tier of an acquisition target by its governor — the agent or force that determines the tier's configuration — its specification surface — the set of observable artifacts that describe the tier's state — and its transferability mode — the mechanism by which the tier does or does not cross an ownership boundary. Table 1 presents the full structure.

**Table 1: The Six-Tier Ontology of an Acquisition Target.**

| Tier | Name | Governor | Specification Surface | Transferability Mode |
|------|------|----------|-----------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | Owner Intent | Owner psyche / board / charter | Stated purpose + time horizon + exit criteria | Imprint on lower tiers only; no direct transfer |
| 2 | Business Model | Market reality + owner choice | Value proposition + customer segments + channels + unit economics | Conceptually replicable; not legally owned |
| 3 | Business Entity | Law / regulator / tax authority | Charter + cap table + contracts + audited financials | By document (equity or asset transfer) |
| 4 | Product | Customer / beneficiary acceptance | Acceptance criteria + tolerance bands + dimensional specification | If written; tacit if not |
| 5 | Process | Management | SOPs + workflow maps + ERP configuration + supply-chain architecture | If codified; tacit if not |
| 6 | Organization | People and culture | Roles + employment contracts + tacit knowledge + relational networks | Partial only (retention contracts at best) |

*Notes*: Tiers are ordered by position in the constraint hierarchy (Tier 1 highest, Tier 6 lowest). SBT = Spectral Brand Theory (Zharnikov 2026a); the SBT eight-dimension framework is one example of a precision specification instrument for Tier 4. The service hierarchy runs in the opposite direction — upward from Tier 6 to Tier 1. The "Service Hierarchy and Constraint Hierarchy" subsection develops both hierarchies formally.

### *Tier 1 — Owner Intent*

Owner Intent is the psychic and strategic commitment of the controlling principal — the answer to "why does this exist, and when would it be rational to stop?" It is the topmost tier in the constraint hierarchy because it constrains every downstream choice: the Business Model chosen, the Products authorized, the Entity structures permitted, the Processes built, and the culture shaped over time. Its specification surface is the owner's stated purpose, time horizon, and exit criteria — observable, though imperfectly, in founding documents, investor communications, and interview data.

Schumpeter (1934) grounds the entrepreneurial function as an economy-transforming commitment to a domain, not merely an optimization problem over financial returns. Jensen and Meckling's (1976) agency theory provides the principal-agent framing: Owner Intent is the principal's objective function, and every lower tier is an agent executing in its service. Selznick (1957) adds the temporal dimension: institutions that survive long enough undergo "infusion with value," transitioning from expendable tools to entities whose existence is considered inherently valuable — the structural origin of the flip case developed in "The Flip Case: Intent Equals Product." Marquis and Tilcsik (2013) establish imprinting as the mechanism by which Intent propagates to lower tiers during the founding period.

*Vignette:* A sixty-two-year-old regional manufacturer has operated in the same trade for forty years. Their Intent, probed across several conversations, produces no sentence that survives without the company's name. Tier 1 is Fused: the owner and the business are the same thing.

### *Tier 2 — Business Model*

The Business Model is the mechanism by which value flows from the market into organizational resources — a formal account of the value proposition, the customer segments served, the channels through which value is delivered, the unit economics governing each transaction, the cost structure, and the revenue model. Its governor is market reality in interaction with owner choice: market conditions constrain the set of viable models, while the owner selects among them in service of Tier 1 Intent. Its transferability mode distinguishes it from every other tier: the Business Model is conceptually replicable — a competitor can observe and copy it without acquiring the legal entity — but it is not legally owned and does not transfer in a share deal.

Teece (2010) formalizes business models as the commercialization side of dynamic capabilities. Osterwalder's (2004) nine-block ontology provides the current specification standard for Tier 2. The critical distinction from Tier 1 is that the Business Model is the chosen answer to the market; the Owner Intent is the purpose for which that answer was chosen. A Domain Craftsman and a Financial Sponsor may adopt structurally identical Business Models while holding opposite Tier 1 Intent configurations — the downstream implications are radically different.

*Vignette:* A private equity sponsor manages a portfolio of three business-services companies under a shared back-office model, written as an investment thesis with explicit CAC/LTV targets and a defined exit timeline. Tier 2 is Independent: the model is documented, the logic is explicit, and it could be replicated by a successor management team without the sponsor's participation.

### *Tier 3 — Business Entity*

The Business Entity is the legal and regulatory construct that holds rights, obligations, and property — what actually transfers in a share deal. Its governor is the legal and regulatory environment. Its specification surface is the charter, the cap table, the debt structure, the counterparty contracts, and the audited financials. Its transferability mode is by document: equity interests or assets change hands through written instruments, and the legal act is complete on execution.

Coase (1937) grounds the firm boundary as the locus at which internal coordination is cheaper than market coordination; Williamson (1985) extends this to a theory of vertical integration as a governance choice. The Entity is the governance structure that these theories predict: the vehicle through which the Business Model's economic logic operates in the market. The critical analytical point is that a clean Tier 3 is a necessary but not sufficient condition for a successful acquisition. Financial due diligence is designed to examine Tier 3 — and Tier 3 only. A financially healthy Entity with clean documentation can hold a business whose Tier 4 Product specification is entirely tacit, whose Tier 1 Intent is Fused to the founder, and whose Tier 6 Organization will fracture within twelve months of close.

*Vignette:* A clean LLC with a three-year audit history passes financial due diligence without exception. What the data room cannot show is that the product specification — the thing generating the EBITDA — lives entirely in the founder's head and will dissolve at exit. The Entity is Independent; Tier 4 is Fused. The gap between them is the value the buyer will discover missing on day sixty of integration.

### *Tier 4 — Product*

The Product is what the business is hired to deliver — the specific outcome a customer or beneficiary is paying for, as determined by customer acceptance. Its governor is the customer or beneficiary. Its specification surface includes acceptance criteria, tolerance bands, and dimensional specifications. Its transferability mode is conditional: a written Product specification transfers; a tacit one does not.

Penrose (1959) grounds the productive resources of the firm as the core from which competitive advantage is derived; the Product is the external expression of that resource bundle. Zharnikov (2026a) provides one example of a precision specification instrument for Tier 4 — the SBT eight-dimension framework — which can serve as a dimensional specification surface where the Product is a brand-anchored offering. Zharnikov (2026ah) develops the brand-portfolio implications of identifying brand with Tier 4, including multi-brand capacity, failed-brand recovery, and marketing as a cross-brand Tier 6 function. The critical distinction from Tier 5 is that Tier 4 defines what is produced, while Tier 5 defines how: Process documentation anchored to an unspecified Product is coordination machinery without a destination.

*Vignette:* A SaaS company with $40M ARR has its Product codified in API contracts, SLA documents, and a formal roadmap with acceptance criteria. A third party could read the documentation and reconstruct the testing regime without the founding team's input. Tier 4 is Independent — the acquisition commands a premium multiple precisely because this independence removes the most common source of post-close value degradation.

### *Tier 5 — Process*

The Process is the coordinated activity that produces the Tier 4 Product, governed by management. Its specification surface covers standard operating procedures, workflow maps, ERP configuration, and supply-chain architecture. Its transferability mode parallels Tier 4: codified Process transfers; tacit Process does not.

Mintzberg (1979) provides the foundational taxonomy of coordination mechanisms whose applicability to a given business determines the degree to which Tier 5 is transferable independently of the people who built it. Pentland and Feldman (2005) refine this view by distinguishing the ostensive aspect of a routine (its abstract, codifiable pattern) from its performative aspect (the situated enactment by specific individuals): a Process whose ostensive specification is fully codified can transfer across owners, while a Process whose value resides in its performative enactment cannot. Porter's (1985) value chain decomposes Tier 5 into primary and support activities, providing the horizontal structure that complements the vertical tier structure of the present framework. Puranam, Singh, and Chaudhuri (2009) establish that structural integration is necessary when tasks are highly interdependent and common ground is low, but destroys value when imposed on loosely coupled processes where autonomy would preserve productive routines — a direct empirical basis for the STSD's Tier 5 Partial classification as the tractable middle ground. Angwin and Meadows (2015), extending the Haspeslagh-Jemison typology empirically, show that post-acquisition integration strategies are more varied than prior typologies recognized, supporting the view that Tier 5 separability states require diagnosis rather than assumption. Felker, Bauer, Friesl, and King (2024) find that routinization and codification of integration processes improve M&A outcomes when strategic intent is stable, but not otherwise — directly supporting the constraint-hierarchy sequencing principle that Tier 1 Intent clarity must precede Tier 5 codification efforts. Zollo and Singh (2004), in an empirical study of U.S. bank mergers, demonstrate that deliberate learning through codification during integration generates superior post-acquisition performance, establishing that Tier 5 codification is not merely descriptive documentation but an active investment in transferability that measurably improves outcomes when timed correctly.

*Vignette:* A holding company imposed a shared back-office Process over eighteen months. Twelve months in, the three operating teams continue to run legacy workflows in parallel with the mandated shared systems. Tier 5 is Partial: the buyer inherits a gap between documented Process and operational reality that will require active harmonization before any cross-sell synergy thesis can be executed.

### *Tier 6 — Organization*

The Organization is the set of people, roles, cultural norms, tacit knowledge, and relational networks that execute the Tier 5 Process, governed by the people and culture themselves. Its specification surface covers formal roles and employment contracts, organizational charts, and — critically — the tacit knowledge and relational networks that formal documents cannot capture. Its transferability mode is partial by structural necessity: employment contracts transfer as legal obligations but not as motivational commitments; culture cannot be acquired by document; tacit knowledge exits with the individual who holds it.

Hannan and Freeman's (1984) structural inertia framework establishes Tier 6 as the site of highest institutional resistance to change. Polanyi's (1966) treatment of tacit knowledge identifies the specific component of Tier 6 that retention contracts cannot capture: the judgment, relational trust, and contextual knowledge encoded in individuals through years of practice. Grant's (1996) knowledge-based theory of the firm establishes that this irreplaceable tacit knowledge is the source of the firm's most defensible competitive advantage — precisely the component that makes Tier 6 simultaneously the most valuable and the least transferable tier in the ontology. Du and Tsolmon (2026) show that post-M&A retention of top managers is mediated by those managers' structural knowledge of the acquiring firm, revealing that what transfers via people is not merely skill but positional knowledge of where information flows — the specific component of Tier 6 that the constraint hierarchy cannot reconstruct once it exits. Stahl and Voigt (2008) and Strobl et al. (2025) provide the empirical grounding for Tier 6 as the tier most thoroughly studied in the M&A literature and, paradoxically, the least connected to the upstream tier configurations that produced its current state.

*Vignette:* A regional NGO has fourteen paid staff plus a volunteer base managed by a program coordinator. The board simultaneously occupies Tier 1 (charter custodian) and Tier 6 (organizational actor) — a dual role that is the structural source of nonprofit governance complexity. Tier 6 is Partial: paid-staff successors are partially identified, but volunteer coordination and board succession are underdeveloped.

## The Dual Hierarchy and Seven Propositions

### *Service Hierarchy and Constraint Hierarchy*

The six tiers form two overlapping hierarchical structures with opposite directionality. Understanding both hierarchies is necessary for deriving the framework's integration-sequencing implications and for grounding the failure-propagation propositions that follow.

The service hierarchy runs upward: each tier contributes instrumentally to the tier above it. Organization serves Process — without the people and culture, the production system cannot operate. Process serves Product — without the production mechanism, the customer promise cannot be fulfilled. Product funds Entity — the revenue the Entity reports is generated by the Product the customer accepted. Entity implements Model — the legal construct is the vehicle through which the Business Model's economic logic operates in the market. Model realizes Intent — the Business Model is the owner's chosen answer to the market, selected in service of their purpose.

The constraint hierarchy runs downward: each tier constrains the admissible set of configurations for the tier below it. Intent constrains the admissible set of Business Models — a Mission Founder whose Intent is identical to their Product cannot adopt a Business Model that treats program outcomes as negotiable. Business Model constrains admissible Entity forms — a cooperative member-benefit Model cannot be held in a C-corp structure without fundamental contradiction. Entity constrains admissible Products — a regulated pharmaceutical Entity cannot deliver a Product that its license does not cover. Product constrains required Process architecture — a zero-defect acceptance criterion requires a Process that detects defects before they reach the customer. Process constrains required Organization — a continuous 24-hour service Process requires an Organization with shift coverage, not a Monday-through-Friday team.

The service and constraint hierarchies are the same set of relationships described from opposite directions. A shock at any tier propagates in both directions: a Tier 6 (Organization) fracture reduces the productive capacity that Tier 5 (Process) requires, and because Organization is the execution surface for Process, and Process is the production mechanism for Product, the downstream effect of a sustained Tier 6 fracture is Tier 4 (Product) drift even if the Product specification itself was never altered. This is the structural basis for Proposition P6 below.

Penrose (1959) grounds the productive services framing: the value of organizational resources is not in their stock but in the services they render, flowing upward through the service hierarchy. Thompson (1967) provides the canonical organizational theory anchor for the directional dual hierarchy: his pooled, sequential, and reciprocal interdependence types establish that organizational layers are linked by directional flows that constrain feasible coordination structures — exactly the structure the present framework formalizes. Williamson (1985) grounds the governance-as-constraint perspective: governance structures exist to reduce opportunism and uncertainty in the specification of productive activity, propagating downward through the constraint hierarchy. Bauer et al. (2024) establish empirically that acquisition integration capabilities must span multiple organizational domains — a finding consistent with the dual-hierarchy structure's implication that integration must address all six tiers.

The integration sequencing implication follows directly from the constraint hierarchy: because upper tiers constrain lower tiers, specification clarity at upper tiers is logically prior to integration execution at lower tiers. A buyer who reorganizes Tier 6 (Organization) before resolving Tier 1 (Intent) compatibility is redesigning a team without knowing what the team is supposed to produce for whom.

### *Failure-Propagation Propositions*

The following seven propositions formalize the failure-propagation pathways implied by the dual hierarchy. Each proposition is presented with its statement, mechanism, empirical anchor, confirming criteria, and falsifying criteria. Propositions P1 through P5 and P7 are theoretical — derived from the dual-hierarchy structure and consistent with adjacent single-layer findings, but not yet traced end-to-end in a single empirical study. Proposition P6 is partially empirically documented. The paper's contribution is the theoretical architecture; empirical validation is the agenda described in "Validation and Future Research Agenda."

**P1 — Intent–Model Misalignment**

*Statement:* When an acquirer's Owner Intent differs structurally from the target's Owner Intent (for example, a financial-return-primary acquirer acquires a mission-primary target), the target's Business Model will require fundamental revision to conform to the new Tier 1 constraint, and this revision will generate downstream friction at Tier 4 (Product) and Tier 5 (Process) that is not visible in pre-close due diligence.

*Mechanism:* Owner Intent constrains the admissible set of Business Models via the downward constraint hierarchy. A mission-primary target's Business Model was designed to optimize for impact metrics, not return on capital. A financial-return-primary acquirer's Tier 1 constraint is incompatible with this Model, forcing a Model migration that propagates downward to disrupt Tier 4 product-market positioning and Tier 5 operational priorities. Bauer and Friesl (2024) establish that attentional crowding-out during integration systematically deprives the Model-revision process of the managerial bandwidth needed to translate Tier 2 changes into coherent Tier 4 and Tier 5 adjustments.

*Empirical anchor:* No end-to-end empirical study traces Intent-level misalignment to Business Model migration to downstream tier failure in an M&A context. King et al. (2004) document that strategic-intent antecedents explain only a small fraction of post-acquisition performance variance. Christensen et al. (2011) frame deal failure in terms of business-model fit, distinguishing target absorption (transferring resources into the acquirer's existing model) from leverage as growth platform (using the target's business model itself), and warn that conflating these intentions is a leading cause of mispricing and post-deal value destruction.

*Confirming criteria:* Studies that (a) measure Tier 1 Intent configuration at close and (b) trace forced Business Model revisions to specific Tier 4 or Tier 5 disruptions in the 12–36 months post-close, controlling for pre-close financial performance.

*Falsifying criteria:* P1 is falsified if a systematic comparison of for-profit acquisitions of mission-primary organizations shows no greater rate of Business Model revision or downstream tier disruption than comparable for-profit-to-for-profit acquisitions of equivalent financial profile.

**P2 — Business Model–Product Friction**

*Statement:* When an acquirer imposes a Business Model revision on the target — following P1 or independently — the target's Product specification will drift toward the new Model's optimization criteria, and customers whose decision to purchase was anchored in the prior Product's dimensional configuration will exhibit higher churn rates than pre-acquisition baselines.

*Mechanism:* The Business Model governs the admissible set of Products via the downward constraint hierarchy: the Product optimized for a mission-primary Model (beneficiary impact) is not the Product that maximizes margin in a return-primary Model. Product revision to match the new Model changes the dimensional configuration of the offering and reduces the perceptual overlap between the Product the customer chose and the Product they now receive. Bauer and Friesl (2024) document synergy-evaluation failures in which acquirers mis-project Tier 4 demand stability under revised Business Models, overestimating the loyalty of customers whose purchase decisions were anchored in the prior Product's configuration.

*Empirical anchor:* No peer-reviewed study traces Business Model revision to Product-dimension drift to customer churn in an M&A context. Post-merger product-portfolio rationalization is documented in practitioner literature as a common integration activity, but downstream demand effects are not quantified in peer-reviewed evidence.

*Confirming criteria:* Studies measuring attribute stability of the acquired brand's attribute profile at close versus 12 and 36 months post-close, with churn disaggregated by customer segment and benchmarked against pre-acquisition segment retention rates.

*Falsifying criteria:* P2 is falsified if Business Model revisions imposed by acquirers show no systematic association with product attribute drift in a sample of completed acquisitions with matched pre- and post-close attribute data.

**P3 — Entity–Operations Disruption**

*Statement:* When Entity restructuring — subsidiary consolidation, re-domiciling, or change of legal form — follows close, operational continuity at Tier 5 (Process) and Tier 4 (Product) will be disrupted by contract renegotiation, regulatory re-certification, and counterparty uncertainty at rates disproportionate to the legal scope of the restructuring.

*Mechanism:* The Business Entity is the contract-holding, license-carrying construct through which the target's Products are delivered and Processes are operated. Restructuring the Entity does not automatically assign counterparty relationships to the new vehicle; many contracts include change-of-control clauses or regulatory certifications that are Entity-specific. The constraint relationship explains why: Tier 3 constrains admissible Products via regulatory scope, and restructuring that scope disrupts the previously acceptable Product without producing a new specification anchor.

*Empirical anchor:* No peer-reviewed evidence documents Entity restructuring as an isolated antecedent to operational disruption in a traced causal design. Legal-complexity variables are included in some M&A performance models but are not disaggregated to the Entity-restructuring event.

*Confirming criteria:* Event studies comparing operational-continuity metrics for acquisitions with and without Entity restructuring in the 90 days post-close, controlling for deal size and industry.

*Falsifying criteria:* P3 is falsified if Entity-restructuring events show no significant association with operational-continuity disruption across a sample of acquisitions with documented Entity changes.

**P4 — Product–Process Disruption**

*Statement:* When an acquirer revises the target's Product specification — following P2 or independently — the Process architecture that was designed to produce the prior Product will exhibit misalignment with the new specification's requirements, generating quality failures or cost overruns that persist until the Process architecture is rebuilt around the new specification.

*Mechanism:* Process is constrained by Product via the downward constraint hierarchy: a zero-defect Product acceptance criterion requires a Process with defect-detection capacity; a high-throughput Product specification requires a Process with high-volume capacity. Revising the Product specification without simultaneously revising the Process creates a mismatch between what the production machinery was designed to produce and what the new specification requires. The impossibility of fully specifying Product requirements to arbitrary resolution (Zharnikov 2026h) means that the mismatch accumulates tacit residuals that cannot be eliminated by documentation alone.

*Empirical anchor:* No peer-reviewed M&A evidence traces Product-specification revision to Process-architecture mismatch. SKU-rationalization literature documents process disruption in manufacturing contexts outside M&A settings but does not establish a causal chain in acquisition-specific designs.

*Confirming criteria:* Case comparisons of acquisitions with and without documented Product-specification revisions, measuring quality-defect rates and process-cost variances at 6 and 18 months post-close.

*Falsifying criteria:* P4 is falsified if Product-specification revisions following acquisition show no systematic association with quality-failure or cost-overrun rates, controlling for baseline Process maturity.

**P5 — Process–Organization Fracture**

*Statement:* When Process integration — ERP harmonization, SOP standardization, workflow reengineering — is imposed on the acquired Organization before Tier 4 (Product) specification clarity is established, employee resistance and attrition will exceed rates observed when Process integration follows Product specification confirmation.

*Mechanism:* Organization is constrained by Process via the downward constraint hierarchy: the staffing structure, role design, and decision-allocation of the Organization were built to execute a specific Process in service of a specific Product. Imposing Process changes without establishing the new Product's acceptance criteria deprives the Organization of the purpose context in which those changes make sense. Klok et al. (2023) establish that emotional dynamics during integration — employees' uncertainty about what they are working toward — are systematically underestimated in integration planning, consistent with the mechanism above. Stahl and Voigt (2008) show that integration approach moderates sociocultural integration outcomes; Smeulders et al. (2023) show that integration controls affect employee resistance, providing adjacent empirical links without tracing the full Process-first sequencing effect.

*Empirical anchor:* Adjacent links partially documented. No study traces the Process-first versus Product-first sequencing effect specifically.

*Confirming criteria:* Quasi-experimental comparisons of integrations sequenced Product-first versus Process-first, measuring executive turnover and employee attrition rates at 12 and 36 months.

*Falsifying criteria:* P5 is falsified if Process-integration sequencing — before versus after Product specification clarity — shows no significant difference in attrition rates across a matched sample of acquisitions.

**P6 — Organization Fracture to All-Layer Collapse**

*Statement:* When key Organization members — executives, technical specialists, or relationship holders — exit following acquisition, the tacit knowledge they carried, including uncodified Product specification, Process exception-handling, and customer relationship maintenance, will degrade across all six tiers at rates that cannot be recovered by retention bonuses or documentation efforts initiated after exit.

*Mechanism:* Tier 6 (Organization) is the sole repository of tacit specification knowledge that was never externalized into the Tier 4 or Tier 5 specification surfaces. This knowledge is irreversible: once the person who holds it exits, the knowledge exits with them. Financial incentives can delay exit; they cannot transfer tacit knowledge to a new holder. The all-layer degradation follows from the service hierarchy: Organization serves Process, so Tier 5 execution degrades; Process serves Product, so Tier 4 quality degrades; Product funds Entity, so Tier 3 revenue degrades; Entity implements Model, so Tier 2 viability degrades. Strobl et al. (2025) establish that contradictory HR signals during integration accelerate exit decisions, amplifying the mechanism above. Bauer et al. (2024) show that integration-capability deficits produce precisely the conditions in which tacit knowledge loss is most severe.

*Empirical anchor:* This is the best-supported pathway. Stahl and Voigt (2008) trace cultural differences to sociocultural integration to synergy realization to shareholder value across 46 studies. Bilgili et al. (2017) establish executive turnover to post-acquisition performance in a meta-analytic design. The chain from Organization fracture to all-layer value degradation is partially documented across these two sources; no single study traces the full pathway end-to-end.

*Confirming criteria:* Longitudinal studies that (a) measure pre-close tacit knowledge codification rates by tier, (b) track executive and key-specialist turnover at 6, 12, and 36 months, and (c) measure tier-specific value preservation — product quality stability, Process efficiency, customer retention — as outcome variables.

*Falsifying criteria:* P6 is falsified if acquisitions with high pre-close tacit knowledge codification — written Tier 4 and Tier 5 specifications — show the same rate of all-layer value degradation following executive turnover as acquisitions with low pre-close codification.

**P7 — Bidirectional Feedback**

*Statement:* Tier failure does not propagate unidirectionally from upper to lower tiers; sustained lower-tier failures — Process breakdown or Organization fracture — force upward revision of Business Model and, in extreme cases, Owner Intent, creating feedback loops that amplify initial shocks and accelerate total deal-value destruction.

*Mechanism:* The service hierarchy runs upward: sustained Process failure reduces the Product quality that the Business Model promised to deliver; sustained Product degradation erodes the revenue that the Entity reports; sustained revenue erosion requires Business Model revision to restore viability. In extreme cases, Tier 6 fracture forces a de facto Tier 1 Intent revision on the acquirer: the deal thesis collapses because the asset the acquirer bought was not the asset received. The feedback loop amplifies because upper-tier revisions generate new lower-tier constraints via the constraint hierarchy, restarting the cascade. Bauer and Friesl (2024) document synergy-evaluation failures in which initial integration-cost underestimates force sequential Business Model revision as each cost anchor fails, consistent with the feedback mechanism above.

*Empirical anchor:* No peer-reviewed evidence documents bidirectional feedback loops in M&A contexts. This is a purely theoretical proposition derived from the dual-hierarchy structure.

*Confirming criteria:* Case studies or panel data documenting sequential tier revisions — upward from Process and Organization fracture to Business Model restructuring — in the 18–48 months post-close, with explicit elimination of concurrent exogenous shocks as alternative explanations.

*Falsifying criteria:* P7 is falsified if a systematic case comparison shows no instances of lower-tier failures precipitating Business Model or Intent revision in acquisitions where exogenous market shocks are controlled for.

## Form-Invariance and Integration Implications

### *Substitution Rules Across Organizational Forms*

The six-tier structure does not require a for-profit assumption. Every tier has a structurally equivalent form across four organizational types: for-profit, NGO/charity, mutual/cooperative, and state-owned enterprise. Table 2 presents the full substitution matrix.

**Table 2: Form-Invariant Six-Tier Ontology Across Organizational Types.**

| Tier | For-Profit | NGO / Charity | Mutual / Cooperative | State-Owned |
|------|------------|---------------|----------------------|-------------|
| 1 Intent | Owner goal (return in domain X) | Charter mission + board fiduciary duty | Member benefit (collective intent) | Government mandate (public service + fiscal contribution) |
| 2 Model | Value capture: revenue, margin, unit economics | Theory of change + funding model (grants / fees / endowment) | Member-benefit model (cost-recovery + dividends to members) | Public-service model + treasury contribution |
| 3 Entity | LLC, C-corp, GmbH | 501(c)(3), CIC, foundation, charity, association | Cooperative, mutual society, credit union | SOE, public-law entity, agency |
| 4 Product | Customer-paid output | Beneficiary-received impact | Member-received service | Citizen-received service |
| 5 Process | Activity producing output | Activity producing impact | Activity producing member service | Activity producing public service |
| 6 Organization | Employees | Employees + volunteers + board | Member-employees + officers | Civil servants + appointees |

*Notes*: Tier 1 and Tier 3 substitutions are anchored in Hansmann (1996) for for-profit, NGO, and cooperative forms and in Bruton, Peng, Ahlstrom, Stan, and Xu (2015) for the state-owned form. Tier 1 and Tier 6 NGO/hybrid substitutions draw additionally on Battilana and Lee (2014). The Tier 2, Tier 4, and Tier 5 substitutions across all four forms are theoretical deductions from the tier definitions, not empirically validated cross-form mappings; they are explicitly provisional (see "Provisional Anchoring of Tiers 2, 4, and 5 Substitutions"). The cooperative Tier 3 entry applies to democratically governed entities requiring member consent for ownership transfer; Tier 1 is not a scalar individual intent but a collective mandate governable only through governance structure.

Hansmann (1996) provides the primary peer-reviewed anchor for the Tier 1 and Tier 3 substitutions across for-profit, NGO, and cooperative forms, establishing that ownership structure — the right to receive residual earnings and the right to exercise control — systematically varies across organizational types in ways that determine the feasibility and terms of ownership transfer. The state-owned enterprise form presents a Tier 1 that is held not by an individual or a membership collective but by a politically constituted authority with its own succession dynamics: the government mandate does not die with a particular minister and does not transfer by individual consent.

The privatization case — state-owned to for-profit acquisition — is the most visible cross-form transaction and the most instructive application of the form-invariance table. A privatization is, in this framework, a Tier 1 substitution: the Intent moves from public-service mandate to private-return-primary. Product and Process tiers do not automatically follow. Privatizations that fail characteristically do so because the acquirer assumes Tiers 4 and 5 will adapt to the new Tier 1 without deliberate extraction and documentation — the same mistake that kills lower-mid-market founder transactions. Bruton et al. (2015) establish that SOEs are hybrid organizations whose governance complexity — the coexistence of public-service and commercial-return objectives — mirrors the form-mismatch challenge that the framework identifies.

### *The Flip Case: Intent Equals Product*

In standard for-profit transactions, Tier 1 (Owner Intent) and Tier 4 (Product) are distinct: the owner's purpose is to capture return in a domain, and the Product is the specific mechanism through which that return is generated. The owner could, in principle, sell the business and apply their Intent elsewhere.

In identity-bound cases — the Domain Craftsman, the Family Steward, the Mission Founder — Intent is not separable from Product. The founder does not have an Intent to which the business happens to be the answer; the business is the Intent's only possible expression. Structurally: Intent ≡ Product. The chain collapses: Intent ≡ Product ← (Model = funding wrapper) ← Entity (legal cover) ← Process ← Organization. The tiers below Intent still exist and remain separately transferable in limited forms: assets can be sold, IP can be licensed, employees can be hired. What collapses is the separability of Intent from Product — the two cannot be split without destroying the thing that made the business what it is.

Selznick (1957) provides the closest prior formulation: institutionalization as "infusion with value" and his "test of expendability" is the structural precursor to the flip case. Marquis and Tilcsik (2013) establish imprinting as the mechanism. Whetten (2006) grounds organizational identity as the claim that certain attributes are central, enduring, and distinctive; the flip case is the limiting condition in which the founder's personal identity and the organization's identity are co-constituted. Pecis, Ge, and Bauer (2025), in a rhetorical history analysis of family businesses, trace how family and business goals become entangled through narrative articulation across generations — producing precisely the structural condition the flip case describes. Berrone, Cruz, and Gomez-Mejia (2012) establish the static consequence through the socioemotional wealth (SEW) lens: identity-bound principals systematically overvalue the firm relative to its financial utility, producing a reservation price above rational financial valuation that makes the firm effectively unsellable to pure financial buyers.

The consequence for M&A practice is categorical: firms in the flipped chain are unsellable as going concerns to a buyer who does not share the founder's identity commitment. They can sell assets. They can sell IP. They can merge with an entity that shares their Intent. They cannot be acquired by a financial buyer without changing what the business fundamentally is.

### *Provisional Anchoring of Tiers 2, 4, and 5 Substitutions*

Hansmann (1996) provides peer-reviewed support for the Tier 1 and Tier 3 substitutions across for-profit, NGO, and cooperative forms. Bruton et al. (2015) anchor the state-owned Tier 1 substitution. Battilana and Lee (2014) anchor the NGO and hybrid Tier 1 and Tier 6 substitutions.

The Tier 2, Tier 4, and Tier 5 substitutions across all four forms are theoretical deductions from the tier definitions, not empirically validated cross-form mappings. "Theory of change + funding model" is the NGO's functional equivalent of "value capture + unit economics" at Tier 2 because both constitute the answer to "how does value flow to the organization from the external environment?" — the defining question of the Business Model tier — adapted to the form-specific value logic. "Citizen-received service" is the state-owned equivalent of "customer-paid output" at Tier 4 because both are defined by the acceptance criteria of the Tier's governor adapted to the form's specific service recipient. These are logically derived from the tier definitions and structurally coherent, but they require empirical validation in the cross-form comparative design described in "Validation and Future Research Agenda."

### *Network and Platform Boundary Conditions*

The four-form substitution table (Table 2) covers for-profit, NGO, cooperative, and state-owned organizational forms but does not extend to network and platform businesses — two-sided markets, ecosystem orchestrators, and multi-sided platforms. These represent a distinct structural boundary condition that the current framework's tier definitions do not fully accommodate.

In a platform business, Tier 4 (Product) is not a unit good or service delivered to a discrete customer; it is a market interface that generates value by connecting participants on multiple sides of the platform. The acceptance criterion governing Tier 4 is not held by a single customer but by two or more participant populations whose preferences must be balanced simultaneously. Tier 6 (Organization) in a platform business extends beyond the payroll: ecosystem participants — developers, third-party vendors, marketplace sellers — whose participation constitutes the platform's value are not employees but are more than external counterparties. Their loyalty is not contractually held in standard employment terms, and their exit following an acquisition may constitute Tier 6 fragmentation that financial due diligence would not detect. Tier 2 (Business Model) in a platform business sits structurally closer to Tier 4 than in unit-good businesses, because the model — the multi-sided pricing architecture, the cross-subsidization logic, the participation incentive structure — is not separable from the interface design itself. Revising Tier 2 in a platform business is simultaneously a Tier 4 revision.

The full extension of the six-tier ontology to platform and network businesses — specifying governors, specification surfaces, and transferability modes for each tier under platform conditions — is a natural and important next step that this paper flags explicitly as future work. The current framework's propositions and diagnostic instrument apply to platform businesses with modification, not without it.

### *The Integration Sequencing Principle*

The constraint hierarchy derives a sequencing principle for M&A integration that is formal rather than prescriptive: because upper tiers constrain the admissible configurations of lower tiers, specification clarity at upper tiers is logically prior to integration execution at lower tiers. The sequencing implication is a structural necessity — attempting to finalize lower-tier configurations before upper-tier specifications are resolved is building on a foundation that the constraint hierarchy has not yet established.

The formal sequence is: (1) resolve Tier 1 (Intent) compatibility before Tier 2 (Business Model) revision; (2) resolve Tier 2 revision before Tier 4 (Product) specification; (3) confirm Tier 4 specification before Tier 5 (Process) integration; (4) confirm Tier 5 architecture before Tier 6 (Organization) role redesign. Tier 3 (Entity) integration is legally required immediately post-close; the framework implies that Tier 3 integration should be designed to minimize disruption to Tiers 4 through 6 while upper-tier specification clarity is being established.

**Table 3: Integration Sequencing Derived from the Constraint Hierarchy.**

| Phase | Tier Priority | Address Before | Theoretical Basis |
|-------|---------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Pre-close | Tier 1 compatibility | Any Tier 2 revision | Constraint hierarchy; P1 |
| Pre-close or Day 1 | Tier 4 specification | Tier 5 integration | P4; Tier 4 tacit knowledge loss risk (P6) |
| Months 1–6 | Tier 2 revision (if required) | Tier 5 harmonization | P2; P4 chaining |
| Months 1–6 | Tier 5 codification | Tier 6 role redesign | P5; Tier 6 resistance without purpose context |
| Months 6–18 | Tier 6 transition | Founder departure | P6; tacit knowledge transfer requires time |

*Notes*: The sequencing principle is derived from the constraint hierarchy, not from empirical observation of integration practice. Deviations from this sequence — the modal failure mode in the literature (Larsson and Finkelstein 1999) — produce the cascade pathways formalized in P1–P7. Tier 3 integration is omitted from the sequencing table because it is legally mandated immediately post-close; the design imperative is to minimize its disruption to Tiers 4–6.

The modal failure mode in practice inverts this sequence: acquirers begin with Tier 6 (Organization) — immediately visible, urgent, and socially demanding — and Tier 5 (Process) — operationally critical in the first weeks — while Tiers 1 through 4 remain unresolved. Larsson and Finkelstein (1999) identify inadequate organizational integration as the dominant predictor of synergy failure in their case-survey methodology; in tier-level vocabulary, their finding maps to Tier 5 forced-merge producing Tier 4 drift — a P4 cascade — whose upstream origin in an unresolved Tier 1 compatibility question the authors do not observe because their unit of analysis is the organizational layer, not the full tier stack. Jemison and Sitkin (1986) provide the foundational AMR framing of this pathology as the "activity trap" — operational urgency crowding out the strategic-intent resolution that the constraint hierarchy requires. Van Oorschot et al. (2023) find that post-M&A reorganization requires simultaneous integration in some sub-elements (product harmonization) and differentiation in others (structural autonomy), a cross-tier complexity that the dual-hierarchy framework accommodates naturally: the constraint hierarchy specifies which sub-elements must converge (Tiers 1–2) while the service hierarchy identifies which sub-elements can retain productive differentiation (Tiers 5–6). Bauer et al. (2024) establish that integration-capability deficits at any domain reduce synergy realization across the full deal, consistent with the dual-hierarchy framework's claim that integration is a cross-tier problem rather than a within-tier problem.

### *The Separability-as-Screening Principle*

The Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic (STSD) — described fully in "The Six-Question Diagnostic" — produces a Fused/Partial/Independent profile for each of the six tiers before close, enabling acquirers to price risk by tier rather than by aggregate financial performance.

A profile with Fused at Tier 1 signals the flip case (see "The Flip Case: Intent Equals Product"): no post-close integration sequence can resolve this condition because the flip case is a structural feature of the target, not a remediable information asymmetry. A profile with Fused at Tier 4 signals that the Product specification lives in the founder's judgment and will degrade post-close regardless of retention efforts; pre-close codification is the only intervention that changes the Tier 4 separability state. A profile with Partial at Tier 5 signals process-maturity gaps that are tractable: codification can close the gap, but must be scoped explicitly as integration cost rather than assumed synergy. A profile with Partial at Tier 6 signals key-person dependency: retention structures should be conditioned on knowledge-transfer milestones, not on calendar milestones.

The framework's primary theoretical contribution to integration theory is the disaggregation of due-diligence risk by tier rather than production of an aggregate pass/fail verdict. Standard financial due diligence produces a Tier 3 verdict: the Entity is clean or it is not. The STSD produces a six-cell profile that maps risk to tier, allowing acquirers to design tier-specific integration interventions. Bauer and Friesl (2024) provide complementary support from an attention-based view: when integration attention is allocated by aggregate severity rather than by tier-specific risk, attentional crowding-out causes high-severity tiers to consume cognitive resources that causally upstream tiers require — the STSD profile makes this allocation problem explicit by surfacing which tiers carry the binding integration risk. Current due diligence examines Tier 3; the STSD examines all six.

### *The Form-Mismatch Principle*

Cross-form acquisitions — a for-profit acquirer targeting a cooperative or NGO — face Tier 1 misspecification risk that no standard due-diligence checklist captures, because standard financial due diligence examines Tier 3 and does not examine Tier 1 compatibility. The form-invariance table (Table 2) reveals the specific substitution that must be negotiated in cross-form deals: the acquiring entity's Tier 1 (Intent) must be compatible with the target's Tier 1, or the downward constraint chain will generate forced revisions at every lower tier.

The cooperative presents a distinctive structural challenge: Tier 1 Intent is collectively held and democratically governed. No single principal can unilaterally authorize a sale; the member assembly must consent. A credit union cannot be acquired by a bank without member consent not because of regulatory friction alone but because the Intent itself — the collective member-benefit mandate — cannot be transferred without being replaced by a fundamentally different Intent. The STSD applied to a cooperative requires a collective-governance version of the diagnostic in which the question "Can the owner state their Intent in one sentence that does not contain the company's name?" becomes "Can the member assembly articulate a purpose that survives the transaction without changing the economic logic of membership?" Battilana and Lee (2014) establish that hybrid organizations at the for-profit/NGO boundary face mission-drift risk when acquired by non-mission buyers — an empirical instantiation of the form-mismatch cascade that the present framework formalizes.

Privatization transactions are the most visible cross-form case and the most tractable natural experiment for empirical testing of the form-mismatch principle: the Tier 1 substitution is explicit, documented in government records, and associated with large-scale post-acquisition operational changes that can be retrospectively coded against the tier-level predictions.

## The Separability Diagnostic and Validation Agenda

### *The Six-Question Diagnostic*

The Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic (STSD) is best understood as a theoretical implication of the dual-hierarchy framework rather than its primary deliverable. If the ontology is valid — if each tier is governed by a distinct force, exposes a distinct transferability mode, and generates distinct downstream constraints — then pre-close tier profiling should predict post-acquisition trajectories better than aggregate financial or strategic fit measures. The STSD operationalizes this theoretical claim as a structured judgment instrument that produces a six-cell profile — one state per tier, three possible states: Fused, Partial, or Independent. Table 4 presents the full diagnostic. Pablo (1994) establishes that integration-level decisions are made prior to integration execution and depend on task, cultural, and political target characteristics — providing direct AMJ-tradition precedent for the STSD's pre-close-decision framing: the instrument is designed to produce a tier-disaggregated risk profile at the point in the acquisition process where integration-level choices are still open.

**Table 4: The Six-Tier Separability Diagnostic.**

| Tier | Diagnostic Question | Fused | Partial | Independent |
|------|---------------------|-------|---------|-------------|
| 1 Intent | Can the owner state Intent in one sentence not naming the company? | No — the company IS the answer | Named but tied to company | Yes — purpose survives without this entity |
| 2 Business Model | Is the model articulated as a written canvas independent of habit? | No — implicit, inarticulable | Some components explicit, others tacit | Yes — fully written; logic independently testable |
| 3 Business Entity | Are debts and obligations decoupled from the owner's balance sheet? | No — personal guarantees, commingled accounts | Partial decoupling; residual obligations remain | Yes — fully decoupled; clean separation |
| 4 Product | Is there a written WHAT-specification a third party could test against? | No — spec lives in founder's judgment | Partial codification; key criteria implicit | Yes — written spec; survives founder exit |
| 5 Process | Does the process run a continuous month without the owner present? | No — owner present for key decisions | Owner required for exceptions only | Yes — runs fully; owner presence optional |
| 6 Organization | Does every owner role have a named, trained successor? | No — owner occupies irreplaceable roles | Some successors named; key roles thin | Yes — all roles covered by named successors |

*Notes*: The STSD produces an ordinal state per tier (Fused / Partial / Independent), not a continuous score. Inter-rater reliability evidence is the first validation priority. Identity-bound owners (Domain Craftsman, Family Steward, Mission Founder) will always show Fused at Tier 1 — a structural state, not a remediable condition.

How to read the profile: a profile of Independent / Independent / Independent / Independent / Partial / Partial indicates a highly transferable business with integration risk concentrated in Tiers 5 and 6 — the SaaS company profile described in the Product tier vignette. A profile of Fused / Fused / Partial / Fused / Partial / Fused indicates a business that presents as a standard transaction from the financial-due-diligence seat but will not survive the transition intact — the regional manufacturer profile described in the Owner Intent vignette. The six-cell structure makes visible what aggregate financial performance measures cannot: the tier-level distribution of value at risk.

### *Validation and Future Research Agenda*

The STSD is currently a structured judgment instrument with no inter-rater reliability data and no published predictive-validity evidence. The roadmap to empirical validation has four priorities.

First, translate the six diagnostic questions into a semi-structured interview protocol with probes for each response type. Two independent assessors conduct the protocol on the same target without contact before scoring. Cohen's κ ≥ .70 is the target threshold per tier, consistent with Landis and Koch's (1977) standard for acceptable inter-rater agreement on ordinal clinical instruments. Second, test predictive validity: the primary hypothesis is that Fused-tier count at close will be negatively associated with acquisition performance at 36 months after controlling for deal size, industry, and financial leverage — the most tractable operationalization of the framework's core claim. Secondary hypotheses follow from individual propositions: Fused Tier 4 at close predicts higher executive churn in the first 18 months (P6 pathway); Fused Tier 1 with a for-profit acquirer predicts higher rate of Business Model revision within 24 months (P1 pathway). Third, validate the form-invariance substitution rules for Tiers 2, 4, and 5 through a cross-form comparative design: a minimum of ten completed acquisitions per organizational form (forty total), with privatization transactions prioritized as natural experiments. Fourth, extend the framework to platform and network businesses (see "Network and Platform Boundary Conditions"): specify governors, specification surfaces, and transferability modes for each tier under multi-sided market conditions, then validate that the modified STSD produces reliable classifications for platform-business acquisitions.

## Discussion

### *Theoretical Contribution*

The six-tier ontology and its dual-hierarchy architecture offer a parsimonious yet powerful theoretical explanation for why ownership transfer so frequently destroys value. By making explicit the governors, specification surfaces, transferability modes, and bidirectional propagation pathways that existing single-layer models leave implicit, the framework converts the "unidentified moderators" lamented by King et al. (2004, 2021) into predictable, tier-contingent mechanisms. The primary theoretical contribution is therefore not another antecedent variable but a new architecture — a constraint-based view of the firm that complements capability, attention, and identity perspectives. Where dynamic capabilities research has shown that integration requires spanning domains (Bauer et al. 2024), this framework specifies which domains, in what order, and why misalignment at one necessarily perturbs others.

The seven propositions (P1–P7) formalize cross-tier failure predictions with explicit confirming and falsifying criteria for each pathway — a new theoretical move that predicts cross-layer cascades (Intent–Model misalignment → Product drift → Process mismatch) rather than within-layer outcomes. Each proposition satisfies the standard for theory papers articulated in AMR's own editorial guidance. Corley and Gioia (2011) argue that high-contribution theory advances both originality and utility — defined as the capacity to inform future scientific work and real-world practice simultaneously; the six-tier ontology aims at both, grounding novel cross-tier cascade claims in an instrument (the STSD) whose predictive-validity program constitutes the scientific utility dimension.

The dual-hierarchy construct itself has implications beyond M&A. It formalizes how service and constraint flows create both stability and vulnerability: the same hierarchical modularity that permits near-decomposability (Simon 1962) also creates leverage points for cascading failure when ownership boundaries are crossed. In institutional theory, imprinting (Marquis and Tilcsik 2013) is the mechanism by which Tier 1 Intent propagates to lower tiers during founding — a Tier-1-to-Tier-6 constraint cascade that the framework makes formally tractable. In routine dynamics (Pentland and Feldman 2005), the ostensive-performative distinction maps precisely onto the Tier 4–Tier 5 transferability boundary: ostensive specifications transfer; performative enactments do not. In hybrid organizing (Battilana and Lee 2014), Tier 1 substitution under cross-form acquisition — the form-mismatch principle — generates identity and mission-drift dynamics that the dual hierarchy explains as a constraint-cascade rather than a cultural friction. Proposition P7 (bidirectional feedback) is particularly relevant to paradox theory: the feedback loop by which lower-tier fractures force upper-tier Intent revision formalizes what paradox scholars describe as the simultaneity of stability and change demands, offering a tier-level mechanism for why paradoxes are not resolvable by organizational intent alone.

The paper's practical contribution is equally concrete: the STSD is the first pre-close instrument that disaggregates due-diligence risk by tier, producing a six-cell profile in which each tier carries its own transferability state rather than an aggregate pass/fail verdict. Standard financial due diligence examines Tier 3 only, generating a clean-entity verdict that is a necessary but not sufficient condition for a successful acquisition. The STSD generates a six-cell profile that surfaces Tier 1, Tier 4, and Tier 5 risk — the three tiers the framework identifies as carrying the highest pre-close transferability risk but least visible to current due-diligence practice.

### *Practical Implications*

The STSD translates the theoretical framework directly into due-diligence practice. A buyer applying the diagnostic before letter-of-intent submission receives a six-cell transferability profile whose practical implications are tier-specific: a Fused Tier 1 signals the flip case and should trigger price renegotiation or deal exit, not integration planning; a Fused Tier 4 signals that pre-close codification of the Product specification is the single highest-return integration investment available, because that investment becomes unavailable on day one of ownership; a Partial Tier 5 signals tractable process-maturity gaps whose codification cost should be scoped as explicit integration cost rather than assumed synergy. The integration sequencing principle provides the actionable corollary: buyers who resolve Tier 1 Intent compatibility before committing to Tier 2 Business Model retention, and who extract Tier 4 Product specifications before imposing Tier 5 Process changes, structure their integration effort around the constraint hierarchy rather than against it. The practical value of the framework is therefore not only in predicting failure — it is in providing a sequenced decision architecture that enables buyers to direct integration attention to causally upstream tiers before the window for pre-close codification closes.

### *Limitations*

**Cascade claim is theoretical.** Propositions P1 through P5 and P7 have no end-to-end empirical support. They are derived from the dual hierarchy and are consistent with adjacent single-layer findings, but they have not been traced in a single integrated study. This is the appropriate framing for an AMR conceptual contribution: the framework provides a theoretical architecture whose value is in the testable predictions it generates, not in empirical findings it has yet to produce.

**Form-invariance Tiers 2, 4, and 5 substitutions are provisional.** The Tier 2, Tier 4, and Tier 5 substitutions in Table 2 are theoretical deductions from the tier definitions, not empirically validated cross-form mappings. Hansmann (1996) anchors Tiers 1 and 3; Bruton et al. (2015) and Battilana and Lee (2014) anchor the state-owned and hybrid Tier 1 substitutions. The Tier 2, 4, and 5 substitutions across forms are logically derived and structurally coherent, but they require empirical validation in the cross-form comparative design described in "Validation and Future Research Agenda" before they can be treated as established.

**No primary empirical data.** The paper contributes a theoretical framework and a structured diagnostic instrument. This choice is deliberate: the contribution is ontological and theoretical, not empirical. A theory paper that rushes to validation risks testing a framework whose definitions are not yet stable; stabilizing the definitions is the necessary precondition for valid empirical testing.

**Multi-principal Intent configurations.** The framework assumes a dominant principal whose Intent can be characterized as a relatively stable stance. When ownership is dispersed — public companies, VC-backed startups with multiple investor classes, family businesses with sibling co-owners — Intent is a vector, not a scalar. The STSD Tier 1 question requires adaptation for multi-principal cases, and this extension is explicitly beyond the scope of the current paper. The framework as specified applies most cleanly to acquisitions of closely-held businesses where a single dominant principal can be identified; extension to publicly-traded targets and dispersed-ownership structures requires the multi-principal Intent vector adaptation noted above and remains a future-research priority.

**Platform and network boundary conditions.** As detailed in "Network and Platform Boundary Conditions," the framework's tier definitions require modification for two-sided markets and ecosystem orchestrators. The current framework's propositions apply to platform businesses with modification, not without it.

Nevertheless, by supplying a shared ontological vocabulary and a propagation logic, this theory reframes the M&A failure problem from an empirical puzzle into a predictable consequence of hierarchical constraint architecture. Ownership transfer necessarily perturbs the acquired firm's constraint hierarchy. The extent to which acquirers can specify and manage that perturbation before the window for pre-close codification closes determines whether value is preserved or destroyed. Future work that tests, extends, or challenges this architecture will advance not only M&A scholarship but our fundamental understanding of how organizations cohere across ownership changes.
